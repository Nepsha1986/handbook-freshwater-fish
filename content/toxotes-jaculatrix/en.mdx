## Overview

_Toxotes jaculatrix_ is one of the most unusual fish available for the home aquarium. This species from the family
Toxotidae inhabits mangrove swamps, estuaries, and coastal waters of Southeast Asia and Northern Australia. The
archerfish's signature trait is its unique hunting technique: the fish forms a tube-like channel in its mouth and shoots
a powerful jet of water, knocking insects off overhanging vegetation from distances of up to two meters.

Adults reach 15–20 cm in length and live 5–10 years with proper care. The body is tall and laterally compressed, silvery
white with 4–6 broad black vertical bands. The dorsal fin is set far back toward the tail, and large eyes provide
excellent binocular vision essential for accurate hunting. This is a diurnal species, most active during daylight hours.

## Tank Requirements

_Toxotes jaculatrix_ requires a spacious aquarium of at least 200 liters. These fish are active swimmers that need
plenty of open space in the upper and middle water layers. The optimal temperature is 24–30°C, pH 7.0–8.5, and general
hardness (GH) 10–20.

The key consideration is brackish water. In the wild, archerfish live where freshwater mixes with seawater. Adding
marine salt at 5–15 g per liter is recommended, using aquarium-grade salt formulated for brackish setups rather than
table salt. Juveniles can adapt to freshwater, but adults thrive best in brackish conditions.

The tank must be fitted with a tight-fitting lid — archerfish are excellent jumpers. Leave 10–15 cm of air space between
the water surface and the lid. Placing overhanging plants or driftwood above the water allows you to observe their
natural hunting behavior. Filtration should be powerful but without strong surface current.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Toxotes jaculatrix_ is an opportunistic predator feeding primarily on insects, small crustaceans, and
invertebrates that fall into the water. In the aquarium, the diet should be as varied as possible.

The staple diet consists of live and frozen foods: crickets, flies, mealworms, bloodworms, brine shrimp, and daphnia.
Live insects placed on overhanging leaves or attached to the glass above the water line stimulate natural hunting
behavior and keep the fish mentally engaged. Over time, archerfish will accept floating pellets and flakes, but dry food
should not be the sole food source.

Feed 1–2 times daily in small portions. Monitor feeding to ensure all tank inhabitants receive their share, as
archerfish can become assertive during feeding and push aside slower tankmates.

## Care and Maintenance

Keeping archerfish requires attention to water quality and parameter stability. Weekly water changes of 20–25% are
essential, and replacement water must be prepared with the same salinity level. Use a refractometer or hydrometer to
monitor water density.

A powerful external canister filter is the best choice for an archerfish tank. Brackish water accumulates nitrates more
quickly, so regular testing is mandatory. Nitrate levels should be kept below 20 mg/l.

Choose moderate lighting, avoiding overly bright light that can cause stress. Driftwood and rock decorations provide
shelter, while floating plants (water lettuce, salvinia) shade the surface and create a natural atmosphere. Avoid
delicate plants that cannot tolerate brackish water.

## Compatibility

_Toxotes jaculatrix_ is a moderately aggressive species that does best in groups of 4–6 individuals. Solitary keeping
leads to stress, and in too small a group, a dominant individual may suppress the others.

Suitable tankmates include other brackish water inhabitants of comparable size. Good companions are the
[knight goby](/en/stigmatogobius-sadanundio) and other brackish water gobies, monos, and scats. Cohabitation with the
[figure eight puffer](/en/tetraodon-biocellatus) is possible given sufficient tank volume.

Avoid small fish — any fish that fits in the archerfish's mouth will be seen as prey. Slow-moving species with long fins
are also unsuitable. Freshwater species that cannot tolerate salt are completely incompatible.

## Breeding

Breeding _Toxotes jaculatrix_ in captivity is extremely difficult, with only isolated successes reported. Sexual
dimorphism is minimal: females are generally slightly larger and fuller than males, but reliably determining sex by
visual inspection alone is challenging.

In nature, spawning occurs during the rainy season when water salinity drops and food availability increases. To
stimulate breeding in the aquarium, you can try replicating these conditions: gradually reduce salinity, increase the
frequency of live food feedings, and slightly lower the temperature before raising it again.

Females can lay up to 3,000 small eggs on the water surface among floating vegetation. Larvae hatch within 12–14 hours
and begin feeding after 2–3 days. Starter food includes brine shrimp nauplii and infusoria. Due to the difficulty of
replicating natural conditions, most fish available in the trade are wild-caught specimens.
