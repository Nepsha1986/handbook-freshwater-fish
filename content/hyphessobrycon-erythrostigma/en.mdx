## Overview

_Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ is one of the most recognizable tetras in the family Characidae, named after the vivid
crimson spot on its flank that resembles a bleeding heart. In the wild, this species inhabits slow-flowing rivers and
flooded forests of the upper Amazon basin in Peru and Colombia, where it favors soft, slightly acidic water stained with
tannins.

The fish reaches a size of 4–5 cm and lives 3–5 years with proper care. The body is tall and laterally compressed, with
a silvery-pink base coloration. Its defining feature is a large red-crimson spot in the center of the body that gives
the species its common name. Males sport an elongated, sickle-shaped dorsal fin with elegant white tips. Females are
more rounded with less developed finnage but still display the characteristic spot.

_Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ is often confused with its close relative, the
[ornate tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-bentosi), which lacks the prominent red spot on its flank. Another similar species is
the [rosy tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-roseus), whose spot is less vivid and smaller.

## Tank Requirements

A school of _Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ requires an aquarium of at least 80 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 22–28 °C, pH 6.0–7.5, and GH 6–12. The species is fairly adaptable, but thrives best in soft, slightly
acidic water.

The tank setup should mimic the natural biotope: dark substrate, driftwood, Indian almond leaves, and dense vegetation
along the perimeter. Leave the central area open — Bleeding Heart Tetras are active swimmers and need room to maneuver.
Floating plants will create diffused lighting, under which the fish display their most vivid coloration.

Keep this species in a school of at least 6–8 individuals. In a group the fish feel more confident, males compete by
displaying their impressive fins, and stress-related behavior is minimized. Water flow should be gentle to moderate.

## Feeding and Diet

_Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ is an omnivore with a moderate appetite. In nature, its diet consists of small insects
and larvae, zooplankton, and plant detritus. In the aquarium, the staple diet should be high-quality flakes or small
granules for tropical fish.

Regularly supplement with live and frozen foods: daphnia, brine shrimp, cyclops, and small bloodworms. Live foods in
particular help maintain the intense coloration of the red spot and overall body brightness. Occasional plant-based
supplements — spirulina flakes or blanched spinach — are also beneficial.

Feed 1–2 times daily in small portions consumed within 2–3 minutes. Bleeding Heart Tetras actively take food in the
water column and at the surface. Avoid overfeeding — small tetras are prone to obesity on a monotonous diet of dry foods
alone.

## Care and Maintenance

The care level for _Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ is rated 2 out of 5 — this is an undemanding species suitable for
aquarists with minimal experience. That said, stable conditions remain the key to health and longevity.

Weekly water changes of 20–25 % are essential. Use an internal or small external filter appropriate for the tank volume.
Monitor parameters: ammonia and nitrites should be at zero, nitrates below 25 mg/l.

Bleeding Heart Tetras are fairly hardy, but under stress or in poorly maintained water they can be susceptible to ich
and fungal fin infections. The best prevention is clean water, a varied diet, and keeping them in a proper school. If
fins are damaged, they regenerate within a few weeks under good conditions.

Pay attention to lighting: under overly bright light, the fish appear paler and tend to seek shelter. Subdued, diffused
lighting will bring out their natural beauty.

## Compatibility

_Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ is a peaceful fish (2 out of 5 on the aggression scale), perfectly suited for a tranquil
community aquarium. Within the school, harmless hierarchical sparring may occur between males, but aggression toward
other species is absent.

Excellent tankmates include other peaceful tetras of similar size: [neon tetras](/en/paracheirodon-innesi),
[black neon tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-herbertaxelrodi), [ornate tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-bentosi),
[emperor tetras](/en/nematobrycon-palmeri), and [glowlight tetras](/en/hemigrammus-erythrozonus). The closely related
[rosy tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-roseus) is also perfectly compatible and together with the Bleeding Heart Tetra creates
a striking color combination.

They coexist well with [harlequin rasboras](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha), [panda corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda),
[pygmy corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus), and dwarf cichlids such as [cockatoo cichlids](/en/apistogramma-cacatuoides)
and [ram cichlids](/en/mikrogeophagus-ramirezi).

Avoid housing them with large or aggressive species, as well as active fin-nippers. The sickle-shaped fins of males are
a frequent target for [tiger barbs](/en/puntius-tetrazona), so such combinations are best avoided.

## Breeding

Breeding _Hyphessobrycon erythrostigma_ is rated 3 out of 5 in difficulty — a task well within reach for aquarists with
basic experience. This is an egg-scattering species with no parental care.

Prepare a breeding tank of 15–20 liters with soft, acidic water: temperature 26–28 °C, pH 5.5–6.5, GH 2–6. Place
fine-leaved plants on the bottom — Java moss or cabomba — or use a spawning grid to protect the eggs. Lighting should be
minimal, as the eggs are light-sensitive.

For 1–2 weeks before spawning, feed the breeders generously with live foods. Select a male with the brightest coloration
and most prominent sickle-shaped fin, and a female with a noticeably rounded belly. Transfer the pair to the breeding
tank in the evening. Spawning typically occurs in the morning: the male performs a courtship dance, after which the
female scatters small, semi-transparent eggs among the plants.

Remove the adults immediately after spawning — they are prone to eating their own eggs. Larvae hatch within 24–36 hours
and begin free-swimming on the 3rd–4th day. Initial food should be infusoria and liquid fry food; after 7–10 days,
transition to baby brine shrimp. Fry grow at a moderate pace, and the characteristic red spot begins to appear at 2–3
months of age.
