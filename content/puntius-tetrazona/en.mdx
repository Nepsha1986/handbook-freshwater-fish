## Overview

The Tiger Barb (_Puntius tetrazona_) is one of the most recognizable aquarium fish in the Cyprinidae family, beloved for
its vibrant striped pattern and tireless energy. In the wild, this species inhabits slow-moving rivers and streams on
the islands of Sumatra and Borneo, as well as the Malay Peninsula, where it favors shaded areas with dense vegetation
and soft substrates.

The body is tall and laterally compressed, reaching 6–8 cm in length. The base coloration is golden-yellow with four
characteristic vertical black bands: through the eye, behind the pectoral fin, in front of the dorsal fin, and at the
base of the tail. The dorsal fin is edged with a black band and a red margin, while the ventral and anal fins display a
rich red-orange hue. Males are more vividly colored, especially during spawning, when their snouts take on a reddish
tint.

Over decades of aquarium breeding, numerous color variations have been developed: the green (moss) barb, albino form,
and long-finned varieties. However, the classic striped form remains the most popular and hardiest.

## Tank Requirements

A school of Tiger Barbs requires an aquarium of at least **100 liters**. These are active swimmers that need ample open
space for movement, so an elongated tank shape is preferable.

**Water parameters:**

- Temperature: 24–28 °C
- pH: 6.0–7.5
- General Hardness (GH): 5–15 dGH

Fine gravel or coarse sand in dark shades works best as a substrate — it makes the fish's colors appear especially
vivid. Plant the aquarium with live plants around the perimeter and along the back wall, leaving the central area open
for swimming. Vallisneria, cryptocorynes, and Java moss are all suitable choices. Driftwood and rocks provide additional
hiding spots and give the tank a more natural appearance.

Lighting should be moderate — overly bright light causes stress. Aim for a medium current that mimics the conditions of
their natural biotope. A secure lid is essential, as Tiger Barbs are excellent jumpers.

## Feeding and Diet

_Puntius tetrazona_ is a typical omnivore that feeds on small invertebrates, insect larvae, algae, and detritus in the
wild. In the aquarium, these fish readily accept virtually any food offered.

**Staple diet:**

- High-quality flakes or small granules for tropical fish

**Supplemental foods (2–3 times per week):**

- Live or frozen bloodworms
- Brine shrimp (nauplii and adult)
- Daphnia
- Cyclops
- Finely chopped vegetables (cucumber, zucchini, spinach)

Feed twice daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. Tiger Barbs are prone to overeating, so it
is important not to overfeed them. A weekly fasting day benefits their digestion and overall health.

## Care and Maintenance

The Tiger Barb is a fairly hardy fish, but stable water quality remains the key to its well-being. With proper care, its
lifespan is **4–6 years**.

**Weekly maintenance:**

- 25–30% water changes
- Gravel vacuuming to remove uneaten food and waste
- Algae cleaning from glass surfaces

Filtration should be efficient without creating excessively strong currents. An external canister filter is the optimal
choice for appropriately sized tanks. Regularly test water parameters for ammonia, nitrites, and nitrates.

Tiger Barbs are reasonably resistant to disease, but sudden fluctuations in water parameters or stress can make them
susceptible to ich (white spot disease). Quarantining new fish before adding them to the main tank is an essential
preventive measure.

## Compatibility

Tiger Barbs are schooling fish and must be kept in groups of **6 or more**. In smaller groups, they become nervous and
tend to nip the fins of their tankmates. In a large school, hierarchical interactions stay within the group, and the
fish pay little attention to other aquarium inhabitants.

**Good tankmates:**

- [Zebra Danio](/en/danio-rerio) and other danios
- [Panda Corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda) and other [corydoras](/en/corydoras-aeneus)
- [Checkered Barb](/en/puntius-oligolepis)
- [Denison Barb](/en/puntius-denisonii)
- [Scissortail Rasbora](/en/rasbora-trilineata)
- [Striped Loach](/en/botia-striata)
- [Clown Loach](/en/chromobotia-macracanthus)

**Unsuitable tankmates:**

- [Bettas](/en/betta-splendens) and other fish with long, flowing fins — barbs will nip at them
- Slow-moving angelfish and gouramis
- Very small fish that may become targets of harassment

The sex ratio within the group is not critical, but having several males allows you to enjoy their ritualized sparring
displays — a truly entertaining sight.

## Breeding

Breeding Tiger Barbs is not particularly difficult and is accessible even for beginner aquarists. Sexual maturity is
reached at 6–8 months of age.

**Spawning preparation:**

- A separate breeding tank of 30–40 liters
- Water temperature: 26–28 °C
- pH: 6.0–6.5 (soft, slightly acidic water)
- Place a mesh or fine-leaved plants (Java moss) on the bottom to protect the eggs
- Water level of 15–20 cm

Separate males and females for 1–2 weeks before spawning and feed them generously with live foods. Females are easily
distinguished by their fuller bellies and less vivid coloration. Spawning typically occurs in the early morning. The
female lays up to 200–300 eggs, which sink to the bottom and adhere to the substrate.

Remove the parents immediately after spawning — _Puntius tetrazona_ readily consume their own eggs. Incubation takes
approximately 36 hours, and the fry become free-swimming after 3–5 days. Initial food should be infusoria and brine
shrimp nauplii. As the fry grow, gradually transition them to larger foods.
