## Overview

The White Cloud Mountain Minnow (_Tanichthys albonubes_) is a small freshwater fish from the Cyprinidae family, first
discovered in 1932 in streams on Baiyun Mountain (White Cloud Mountain) near Guangzhou, Guangdong Province, China. The
species name albonubes translates to "white clouds," referring to its place of origin. In the wild, this species has
nearly disappeared due to urbanization, but it has been fully preserved through aquarium breeding.

Adults reach only 3-4 cm in length. The body is slender and elongated, colored in bronze-brown tones with a bright
iridescent stripe running from the eye to the base of the tail. The fins have a reddish tint, and the caudal fin
displays a characteristic red coloration. Juveniles show a neon-blue stripe, earning them the nickname "poor man's neon"
— though in beauty and behavioral interest, this fish is every bit as captivating as the
[neon tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi).

The White Cloud Mountain Minnow is one of the best fish for beginners: it is hardy, peaceful, and does not require a
heated aquarium. With proper care, its lifespan reaches 5-7 years.

## Tank Requirements

A school of 8-10 White Cloud Mountain Minnows requires an aquarium of at least 60 liters. This is a schooling fish that
becomes shy and pale when kept alone or in small groups — I recommend keeping a minimum of 8 individuals.

The key feature of White Clouds is their preference for cool water. The optimal temperature is 16-22°C, making them one
of the few tropical fish that do not need a heater at room temperature. Water parameters: pH 6.0-8.0, hardness GH 5-19.
As you can see, the acceptable parameter range is very broad, confirming this species' undemanding nature.

In the aquarium, aim to recreate conditions resembling a mountain stream: moderate flow from the filter, plenty of live
plants (vallisneria, hornwort, Java moss), and open swimming areas. Fine gravel or sand works well as substrate.
Lighting should be moderate. A lid or cover glass is essential, as White Clouds are capable of jumping out of the water.

## Feeding and Diet

White Cloud Mountain Minnows are omnivorous and completely undemanding when it comes to food. The dietary staple can be
high-quality dry food — flakes or micro-pellets sized appropriately for their small mouths.

To maintain vibrant coloration and good health, I recommend regularly supplementing the diet with live or frozen foods:
daphnia, cyclops, brine shrimp, and small bloodworms. In nature, White Clouds feed on small invertebrates, insect
larvae, and algae, so dietary variety is very important for them.

Feed twice daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2-3 minutes. Overfeeding is dangerous: it leads to
obesity and deteriorating water quality. A fasting day once a week is beneficial.

## Care and Maintenance

Caring for White Cloud Mountain Minnows requires minimal effort, making them an ideal choice for beginning aquarists.
Weekly water changes of 20-25% are the foundation of stable maintenance. Use a gravel vacuum to clean the substrate of
food remnants and organic waste.

Monitor water parameters with test kits — despite their hardiness, sudden parameter swings can cause stress. Filtration
should be gentle: an internal filter or air-driven sponge filter is the optimal choice, as strong currents tire these
fish.

White Clouds are resistant to most diseases, but when kept in water that is too warm (above 24°C), their immune system
weakens. This is precisely why preventing overheating is important. When purchasing new fish, always quarantine them to
avoid introducing infections into the main aquarium.

## Compatibility

White Cloud Mountain Minnows are peaceful schooling fish that are perfectly suited for community aquariums. They coexist
excellently with other peaceful species of similar size: [zebra danios](/en/danio-rerio),
[harlequin rasboras](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha), [chili rasboras](/en/boraras-brigittae), small
[pygmy corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus), as well as shrimp and snails.

Do not house White Clouds with large or aggressive fish — due to their small size, they easily become prey. Also avoid
keeping them with [betta fish](/en/betta-splendens), which may display aggression.

An important consideration: White Clouds are cool-water fish, so ideal tankmates are species with similar temperature
preferences. Keeping them with typical tropical species requiring 26-28°C is inadvisable, as White Clouds live
significantly shorter lives in warm water.

## Breeding

Breeding White Cloud Mountain Minnows is one of the easiest tasks in the aquarium hobby, well within the capabilities of
even beginners. Sexual dimorphism is subtle: males are slightly slimmer and more colorful, while females are
fuller-bodied, especially before spawning.

For spawning, you can use a separate 10-20 liter tank with fine-leaved plants or spawning mops. Raise the temperature by
1-2 degrees and soften the water slightly. White Clouds are egg scatterers that deposit their eggs among plants.

A unique feature of White Clouds — unlike many cyprinids, they rarely eat their own eggs, which makes it possible to
breed them even in the main aquarium. Nevertheless, for maximum fry survival, a separate breeding tank is preferable.

Eggs develop over 2-3 days at 20°C. The fry are very small, and for the first few days should be fed infusoria or liquid
fry foods. After a week, you can transition to baby brine shrimp. The fry grow quickly and acquire adult coloration by
2-3 months of age.
