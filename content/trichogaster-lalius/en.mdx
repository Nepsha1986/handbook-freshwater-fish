## Overview

_Trichogaster lalius_ is a small labyrinth fish from the family Osphronemidae, native to slow-moving rivers, ponds, and
rice paddies of India, Bangladesh, and Pakistan. Males display striking coloration: alternating diagonal stripes of red
and blue cover the entire body, while the fins are adorned with red and orange hues. Females are more modestly colored
in silvery-gray tones with pale stripes.

Thanks to their small size (6-8 cm), calm temperament, and stunning appearance, _Trichogaster lalius_ has become one of
the most popular aquarium fish worldwide. Like all labyrinth fish, they possess a special respiratory organ — the
labyrinth — which allows them to breathe atmospheric air. This is why they are often found in oxygen-poor waters in the
wild. Numerous color morphs have been developed in the hobby, including neon, coral, rainbow, and others.

A close relative, the [honey gourami](/en/trichogaster-chuna), is also frequently recommended for beginners.

## Tank Requirements

A pair or small group of _Trichogaster lalius_ requires an aquarium of at least 40 liters. Water temperature should be
maintained at 24-28°C, pH at 6.0-7.5, and hardness (GH) at 5-15. Parameter stability is more important than hitting
exact values: sudden fluctuations in temperature and pH cause significant stress.

The aquarium should be densely planted with live vegetation, including floating plants — these create diffused lighting
and serve as anchor points for bubble nests during breeding. Vallisneria, cryptocoryne, hornwort, and water lettuce work
well. Filter flow should be minimal: in nature, these fish inhabit still or slow-moving waters. Use an internal filter
with adjustable flow or a sponge filter.

A tight-fitting lid is essential: Dwarf Gouramis are excellent jumpers, and a layer of warm, humid air above the water
surface is important for proper labyrinth organ function.

## Feeding and Diet

_Trichogaster lalius_ is an omnivore that feeds on small insects, larvae, zooplankton, and algae in the wild. In the
aquarium, the staple diet can consist of high-quality flakes or micro-pellets formulated for labyrinth fish. To maintain
health and vibrant coloration, supplement regularly with live or frozen foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, cyclops, and small
bloodworms.

Feed small portions twice daily — only as much as the fish consume within 2-3 minutes. Dwarf Gouramis are prone to
overeating, so a weekly fasting day is beneficial. Dietary variety is the key to a strong immune system and a long
lifespan (4-6 years with proper care).

## Care and Maintenance

Weekly water changes of 20-25% are the foundation of _Trichogaster lalius_ health. Use a gravel vacuum to remove
leftover food and waste from the substrate. Monitor water parameters with test kits: ammonia and nitrites should remain
at zero, and nitrates below 20 mg/l.

Dwarf Gouramis are susceptible to bacterial infections, particularly the so-called Dwarf Gourami Iridovirus (DGIV).
Purchase fish from reputable breeders and always quarantine new arrivals for 2-3 weeks before introducing them to the
main tank. Signs of illness include loss of appetite, faded coloration, body swelling, and ulcers.

Provide ample hiding spots using plants, driftwood, and ceramic decorations. This reduces stress and allows more timid
individuals (especially females) to feel secure.

## Compatibility

_Trichogaster lalius_ is a peaceful fish, though males can display territorial behavior toward one another. The optimal
arrangement is one male with one or two females. In a small aquarium, two males will constantly conflict.

Good tankmates include small, peaceful species that occupy the middle and lower levels of the aquarium:
[rasboras](/en/rasbora-brigittae), small tetras, [corydoras catfish](/en/corydoras-panda), and otocinclus. Avoid keeping
them with large or aggressive cichlids, active barbs (tiger barbs will nip their thread-like ventral fins), or other
labyrinth fish such as [bettas](/en/betta-splendens) or [paradise fish](/en/macropodus-opercularis), which will compete
for surface territory.

Avoid adding overly active or fast-swimming fish: Dwarf Gouramis are slow-moving and shy, and such tankmates will cause
constant stress.

## Breeding

Breeding _Trichogaster lalius_ is a fascinating process accessible to intermediate-level aquarists. Prepare a separate
breeding tank of 20-30 liters with a temperature of 26-28°C, soft, slightly acidic water (pH 6.0-6.5, GH 4-8), and
plenty of floating plants.

The male constructs a characteristic bubble nest at the water surface using air bubbles and plant fragments. The
spawning process is beautiful to observe: the male wraps around the female, and she releases a batch of eggs, which the
male collects in his mouth and places into the nest. A single spawning can produce up to 600 eggs.

After spawning, the female must be removed — the male takes sole responsibility for caring for the offspring. He guards
the nest, returns fallen eggs, and maintains its structure. The fry hatch within 24-36 hours, and after 3-4 days they
begin to swim freely. At this point, remove the male. First foods should be infusoria and newly hatched brine shrimp.
The fry grow slowly, and male coloration only develops at around 3-4 months of age.
