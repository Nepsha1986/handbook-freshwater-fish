## Overview

The Japanese Stream Goby (_Sicyopterus japonicus_) is a freshwater fish from the family Gobiidae, native to fast-flowing
mountain streams and rivers of Japan, as well as other parts of East Asia, including Taiwan and the southern Korean
Peninsula. This species attracts aquarists with its unusual lifestyle and interesting behavior: the fish spends most of
its time clinging to rocks using modified pelvic fins that form a sucker-like disc.

Adults reach a size of 10–15 cm. Males develop striking coloration during the breeding season, displaying vivid
blue-purple and reddish hues, while females are more modestly colored in grayish-olive tones. The body is elongated and
slightly flattened on the underside, which is typical of bottom-dwelling inhabitants of fast-flowing waterways.

_Sicyopterus japonicus_ is an amphidromous species. In the wild, newly hatched larvae are carried downstream to the sea,
where they undergo early developmental stages before migrating back into freshwater rivers, climbing past rapids and
even small waterfalls thanks to their sucker-like fins. This remarkable adaptation makes the species one of the most
fascinating representatives of freshwater ichthyofauna.

### How to tell males from females?

- **Coloration**: Males are significantly brighter — during the breeding season they develop blue-purple and red tones.
  Females remain grayish-olive.
- **Size**: Males are usually slightly larger than females.
- **Fins**: Males have more developed dorsal and anal fins.
- **Behavior**: Males are territorial and actively display their coloration to rivals.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Sicyopterus japonicus_ requires an aquarium of at least **100 liters** with a mandatory mountain stream biotope
setup. This species has strict environmental requirements, and successful husbandry depends on proper habitat
organization.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature**: 18–24°C
- **pH**: 6.5–8.0
- **General Hardness (dGH)**: 5–15

The key element is powerful filtration that creates a strong current. A wave pump or external canister filter with a
directed output is recommended. Coarse gravel and rocks of various sizes arranged to form a natural riverbed landscape
make the ideal substrate. Smooth rocks are particularly important — they serve as surfaces for biofilm and algae growth,
which constitute the fish's primary food source.

Live plants such as Anubias and Java moss can complement the setup but are not essential. More important is good
aeration — this species is demanding when it comes to dissolved oxygen levels. The aquarium must have a tight-fitting
lid, as _Sicyopterus japonicus_ is capable of climbing out of the water and escaping the tank.

## Feeding and Diet

_Sicyopterus japonicus_ is a predominantly herbivorous species. In the wild, the fish feeds on biofilm, microalgae, and
detritus, scraping them from rock surfaces with its specialized mouthparts.

In the aquarium, the core diet should include:

- Algae growth on rocks and decorations (the tank must be mature, with established biofilm)
- Spirulina-based foods — tablets and flakes
- Blanched vegetables: zucchini, cucumber, spinach, lettuce leaves
- Frozen foods (brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms) — no more than 1–2 times per week as supplementation

An important consideration: the aquarium for this species must be well-established — in a freshly set up tank without
biofilm, the fish will starve even if offered artificial foods. It is recommended to cycle and mature the aquarium for
at least 2–3 months before introducing the gobies. Feeding is best done in the evening, placing tablets near the rocks
where the fish typically rest.

## Care and Maintenance

_Sicyopterus japonicus_ is a moderately challenging species to keep, but it requires stable environmental conditions.
Life expectancy with proper care is 3–5 years.

Key recommendations:

- **Water changes**: 25–30% weekly. During changes, try not to disturb the biofilm on rocks — it is the primary food
  source.
- **Filtration**: Clean the filter regularly using aquarium water. Do not allow pump performance to decline — water flow
  is critically important for the fish's well-being.
- **Parameter monitoring**: Regularly check temperature, pH, and nitrate levels. This species cannot tolerate
  temperatures above 26°C and is sensitive to organic waste buildup.
- **Aeration**: Must run around the clock. A drop in oxygen levels is one of the most common causes of mortality.

Pay attention to the fish's behavior: healthy individuals actively move across rocks during the daytime. Lethargy, color
loss, or refusal to eat are the first signs of water quality issues or health problems.

## Compatibility

_Sicyopterus japonicus_ is a peaceful bottom-dwelling species with a behavior rating of 2, making it a good candidate
for a community tank with suitable companions. The critical requirement is that all inhabitants must thrive in cool
water with strong current.

Suitable tank mates:

- [Stiphodon Ornatus](/en/stiphodon-ornatus) and other stream gobies
- [Beaufortia](/en/beaufortia-kweichowensis) — an excellent companion from a similar biotope
- [Zebra Danio](/en/danio-rerio) and other danios that prefer cooler water
- [White Cloud Mountain Minnow](/en/tanichthys-albonubes) — an ideal companion for upper water layers
- [Panda Corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda) — a peaceful bottom-dwelling neighbor
- [Otocinclus](/en/otocinclus-vittatus) — shares similar dietary preferences

Avoid keeping with large aggressive fish, heat-loving species (discus, angelfish), and active bottom-dwellers that would
compete for territory and food. Male _Sicyopterus japonicus_ may show territoriality toward each other, so when keeping
multiple specimens, ensure sufficient space and hiding spots.

## Breeding

Breeding _Sicyopterus japonicus_ in an aquarium is an extremely challenging task, rated 4 out of 5 on the difficulty
scale. The reason lies in the amphidromous life cycle of this species.

In the wild, spawning occurs in freshwater streams: the male prepares a shelter beneath a rock and attracts the female
with vibrant coloration and characteristic movements. The female deposits fine eggs on the underside of the rock, after
which the male guards the clutch. After hatching, the tiny larvae are carried downstream to the sea, where they spend
several weeks feeding on plankton. The juveniles then migrate back into the rivers.

Replicating such a cycle in a home aquarium is virtually impossible: one would need to transition the larvae from fresh
to saltwater, provide appropriate plankton, and then transfer the growing fry back to freshwater. The few successful
breeding cases have been documented only in specialized laboratories.

For this reason, the vast majority of _Sicyopterus japonicus_ available for sale are wild-caught specimens. When
purchasing, pay attention to the condition of the fish: healthy individuals are active, display good coloration, and
firmly suction onto surfaces.
