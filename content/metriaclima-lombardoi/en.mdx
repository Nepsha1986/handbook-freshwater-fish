## Overview

_Metriaclima lombardoi_ (synonym — _Maylandia lombardoi_), commonly known as the Kenyi Cichlid or Lombardoi Cichlid, is
one of the most striking and memorable Mbuna cichlids from Lake Malawi. In the wild, this species inhabits rocky
biotopes around Mbenji Island, clinging to rocky substrates at shallow depths.

The most distinctive feature of this species is its unique reverse sexual dimorphism. Unlike most cichlids where males
are more colorful, _Metriaclima lombardoi_ is the opposite: **females display a rich blue coloration with dark vertical
bars**, while **males develop a solid bright yellow color as they mature**. This frequently confuses beginner aquarists
who mistake the blue females for males.

The fish grows to 12–15 cm in length and lives 7–10 years with proper care. This is an extremely territorial and
aggressive species that is categorically not recommended for beginner aquarists.

## Tank Requirements

_Metriaclima lombardoi_ requires a tank of at least 200 liters. Optimal water parameters are: temperature 24–28 °C, pH
7.8–8.6, and hardness (GH) 10–18. These parameters replicate the conditions of Lake Malawi, and maintaining their
stability is critically important for the fish's health.

The aquarium should be decorated with a large number of rocks and stone formations creating numerous caves and hiding
spots. Each fish seeks to claim its own territory, so the more shelters available, the fewer conflicts will arise. A
sandy substrate is recommended to mimic the natural biotope. Live plants generally do not fare well with Mbuna, as the
fish tend to uproot and eat them.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Metriaclima lombardoi_ is a predominantly herbivorous species that feeds on algal growth on rocks. In the
aquarium, the diet should consist primarily of spirulina-based foods, vegetable flakes, and pellets designed for
herbivorous cichlids. High-quality dry foods with plant-based ingredients may be offered periodically.

**Important:** _Metriaclima lombardoi_ is extremely susceptible to Malawi bloat — a dangerous condition that often
develops when too much protein is included in the diet. Feeding bloodworms and tubifex should be avoided entirely. Brine
shrimp and other protein-rich foods should only be given rarely and in minimal amounts. A balanced plant-based diet is
the key to maintaining the health and vibrant coloration of these fish.

## Care and Maintenance

_Metriaclima lombardoi_ requires stable water parameters and diligent care. Weekly water changes of 25–30 % are
mandatory, as the fish are sensitive to nitrate accumulation and organic pollution. A powerful filtration system with
effective biological and mechanical filtration is essential.

Regularly test water parameters, maintaining pH at 7.8–8.6 and temperature at 24–28 °C. Overstocking is undesirable,
though a moderately dense population of Mbuna helps to disperse aggression from the dominant male. Monitor the fish
closely — abdominal swelling, refusal to eat, or fading coloration may indicate Malawi bloat and require immediate
intervention.

## Compatibility

_Metriaclima lombardoi_ is one of the most aggressive members of the Mbuna group. Keeping it with peaceful species is
absolutely out of the question. Males are extremely territorial and may harass not only tankmates but also females of
their own species.

The optimal stocking arrangement is one male to 3–4 females, which helps distribute aggression. Keeping two or more
males in the same tank is only feasible with a very large volume and an abundance of hiding spots. Suitable tankmates
include other large and hardy Mbuna cichlids, such as [Pseudotropheus zebra](/en/pseudotropheus-zebra),
[Yellow Lab](/en/labidochromis-caeruleus), or [Blue Dolphin Cichlid](/en/cyrtocara-moorii). Avoid housing the Kenyi
Cichlid with peaceful species from other regions, as well as similarly colored Mbuna, to prevent heightened
intraspecific aggression.

## Breeding

Breeding _Metriaclima lombardoi_ is not particularly difficult given suitable conditions. This is a maternal
mouthbrooder — the female incubates the fertilized eggs in her mouth for 18–21 days, after which she releases fully
formed fry.

Spawning typically takes place on a flat rock or in a depression in the substrate. The male attracts the female with his
bright coloration and a characteristic courtship dance. After fertilization, the female collects the eggs in her mouth
and retreats into seclusion. During this period she refuses food, so it is important to provide her with a calm hiding
spot, protected from aggression by the male and other tank inhabitants.

The fry are relatively large upon release and immediately accept crushed spirulina flakes and artemia nauplii. To
improve survival rates, it is recommended to move the brooding female to a separate tank.
