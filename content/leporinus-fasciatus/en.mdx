## Overview

The Banded Leporinus (_Leporinus fasciatus_) is a striking freshwater fish from the Anostomidae family, native to the
Amazon and Orinoco river basins. This species captivates with its vibrant coloration: an elongated, torpedo-shaped body
adorned with alternating black and yellow vertical bands. A fascinating feature of this species is that the number of
bands increases with age — juveniles have around 5 bands, while adults develop up to 10 distinct vertical stripes.

In the wild, _Leporinus fasciatus_ inhabits fast-flowing rivers and tributaries with rocky bottoms and abundant
vegetation. In an aquarium, this fish reaches 15–30 cm in length and lives 5 to 10 years with proper care. It is an
active and energetic species that requires a spacious tank and is not recommended for beginners due to its size and
behavioral traits.

Like its close relative, the [Marbled Headstander](/en/abramites-hypselonotus), the Banded Leporinus belongs to the
Anostomidae family and has a tendency to consume aquarium plants.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Leporinus fasciatus_ requires a tank of at least **200 liters**, though a larger aquarium is preferable for
adult specimens. Optimal water parameters include:

- **Temperature**: 23–28°C
- **pH**: 6.0–7.5
- **Hardness (dGH)**: 5–15

The tank must be equipped with a tightly fitting lid, as Banded Leporinus are excellent jumpers. The decor should
include driftwood, large rocks, and gravel to create hiding spots. Water flow should be moderate to strong, mimicking
their natural habitat conditions.

It is important to note that _Leporinus fasciatus_ actively consumes soft aquarium plants. For greenery, choose hardy
species such as Anubias and Java moss, or use artificial plants.

## Feeding and Diet

_Leporinus fasciatus_ is an omnivorous species with a strong preference for plant matter. The core diet should include:

- High-quality pellets and flakes with plant-based ingredients
- Vegetables: spinach, zucchini, cucumber, green peas
- Algae wafers and spirulina

The protein component can be provided by:

- Live or frozen bloodworms
- Brine shrimp and daphnia
- Small pieces of shrimp

Feed 2–3 times daily in small portions. Providing sufficient plant-based food in the diet helps reduce the fish's
tendency to eat aquarium plants.

## Care and Maintenance

Keeping the Banded Leporinus requires regular care and attention. Weekly water changes of 25–30% are essential, along
with a powerful filtration system. These fish produce significant waste due to their size and appetite, making a quality
external filter a necessity.

Lighting should be moderate. A sandy or fine gravel substrate is preferred, approximating the natural biotope
conditions. Regular monitoring of water parameters and fish condition is important — stressed fish may lose color
vibrancy.

It should be noted that _Leporinus fasciatus_ can become more assertive and pushy in behavior as it matures, so it is
essential to provide adequate space and hiding places for all tank inhabitants.

## Compatibility

The Banded Leporinus can be kept in a community tank, but tankmates should be chosen carefully. Suitable companions
include:

- Large cichlids such as the [Oscar](/en/astronotus-ocellatus)
- Large catfish: [Sailfin Pleco](/en/pterygoplichthys-gibbiceps), [Featherfin Synodontis](/en/synodontis-eupterus)
- Fast-moving fish of comparable size

_Leporinus fasciatus_ should not be kept with:

- Small fish that may be perceived as prey
- Slow-moving species with long fins
- Small invertebrates (shrimp, snails)

When keeping multiple Banded Leporinus in the same tank, a group of 5 or more is recommended to diffuse intraspecies
aggression. A pair may exhibit confrontational behavior.

## Breeding

Breeding _Leporinus fasciatus_ in a home aquarium is a challenging task and is rarely successful. In the wild, these
fish spawn during the rainy season in large rivers, when water levels rise and water parameters shift.

Sexual dimorphism is minimal: females are generally slightly larger and fuller-bodied than males, particularly when
carrying eggs. To stimulate spawning in an aquarium, the following conditions are needed:

- A very large breeding tank (at least 500 liters)
- Gradual reduction in water hardness and increase in temperature
- Simulating the rainy season through frequent water changes with soft water

Most specimens available in the trade are wild-caught or bred on specialized farms in Southeast Asia and South America
using hormonal stimulation.
