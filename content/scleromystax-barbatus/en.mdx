## Overview

_Scleromystax barbatus_ is one of the most visually impressive armored catfish in the family Callichthyidae, native to
the coastal rivers and streams of southeastern Brazil, in the states of Rio de Janeiro, Sao Paulo, and Santa Catarina.
This species is the largest among all corydoras-type fish, reaching 8-12 cm in size, significantly exceeding most
relatives from the Corydoras and Aspidoras genera.

The defining feature of _Scleromystax barbatus_ is its pronounced sexual dimorphism. Adult males develop impressive
bristles (odontodes) on their cheeks and the front of the head, which gave the species its common name — "bearded." The
body of males is covered with a contrasting pattern of dark spots and golden patches, and their pectoral fins have
elongated first rays. Females are considerably more subdued: their coloration is more uniform, with small dark dots on a
lighter background.

In the wild, this species inhabits cool mountain streams with water temperatures of 18-24°C, which distinguishes it from
most tropical corydoras. It is diurnal, like related species such as the [panda cory](/en/corydoras-panda),
[Sterba's cory](/en/corydoras-sterbai), and [bronze cory](/en/corydoras-aeneus). With good care, _Scleromystax barbatus_
lives 5-7 years, delighting its owner with an unusual appearance and fascinating behavior.

## Tank Requirements

A group of _Scleromystax barbatus_ requires an aquarium of at least 100 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 20-25°C, pH 6.0-7.5, and general hardness (GH) 2-15. It is important to note that this species prefers cool
water — in warm rooms, additional cooling may be needed during the summer months. Temperatures above 26°C cause stress
and increase susceptibility to disease.

Substrate is a key element in any armored catfish aquarium. Use only fine rounded sand: coarse gravel or substrate with
sharp edges damages the sensitive barbels through which the fish searches for food. Barbel damage leads to bacterial
infections and loss of the ability to feed properly.

Decorate the aquarium with driftwood, smooth rocks, and live plants that create hiding spots and shaded areas.
_Scleromystax barbatus_ appreciates subdued lighting and the presence of floating plants on the surface. Provide
moderate flow and efficient filtration — these catfish are sensitive to ammonia and nitrate levels in the water. Good
aeration is essential, although, like all corydoras, they are capable of gulping atmospheric air from the surface.

## Feeding and Diet

_Scleromystax barbatus_ is an omnivorous species that in the wild feeds on small invertebrates, insect larvae, and
organic debris, sifting through the sandy bottom with its sensitive barbels. In the aquarium, the dietary foundation
should consist of high-quality sinking granules and tablets designed for bottom-dwelling fish.

For a well-rounded diet and to bring out the vivid coloration of males, regularly supplement with live and frozen foods:
bloodworms, brine shrimp, tubifex, and daphnia. Protein-rich foods are especially important during pre-spawning
conditioning — they stimulate odontode development in males and promote egg production in females.

Feed the fish 1-2 times daily, ensuring that food reaches the bottom. Evening feeding is just as important as morning
feeding, since the catfish actively forage along the bottom during twilight hours as well. Avoid overfeeding —
decomposing food remnants quickly degrade water quality, to which this species is quite sensitive.

## Care and Maintenance

The care difficulty for _Scleromystax barbatus_ is rated as low (2 out of 5), although this species requires attention
to temperature management. Weekly water changes of 25-30% are mandatory to maintain low nitrate levels. When performing
water changes, use water of the same temperature or slightly cooler — sudden fluctuations can trigger unwanted spawning
or stress.

_Scleromystax barbatus_ is a schooling fish and must be kept in groups of 6 or more, with a female-heavy ratio (1 male
to 2-3 females). In a group, the catfish display natural behavior and vivid coloration. Males in a group compete for
female attention by flaring their fins and displaying their bristles — a captivating sight for any observer. Solitary
keeping leads to chronic stress and faded coloration.

Regularly inspect the fish's barbels: shortening or inflammation is the first sign of problems with the substrate or
water quality. Also pay attention to the condition of bristles on males — their loss may indicate stress or illness.
When kept on fine sand with timely water changes, these issues generally do not arise.

## Compatibility

_Scleromystax barbatus_ is a peaceful species with an aggression rating of 2 out of 5. Males may display territorial
behavior toward each other, but this rarely leads to serious conflict and is limited to posturing.

The best tankmates are small peaceful fish that prefer cooler water: [neon tetras](/en/paracheirodon-innesi),
[black neon tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-herbertaxelrodi), [flame tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-flammeus), and small
rasboras. [Apistogrammas](/en/apistogramma-borellii) that inhabit the middle water layers make good companions. Among
bottom-dwelling neighbors, other corydoras species work well, such as [panda cory](/en/corydoras-panda) or
[pygmy cory](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus).

Avoid housing them with large or aggressive cichlids, as well as active bottom-dwelling fish that would compete for
territory. Keeping them with heat-loving species such as [discus](/en/symphysodon-discus) is not recommended due to
differing temperature preferences.

## Breeding

Breeding _Scleromystax barbatus_ in the aquarium is of moderate difficulty (3 out of 5) and presents an interesting
challenge for the experienced aquarist. To stimulate spawning, use the classic rainy season simulation method: perform a
large water change (up to 50%) with cooler water (3-4°C below the usual temperature). A temperature drop combined with
generous feeding of live foods over 1-2 weeks typically triggers spawning behavior.

A male's readiness to spawn is easily identified by intensified coloration and the development of cheek bristles. The
male actively pursues the female, pressing against her sides. Spawning follows the classic corydoras pattern: the female
holds eggs between her ventral fins and attaches them to pre-cleaned surfaces — the aquarium glass, broad plant leaves,
or smooth stones. A single spawning event produces 50 to 150 large adhesive eggs.

After spawning, the parents must be removed, as they may consume their own eggs. To prevent fungal infections, add
methylene blue or alder cones to the water. The eggs develop over 4-6 days at 22-24°C. Newly hatched fry spend their
first 2-3 days consuming their yolk sacs, after which they should be fed baby brine shrimp, microworms, and finely
crushed dry food. Fry growth is slow, but with clean water and regular feeding, they develop steadily.
