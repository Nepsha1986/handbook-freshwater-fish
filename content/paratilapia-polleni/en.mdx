## Overview

_Paratilapia polleni_, known among aquarists as the polleni cichlid or starry night cichlid, is one of the most visually
striking freshwater fish available in the hobby. This endemic species from the island of Madagascar belongs to the
family Cichlidae and is rightfully considered a gem among cichlids thanks to its unmistakable appearance: a dark, nearly
black body densely covered with bright pearlescent spots reminiscent of a starry sky. Under certain lighting, the scales
take on a deep blue or violet sheen.

In the wild, _Paratilapia polleni_ inhabits rivers and lakes of northwestern Madagascar, preferring areas with rocky
substrates and moderate current. Adults reach an impressive 15–30 cm in length, with males typically being larger than
females and displaying more intense coloration. Life expectancy in an aquarium is 10–12 years with proper care.

It is worth noting that this species is endangered in the wild due to habitat destruction and the introduction of
invasive species. Keeping _Paratilapia polleni_ in aquariums contributes to preserving the gene pool of this remarkable
species.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Paratilapia polleni_ requires a spacious aquarium of at least 300 liters. For a pair or a community setup, a
tank of 400–500 liters or more is preferable, as this significantly reduces territorial conflicts.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature:** 23–27 °C
- **pH:** 6.5–7.5
- **Hardness (GH):** 5–15

Fine sand or smooth gravel works best as substrate, as these fish enjoy digging through it in search of food. The
aquarium should be decorated with large rocks, driftwood, and caves to create natural shelters and visual barriers for
territory division. Live plants are difficult to maintain since cichlids tend to uproot them, but hardy species attached
to rocks (such as Anubias) can survive.

Filtration must be powerful — an external canister filter rated at 4–6 times the tank volume per hour is the optimal
choice. Weekly water changes of 25–30% are mandatory, as large cichlids produce a significant biological load.

## Feeding and Diet

_Paratilapia polleni_ is a predator with a predominantly carnivorous diet. In the wild, its diet consists mainly of
small fish, crustaceans, and aquatic insects.

In the aquarium, the diet should be varied:

- **Staple (60–70%):** high-quality pellets for large cichlids with high protein content
- **Live and frozen foods (25–30%):** bloodworms, shrimp, krill, mussels, fish fillet
- **Vegetable supplement (5–10%):** blanched spinach, peas, spirulina

Adult fish should be fed once or twice daily in portions they can consume within 3–5 minutes. One fasting day per week
benefits digestion. Juveniles should be fed more frequently — three to four times daily in small portions to promote
growth.

In my experience, polleni readily accept food from the surface and mid-water, but become particularly animated at the
sight of live food — their hunting instinct is remarkably strong.

## Care and Maintenance

The care level for _Paratilapia polleni_ is rated as moderate, though there are several nuances worth keeping in mind.

These fish are sensitive to water quality, so regular parameter testing is an essential part of their care routine. Pay
particular attention to ammonia and nitrite levels, which must remain at zero, while nitrates should not exceed 20–30
mg/L.

Water changes should be performed weekly at 25–30% volume. It is important that fresh water closely matches the tank
water in temperature and pH — sudden parameter shifts cause stress.

_Paratilapia polleni_ are active daytime fish. They are curious, quickly become accustomed to their owner, and over time
learn to recognize the person who feeds them. This makes them truly interactive pets. However, caution is needed during
tank maintenance — large specimens can bite, especially during spawning periods.

Pay attention to lighting — moderate light with shaded areas highlights the fish's starry coloration and creates
comfortable conditions. A secure lid on the aquarium is essential, as excited fish are capable of jumping out of the
water.

## Compatibility

_Paratilapia polleni_ has a semi-aggressive temperament that intensifies with age and especially during breeding.
Selecting tank mates requires a thoughtful approach.

Suitable tank mates:

- Large cichlids with a similar temperament — [oscar](/en/astronotus-ocellatus), [severum](/en/heros-severus),
  [red terror cichlid](/en/cichlasoma-festae)
- Large catfish — [featherfin squeaker](/en/synodontis-eupterus), [sailfin pleco](/en/pterygoplichthys-gibbiceps)
- [Jaguar cichlid](/en/parachromis-managuensis) — provided the tank is large enough

Unsuitable tank mates:

- Small and peaceful fish (they will be seen as prey)
- Overly aggressive species that could dominate the polleni
- Fish with similar coloration that may provoke conflicts

The ideal scenario is keeping a pair of _Paratilapia polleni_ in a species-only tank. If a community setup is planned,
the tank volume should be at least 500–600 liters with plenty of hiding spots and visual barriers.

## Breeding

Breeding _Paratilapia polleni_ is a challenging endeavor (difficulty rated as hard), but entirely achievable under the
right conditions.

Sexual dimorphism is subtle: males are slightly larger and have more elongated fins. To form a pair, it is best to raise
a group of 5–6 juveniles and allow them to choose their own partner naturally.

To stimulate spawning:

- Raise the temperature to 26–27 °C
- Increase aeration
- Provide flat rocks or ceramic tiles as spawning substrate
- Increase the frequency of water changes with smaller volumes (10–15% every 2–3 days)

The female deposits 200–500 eggs on a prepared flat surface. Both parents actively tend to the clutch, fanning the eggs
with their fins and removing unfertilized ones. During this period, the pair's aggression increases sharply — all tank
mates should be removed or separated with a divider.

The larvae hatch after 3–4 days and begin free-swimming after another 5–7 days. First foods include baby brine shrimp,
microworms, and finely crushed flakes. The parents continue to guard the fry for several weeks, which is one of the most
fascinating spectacles in the aquarium hobby.

It is important to note that a pair that has successfully spawned usually maintains their bond for a long time, and
subsequent spawnings become easier.
