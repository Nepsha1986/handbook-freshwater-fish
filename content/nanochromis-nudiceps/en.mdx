## Overview

_Nanochromis nudiceps_ is a dwarf cichlid from the family Cichlidae, native to the Congo River basin in Central Africa
(Democratic Republic of Congo). The species name "nudiceps" translates from Latin as "bare head" (nudus meaning bare,
ceps meaning head), referring to the characteristic reduced scaling on the head of this species. Adults reach a size of
5-7 cm, making them one of the most compact representatives of the _Nanochromis_ genus.

A fascinating feature of this species is its reversed sexual dimorphism in coloration: female _Nanochromis nudiceps_ are
actually more colorful than males, which is unusual among most cichlids. During breeding condition, females develop a
vivid purple-violet belly coloration that serves to attract mates and signal reproductive readiness. In terms of
temperament, this species is relatively peaceful, similar to other dwarf cichlids such as
[kribensis](/en/pelvicachromis-pulcher) and [cockatoo cichlid](/en/apistogramma-cacatuoides).

## Tank Requirements

_Nanochromis nudiceps_ requires an aquarium with a minimum volume of 60 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 24-28 °C, pH 6.0-7.5, and general hardness (GH) 4-10. This species prefers soft, slightly acidic water that
closely resembles the conditions of their natural habitats in the Congo basin rivers.

Fine dark-colored sand works best as a substrate. The aquarium should be generously decorated with driftwood, rocks, and
ceramic shelters that form caves and crevices — this is critically important since the species is a cave spawner. Live
plants are welcome: anubias and Java moss will create a natural environment and provide additional cover. Subdued
lighting is recommended, matching the conditions of the shaded sections of Congo rivers.

Filtration should maintain clean water without creating strong currents. An external canister filter or an internal
sponge filter works well.

## Feeding and Diet

_Nanochromis nudiceps_ is an omnivorous species. In the wild, these fish feed on small invertebrates, insect larvae, and
detritus. In the aquarium, the base diet can consist of high-quality small-sized cichlid pellets and flakes.

Be sure to supplement the menu with live or frozen foods: daphnia, brine shrimp, bloodworms, tubifex, and cyclops. This
variety not only maintains the fish's health but also enhances color intensity, especially the violet hue of the
females' bellies. Feeding once or twice daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2-3 minutes is recommended.

## Care and Maintenance

_Nanochromis nudiceps_ is an intermediate-level species in terms of care difficulty. These fish are sensitive to water
quality, making parameter stability the key factor in their well-being. Weekly water changes of 20-30% are essential. It
is important to regularly monitor ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate levels using test kits.

When water quality deteriorates, these fish are susceptible to bacterial infections, including fin rot and ich. To
prevent disease, maintain aquarium cleanliness, avoid overstocking, and provide sufficient hiding places to reduce
stress. With proper care, their lifespan is 4-6 years.

## Compatibility

_Nanochromis nudiceps_ is a relatively peaceful cichlid that may display territorial behavior during spawning periods.
For community tank keeping, select calm tankmates of similar size that prefer comparable water parameters.

Good companions include [Congo tetras](/en/phenacogrammus-interruptus), small [corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus), and
other peaceful fish that occupy the upper and middle water layers. Among cichlids,
[kribensis](/en/pelvicachromis-kribensis) and [Thomas' dwarf cichlid](/en/anomalochromis-thomasi) are compatible,
provided there are enough hiding spots and territories for each pair. Avoid keeping them with large or aggressive
species, as well as fin-nipping fish.

## Breeding

_Nanochromis nudiceps_ is a cave spawner, and breeding in the aquarium is quite achievable when the necessary conditions
are met. To stimulate spawning, it is recommended to raise the temperature to 26-28 °C and provide several caves made
from rocks, coconut shells, or ceramic tubes.

When ready to spawn, the female develops an especially vivid purple-violet belly coloration — this serves as a signal to
the male. The female deposits eggs on the ceiling or walls of the cave, then takes on the primary role of caring for the
eggs and larvae, actively defending the clutch against any potential threats. The male guards the surrounding territory.

Incubation lasts approximately 3-4 days, and after about a week the fry become free-swimming. Suitable first foods
include artemia nauplii and infusoria. Parental care in this species is well developed, and with adequate food supply
and stable water parameters, fry survival rates are high.
