## Overview

_Sciaenochromis fryeri_, commonly known as the Electric Blue Hap, is one of the most visually striking cichlids from
Lake Malawi. This species belongs to the Haplochromis (Haps) group and is found throughout most of the lake, inhabiting
transitional zones between rocky and sandy biotopes at depths of 10 to 40 meters.

Male _Sciaenochromis fryeri_ display a deep metallic blue coloration that intensifies with age and becomes especially
vivid during spawning periods. Females and juveniles are much more subdued in appearance, showing silvery-grey tones
with several faint dark vertical bars. Adults reach 12-15 cm in length, with males typically being larger than females.
With proper care, they can live 7-10 years in an aquarium.

In the wild, _Sciaenochromis fryeri_ is an active predator that hunts fry of other cichlid species. Understanding this
natural behavior is key to meeting its needs in captivity. Despite its semi-aggressive temperament, this species
coexists well with other Malawi cichlids when tank volume is adequate and tank mates are chosen carefully.

## Tank Requirements

_Sciaenochromis fryeri_ requires a tank of at least 200 liters. A volume of 300 liters or more is preferable, especially
when keeping a group or housing them in a community setup with other cichlids.

Water parameters should replicate Lake Malawi conditions:

- Temperature: 24-28°C
- pH: 7.8-8.6
- General Hardness (GH): 10-20

Coral sand or fine limestone gravel works best as a substrate, as it helps maintain the required pH level. The aquascape
should feature plenty of rocks arranged to form caves and crevices. This mimics the natural biotope and provides
essential hiding spots for females and subdominant individuals. Leave open swimming areas between rock formations —
_Sciaenochromis fryeri_ actively uses the water column.

Filtration must be robust, turning over at least 6-8 times the tank volume per hour. Malawi cichlids are sensitive to
nitrate levels, making effective biological filtration critically important.

## Feeding and Diet

_Sciaenochromis fryeri_ is a natural predator, and its diet in captivity should reflect this. The staple food should be
high-quality protein-rich pellets designed for predatory cichlids. Supplement this with:

- Frozen bloodworms, brine shrimp, and krill
- Finely chopped shrimp meat
- Live foods when available

Feed 2-3 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2-3 minutes. Avoid large amounts of plant-based
foods — unlike many Mbuna species, Haplochromis cichlids do not digest coarse plant fiber well. Also avoid foods high in
fat, as this can lead to bloat, a common and dangerous condition in Malawi cichlids.

## Care and Maintenance

Weekly water changes of 25-30% are essential for successfully keeping _Sciaenochromis fryeri_. Malawi cichlids do not
tolerate the accumulation of organic waste, and regular water changes help maintain consistently low nitrate levels.

Monitor key water parameters regularly: temperature, pH, and hardness. Sudden fluctuations in these values cause stress
and weaken the immune system. When performing water changes, ensure the fresh water matches the tank parameters.

Clean filter media in tank water every 2-3 weeks. Avoid cleaning all filter elements at once to preserve the biological
balance.

Pay close attention to fish behavior. Loss of coloration in males, refusal to eat, rapid breathing, or clamped fins are
all signs of potential health problems. At the first signs of illness, isolate the affected fish in a quarantine tank.

## Compatibility

_Sciaenochromis fryeri_ has a semi-aggressive temperament typical of Lake Malawi Haplochromis. Males are territorial and
may show aggression toward fish of similar coloration. The ideal setup is one male with several females (a ratio of
1:3-4).

Good tank mates for _Sciaenochromis fryeri_:

- [Blue Dolphin Cichlid](/en/cyrtocara-moorii) — a peaceful Haplochromis occupying a different ecological niche
- [Red Empress](/en/protomelas-taeniolatus) — a large and calm companion
- [Borleyi Cichlid](/en/copadichromis-borleyi) — differs in both coloration and temperament
- [Venustus Cichlid](/en/nimbochromis-venustus) — large but relatively calm predator
- [Aulonocara](/en/aulonocara-nyassae) — suitable in a sufficiently large tank

Avoid keeping with:

- Small Mbuna species ([Yellow Lab](/en/labidochromis-caeruleus), [Zebra Cichlid](/en/pseudotropheus-zebra)) — due to
  differences in temperament and dietary needs
- Highly aggressive species ([Auratus Cichlid](/en/melanochromis-auratus)) — constant conflicts will occur
- Small fish from other families — they will be viewed as prey

## Breeding

Breeding _Sciaenochromis fryeri_ in an aquarium is well within reach for an intermediate-level aquarist. Like most
Malawi cichlids, this species is a maternal mouthbrooder — the female incubates eggs and shelters fry in her buccal
cavity.

To encourage spawning, raise the water temperature slightly to 26-28°C and provide a varied diet. The male will claim a
territory, usually near a flat rock or on a sandy patch, and actively display his vibrant colors to attract a female.

Spawning follows the typical Malawi cichlid pattern: the female lays eggs in small batches, immediately collecting them
in her mouth. The male fertilizes the eggs using the characteristic egg-spot releasers on his anal fin. The female
incubates 20 to 60 eggs over a period of 18-21 days, eating little to nothing during this time.

Once the fry are released from the mother's mouth, they can be fed newly hatched brine shrimp or finely crushed
high-quality flake food. It is recommended to move the brooding female to a separate tank or use a breeder box to
protect the fry from predation by adult fish.
