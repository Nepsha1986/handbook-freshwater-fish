## Overview

_Oreichthys cosuatis_, commonly known as the Cosmo Barb or Indian Swamp Barb, is a small freshwater fish belonging to
the Cyprinidae family. In the wild, this species inhabits marshes, ponds, and slow-moving waters of India and
Bangladesh. Despite its understated appearance at first glance, the Cosmo Barb possesses a refined beauty: a
golden-olive body coloration complemented by a characteristic dark lateral spot. Males are particularly striking,
developing a distinctive dark blotch on the dorsal fin that intensifies during spawning periods.

This species remains relatively rare and lesser-known in the aquarium hobby, making it an exciting find for enthusiasts
who appreciate unusual fish. Adults reach a size of 5-6 cm, allowing them to be kept even in smaller aquariums. The
Cosmo Barb is a diurnal species and can live for 3-4 years with proper care.

## Tank Requirements

A tank of at least 50 liters is required to house a school of Cosmo Barbs. Optimal water parameters are: temperature
22-28 °C, pH 6.0-7.5, and general hardness (GH) 4-15. In the wild, these fish inhabit still or slow-moving waters, so
strong currents should be avoided in the aquarium.

The tank should be densely planted with live vegetation, creating shaded areas and hiding spots. Fine-leaved plants such
as Java Moss and Cabomba work particularly well. A dark substrate is recommended, as it brings out the golden coloration
of the fish most effectively. Subdued lighting will help the fish feel more confident and display their natural
behavior.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Oreichthys cosuatis_ feeds on small invertebrates, algae, and plant matter. In the aquarium, these fish
are undemanding eaters and readily accept high-quality dry foods such as flakes and small pellets.

To maintain health and vibrant coloration, it is recommended to regularly supplement their diet with live or frozen
foods: daphnia, cyclops, bloodworms, and brine shrimp. Vegetable supplements such as blanched spinach or zucchini are
also beneficial. Feed the fish small portions once or twice daily, ensuring all food is consumed within a few minutes.

## Care and Maintenance

The Cosmo Barb is an easy fish to care for, suitable even for beginner aquarists (care level 2 out of 5). These fish are
best kept in a school of 6-8 or more individuals — in a group, they behave more confidently and display more vivid
behavior. Within the school, males actively compete with each other, showing off their best colors.

Weekly water changes of 20-30% are necessary to maintain stable parameters and cleanliness. It is important to monitor
ammonia and nitrite levels, especially in smaller tanks. Good filtration with moderate flow and regular plant
maintenance will ensure comfortable conditions for these fish.

## Compatibility

_Oreichthys cosuatis_ is an exceptionally peaceful species (aggression level 1 out of 5) that is perfectly suited for
community aquariums. Ideal tank mates include other small, peaceful fish: [rasboras](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha),
[danios](/en/danio-rerio), small [tetras](/en/pethia-padamya), as well as bottom dwellers such as
[pygmy corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus). They also get along well with other peaceful barbs such as the
[cherry barb](/en/puntius-titteya) or the [rosy barb](/en/pethia-conchonius).

Avoid housing them with large or aggressive species that may intimidate these timid fish. It is also not recommended to
keep them with long-finned fish, as barbs may occasionally show interest in trailing fins.

## Breeding

Breeding _Oreichthys cosuatis_ in a home aquarium is possible but requires some experience (breeding difficulty 3 out of
5). These are egg-scattering fish that do not exhibit parental care.

For spawning, prepare a separate tank with fine-leaved plants or synthetic spawning mops. The water should be soft and
slightly acidic, with the temperature raised to the upper end of the range (26-28 °C). Before spawning, condition the
breeders with generous amounts of live food for 1-2 weeks.

After spawning, the adults must be removed immediately from the breeding tank, as they will readily consume their own
eggs. The eggs hatch within 24-48 hours. Initial food for the fry consists of infusoria and micro worms, and as they
grow, they can be transitioned to baby brine shrimp and finely crushed dry foods.
