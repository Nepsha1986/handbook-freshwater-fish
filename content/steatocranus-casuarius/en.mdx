## Overview

The Buffalo Cichlid (_Steatocranus casuarius_) is one of the most distinctive and recognizable cichlids in the aquarium
hobby. Native to the fast-flowing rapids and cascades of the lower Congo River in Central Africa, this fish differs
dramatically from most of its Cichlidae relatives in both appearance and lifestyle.

The most striking feature of _Steatocranus casuarius_ is the large fatty hump on its forehead, which is especially
pronounced in adult males and gives the fish its common name. The body is robust and elongated, with a flattened head
and powerful pectoral fins. The coloration is predominantly grey-brown with an olive hue, providing excellent camouflage
among rocks. Males grow up to 10-12 cm, while females are smaller at around 8 cm.

Unlike most cichlids, the Buffalo Cichlid leads a predominantly bottom-dwelling life. The swim bladder in this species
is partially reduced — an adaptation to life in fast-flowing currents. The fish moves with characteristic short hops
along the bottom, darting from one shelter to another. This behavior is captivating and makes observing them a genuine
pleasure. With proper care, their lifespan reaches 8-10 years.

## Tank Requirements

A pair of _Steatocranus casuarius_ requires an aquarium of at least 200 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 24-28°C, hardness 10-20 GH, and pH 7.0-8.5. This species tolerates neutral to slightly alkaline water well,
which sets it apart from many Congolese fish that prefer softer water.

In the wild, Buffalo Cichlids live among rock piles in turbulent streams, and the aquarium setup should reflect this
habitat. Arrange the bottom with large rocks, creating numerous caves, crevices, and labyrinths. Each fish, and
especially a bonded pair, needs its own shelter. A sandy substrate or fine gravel works best.

Live plants can be used, but choose hardy species — anubias attached to rocks and java moss are ideal, as Buffalo
Cichlids actively rearrange the space around their dwelling. Filtration should be powerful: these fish are accustomed to
highly oxygenated water. Creating moderate current will benefit them — this is one of the few cichlids that genuinely
appreciates water movement in the aquarium.

## Feeding and Diet

_Steatocranus casuarius_ is an omnivorous species with a preference for meaty foods. In the wild, they feed on small
invertebrates, insect larvae, and algae growing on rocks.

In the aquarium, the staple diet should consist of high-quality sinking pellets formulated for cichlids — specifically
sinking, as Buffalo Cichlids rarely rise to the surface to feed. Regularly supplement their diet with live or frozen
foods: bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, and tubifex. From personal experience, regular live food feedings make the
fish noticeably more active and eager to display their natural behavior.

A vegetable component is also important — offer blanched pieces of zucchini, cucumber, or spirulina. Feed adult fish 1-2
times daily in small portions. Buffalo Cichlids are prone to overeating, so control the amount of food to ensure it is
consumed within 2-3 minutes.

## Care and Maintenance

The care difficulty for _Steatocranus casuarius_ is moderate (3 out of 5). These fish are fairly hardy but require
attention to water quality and proper tank setup.

Weekly water changes of 25-30% are critical, given the need for powerful filtration and high oxygen levels. Regularly
test water parameters, especially nitrate levels — they should not exceed 20-25 mg/l. Good aeration is essential.

Keep in mind the territorial nature of Buffalo Cichlids. They defend their cave and surrounding territory, which
intensifies during breeding season. Aggression is typically directed at other bottom-dwelling fish that intrude on the
pair's territory. Proper space organization with plenty of hiding spots and visual barriers is the key to peaceful
cohabitation.

When transporting or moving the fish, exercise caution: Buffalo Cichlids are skilled jumpers and can escape from an open
tank, so a tight-fitting lid is essential.

## Compatibility

_Steatocranus casuarius_ has a moderate aggression level (3 out of 5) and can be kept in a community tank provided tank
mates are chosen carefully.

Good companions include:

- [Congo Tetras](/en/phenacogrammus-interruptus) — active schooling fish occupying the upper and middle water column,
  posing no territorial competition
- [Synodontis](/en/synodontis-eupterus) — large African catfish capable of holding their own
- [Ctenopoma](/en/ctenopoma-acutirostre) — a calm predator from the same geographic range, occupying the middle water
  column
- [African Butterfly Fish](/en/pantodon-buchholzi) — a surface-dwelling species that does not compete for bottom
  territory

Avoid keeping them with small peaceful fish that could be bullied or eaten. It is not recommended to house Buffalo
Cichlids with other bottom-dwelling territorial cichlids — conflicts are inevitable in limited space. Cohabitation with
[Kribensis](/en/pelvicachromis-pulcher) is only feasible in spacious tanks of 300 liters or more with abundant shelters.

## Breeding

Breeding _Steatocranus casuarius_ is a considerable challenge (difficulty 4 out of 5), but achievable with patience.
They are cave spawners that form stable monogamous pairs.

To stimulate spawning, raise the temperature to 26-28°C and provide the pair with a spacious cave — a ceramic pot, an
inverted coconut shell with an opening, or a rock arrangement with an interior cavity will work well. Pair formation
takes time, and not every male and female will accept each other. The best approach is to raise a group of 6-8 juveniles
and allow pairs to form naturally.

A female ready to spawn displays a noticeably rounded belly and spends increasing time inside the cave. The female lays
50-100 large eggs on the ceiling or walls of the cave. Both parents participate in brood care: the female fans the eggs
with her fins and removes unfertilized ones, while the male guards the territory around the nest.

Incubation lasts 3-5 days at 26-28°C. The larvae feed on their yolk sacs for the first few days, then begin moving
freely along the bottom after 7-10 days. First foods should be baby brine shrimp and microworms. The parents continue to
care for the fry for several weeks, which is one of the most fascinating aspects of this species. Watching a pair escort
their brood on a "walk" around the aquarium is an unforgettable sight for any aquarist.
