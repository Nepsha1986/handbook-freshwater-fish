## Overview

_Nomorhamphus liemi_ is a small livebearing fish from the family Zenarchopteridae, endemic to highland streams on the
island of Sulawesi (Celebes), Indonesia. The most distinctive feature of this species is its characteristic "half-beak"
— an elongated lower jaw that extends well beyond the upper jaw. Males possess a fleshy red-and-black appendage at the
tip of the lower jaw, which is used for courtship displays.

The body is streamlined and silver-grey, reaching 6–8 cm in length. _Nomorhamphus liemi_ inhabits the water surface and
is active during the day. Unlike most tropical fish, this species prefers moderately hard, slightly alkaline water,
which reflects the conditions of the highland streams in its native range. Lifespan in an aquarium is 3–5 years.

## Tank Requirements

A tank of at least 60 litres is suitable for keeping _Nomorhamphus liemi_. Optimal water parameters are: temperature
22–28 °C, pH 7.0–8.0, and general hardness GH 10–20. These fish prefer moderate water flow that mimics highland stream
conditions.

The aquarium should be densely planted, including floating plants that create shaded areas near the surface. This helps
the fish feel secure and reduces stress. A tight-fitting lid is essential — halfbeaks are excellent jumpers and can
easily escape from an uncovered tank. It is advisable to leave an air gap of several centimetres between the lid and the
water surface.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Nomorhamphus liemi_ feeds at the water surface, consuming small insects, their larvae, and invertebrates
that have fallen onto the water. In the aquarium, the diet should include high-quality flakes and pellets for
surface-feeding fish, as well as live and frozen foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, and mosquito larvae.

It is particularly beneficial to occasionally offer small insects (fruit flies, gnats), which closely replicate the
natural diet. Feed small portions 2–3 times a day. Food should remain on the surface, as halfbeaks very rarely pick up
food from the bottom.

## Care and Maintenance

Caring for _Nomorhamphus liemi_ requires attention to water quality. Weekly water changes of 20–30 % are essential for
maintaining stable parameters. Efficient filtration with moderate flow is necessary, as the fish are sensitive to the
accumulation of organic waste.

Water parameters should be checked regularly, with special attention to hardness and pH, since halfbeaks do not tolerate
soft, acidic water well. It is important to inspect the tank lid for any gaps that could serve as escape routes. With
proper care, these fish rarely fall ill and reward the keeper with active surface-level behaviour.

## Compatibility

_Nomorhamphus liemi_ is a relatively peaceful species, though males can be aggressive toward one another. The ideal
arrangement is one male with several females (a ratio of 1:3–4). Suitable tank mates include peaceful fish that occupy
the middle and lower levels: [corydoras catfish](/en/corydoras-aeneus), [rasboras](/en/rasbora-trilineata), and small
[tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-amandae).

Avoid keeping halfbeaks with large aggressive species or with fish that compete for the surface layer. Very small fish
that halfbeaks might view as prey are also unsuitable. Good companions include livebearers with similar water hardness
requirements, such as [mollies](/en/poecilia-sphenops).

## Breeding

_Nomorhamphus liemi_ is one of the few livebearing species outside the family Poeciliidae. Males possess a modified anal
fin called an andropodium, which is used for internal fertilisation. Males are easily identified by this fin and the
red-and-black fleshy appendage on their lower jaw; females are larger with more rounded bellies.

Gestation lasts approximately 6–8 weeks. The female gives birth to 5–20 fully formed fry. For successful breeding,
provide dense vegetation and floating plants where the fry can hide from adult fish. Newborn fry should ideally be moved
to a separate rearing tank and fed with brine shrimp nauplii and finely crushed flakes. Fry grow relatively quickly and
reach sexual maturity at 4–6 months of age.
