## Overview

_Sawbwa resplendens_ is the sole member of the genus Sawbwa within the family Cyprinidae, endemic to Inle Lake in
Myanmar. This tiny fish, reaching only 3–4 cm in length, captivates aquarists with its striking appearance: males
display a silvery-blue body with vivid red markings on the head and tail, while females are more subtly colored in
olive-silver tones. Interestingly, _Sawbwa resplendens_ is not a true rasbora — it earned this common name due to its
superficial resemblance to the [rummy-nose tetra](/en/hemigrammus-bleheri).

In the wild, these fish inhabit shallow areas of Inle Lake with dense aquatic vegetation and crystal-clear water. Their
natural habitat is threatened by human activity, making captive breeding particularly important for species
conservation. With proper care, they live 3–5 years.

## Tank Requirements

A group of 8–10 individuals requires a tank of at least 60 liters. Optimal water parameters: temperature 22–26°C, pH
6.5–7.5, general hardness (GH) 5–12. _Sawbwa resplendens_ prefer clean, well-oxygenated water with minimal current — in
nature, they inhabit still or slow-moving waters.

Plant the tank densely with live vegetation, including fine-leaved species (cabomba, hornwort) and floating plants to
create diffused lighting. A dark substrate accentuates the fish's coloration. Driftwood and rocks make excellent
additions, providing shelter and naturally softening the water. Avoid bright direct lighting — under moderate light, the
fish feel more secure and display their best colors.

## Feeding and Diet

In nature, _Sawbwa resplendens_ feed on small invertebrates, algae, and microorganisms. In the aquarium, they accept
quality dry foods — micro-pellets and crushed flakes. However, live and frozen foods should form the core of their diet:
baby brine shrimp, daphnia, cyclops, and micro worms. This type of nutrition is key to maintaining the vivid red
pigmentation on the head and tail.

Feed twice daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. Keep in mind that these miniature fish have
small mouths, so food must be appropriately sized. Dietary variety is essential for health and vibrant coloration.

## Care and Maintenance

_Sawbwa resplendens_ is a relatively undemanding species provided water parameters remain stable. Weekly water changes
of 20–25% are essential. Use a quality filter with gentle flow — strong currents stress these fish. A sponge filter is
the optimal choice: it provides biological filtration without creating a dangerous current for small fish.

These fish are sensitive to sudden fluctuations in water parameters. Use aged water at the same temperature when
performing changes. Keep nitrate levels below 20 mg/l. Test water parameters regularly, especially during the first
months after the tank is established.

## Compatibility

_Sawbwa resplendens_ are peaceful schooling fish that should be kept in groups of at least 8. Within the school, a
natural hierarchy forms, with males displaying fascinating behavior as they compete for female attention and show their
finest colors.

Ideal tankmates are small peaceful species of similar size: [Danio erythromicron](/en/danio-erythromicron) (a fellow
Inle Lake native), [chili rasbora](/en/boraras-brigittae), [celestial pearl danio](/en/danio-margaritatus),
[pygmy corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus). They coexist well with [otocinclus](/en/otocinclus-vittatus) and small shrimp
(Neocaridina).

Avoid larger or active fish — [black skirt tetras](/en/gymnocorymbus-ternetzi), large barbs, or cichlids. These will
stress the sawbwa and outcompete them for food. Also avoid fish that require strong water flow.

## Breeding

Breeding _Sawbwa resplendens_ in the aquarium is quite achievable, though it requires some preparation. The fish scatter
eggs among fine-leaved plants and show no parental care.

For spawning, set up a separate 20–30 liter tank with soft water (GH 3–6, pH 6.5–7.0) and a temperature of 24–26°C.
Place clumps of Java moss or synthetic spawning mops as substrate. Select a well-colored pair and feed them abundantly
with live food for 1–2 weeks before introducing them to the breeding tank.

After spawning, remove the adults promptly — they will readily eat their own eggs. The eggs are small and transparent,
with incubation lasting 3–5 days. Newly hatched larvae feed on their yolk sacs for the first day, after which they
should be offered infusoria and micro worms. After 7–10 days, the fry can accept baby brine shrimp. Growth is slow —
adult coloration typically appears at 3–4 months of age.
