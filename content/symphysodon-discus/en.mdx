## Overview

_Symphysodon discus_, also known as the Heckel Discus, is a freshwater fish belonging to the family Cichlidae, native to
the Amazon River basin. This species was first described in 1840 by Johann Jacob Heckel, after whom it is named. In the
wild, Heckel Discus is found predominantly in the waters of the Rio Negro — a tributary of the Amazon known for its
soft, acidic, and dark-stained water.

The Heckel Discus is distinguished from the closely related _Symphysodon aequifasciatus_
([Blue Discus](/en/symphysodon-aequifasciatus)) by its characteristic wide fifth vertical bar running through the center
of its body. Adults reach 15–20 cm in size, and with proper care, their lifespan can reach 10–15 years. The body is
strongly laterally compressed and nearly circular in shape — a feature that makes discus fish among the most
recognizable aquarium species.

The Heckel Discus is considered the most demanding of all discus varieties, making it a fish for experienced aquarists.

## Tank Requirements

A group of Heckel Discus requires an aquarium of at least 300 liters. These fish are extremely sensitive to water
parameters, so stability is the key to success.

**Optimal water parameters:**

- Temperature: 26–30 °C (ideally 28–29 °C)
- pH: 5.5–7.5 (preferably 5.5–6.5)
- General Hardness (GH): 1–10 dGH (soft water below 5 dGH is best)

The aquarium should be equipped with a powerful external filter providing excellent biological filtration. Water flow
should be moderate — discus do not appreciate strong currents. Lighting is best kept subdued, using floating plants to
diffuse the light.

Driftwood and densely planted areas with shade-tolerant plants work well as decoration. It is important to leave
sufficient open swimming space. The substrate should be fine sand or none at all (a so-called "bare-bottom tank"), which
makes maintaining cleanliness much easier.

## Feeding and Diet

_Symphysodon discus_ is an omnivorous species, but a varied and high-quality diet is essential for maintaining health
and vibrant coloration.

**Staple diet:**

- Specialized discus granules and flakes
- Frozen bloodworms, brine shrimp, and glassworms
- Beef heart (finely minced, fat-free) — a classic discus food

**Supplementary foods:**

- Spirulina and vegetable-based supplements
- Live foods (daphnia, cyclops) as occasional treats

Discus should be fed 2–3 times daily in small portions. Uneaten food should be removed within 5–10 minutes to prevent
water quality deterioration. Juveniles benefit from more frequent feeding — up to 4–5 times daily for optimal growth.

## Care and Maintenance

The Heckel Discus is one of the most demanding aquarium fish, and successful keeping requires discipline and attention
to detail.

**Regular water changes** are the cornerstone of discus care. It is recommended to change 25–30% of the water 2–3 times
per week, and even more frequently in tanks housing juvenile fish. Replacement water must match the temperature and
parameters of the aquarium water.

Discus must be kept in groups of at least 5–6 individuals — they are schooling fish that become stressed and lose
appetite when kept alone. Within a group, a natural hierarchy forms, and the fish display more vivid coloration and
active behavior.

Regularly monitor ammonia levels (should be 0), nitrites (0), and nitrates (no more than 20 mg/l). Use reliable water
test kits and keep a parameter log — this will help you catch deviations early.

## Compatibility

_Symphysodon discus_ is a peaceful fish, but its high temperature requirements and sensitivity to water quality limit
the choice of tank mates.

**Good tank mates:**

- [Cardinal Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-axelrodi) — the classic discus companion
- [Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi) and [Green Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-simulans)
- [Sterba's Corydoras](/en/corydoras-sterbai) — one of the few corydoras species that tolerates high temperatures well
- [Otocinclus](/en/otocinclus-vittatus) — helpful in controlling algae
- [Blue Discus](/en/symphysodon-aequifasciatus) — can be kept together, though crossbreeding is not recommended

**Unsuitable tank mates:**

- Large and aggressive cichlids
- Barbs and other fin-nipping species
- Small, hyperactive fish that cause stress
- Shrimp and small invertebrates — may become food

During spawning, discus become territorial, and this should be considered when planning tank inhabitants.

## Breeding

Breeding _Symphysodon discus_ is a moderately challenging task that requires patience and experience. Discus form stable
monogamous pairs that may stay together for several years.

To stimulate spawning, it is recommended to lower the pH slightly (to 5.5–6.0), raise the temperature to 30–31 °C, and
ensure soft water (GH 1–3). The pair selects a vertical surface — a broad plant leaf, a spawning cone, or the tank wall
— and meticulously cleans it before laying eggs.

The female deposits 100–300 eggs, which both parents guard and fan with their fins. Incubation lasts approximately 48–60
hours. A unique feature of discus is that fry feed on the parents' skin secretion during their first days, attaching
themselves to the parents' flanks. This is why fry must remain with their parents for at least 2–3 weeks.

For successful breeding, it is best to set up a dedicated spawning tank of at least 100 liters, with no substrate and
minimal decoration. This makes maintaining cleanliness easier and reduces stress from the presence of tank mates.
