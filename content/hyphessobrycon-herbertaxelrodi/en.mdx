## Overview

_Hyphessobrycon herbertaxelrodi_ is a small characin from the family Characidae, widely known among aquarists as the
Black Neon Tetra. In the wild, this species inhabits the Taquari River basin in Mato Grosso do Sul, Brazil, where it
lives in shaded forest streams with soft, slightly acidic water.

The Black Neon Tetra reaches a size of 4-5 cm and features an elegant coloration: a broad black stripe runs along the
body, with a bright iridescent greenish-white line above it. This striking contrast gives the fish its distinctive and
recognizable appearance. The body is elongated and slightly laterally compressed, with transparent fins. Despite the
name, the Black Neon Tetra is not a close relative of the [common neon tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi) — they belong to
different genera.

Thanks to its peaceful temperament, hardiness, and undemanding nature, this fish is an excellent choice for both
beginners and experienced aquarists. In a school of six or more, Black Neon Tetras display their natural behavior and
look particularly impressive.

## Tank Requirements

A school of Black Neon Tetras can be kept in an aquarium with a minimum volume of 40 liters. Optimal water parameters
are: temperature 24-28°C, pH 6.0-7.5, and general hardness (GH) 5-15. The fish prefer soft, slightly acidic water that
approximates the conditions of their natural biotope.

The aquarium should be densely planted with live vegetation while leaving enough open swimming space. Floating plants
help create the subdued lighting that Black Neon Tetras prefer in the wild. Driftwood and fallen leaves make excellent
decorations — they tint the water with a yellowish hue and release beneficial tannins.

A dark substrate is recommended, as it will make the fish's coloration appear significantly more vivid. Be sure to
provide quality filtration and moderate water flow.

## Feeding and Diet

_Hyphessobrycon herbertaxelrodi_ is an omnivorous species with no special dietary requirements. The staple diet can
consist of high-quality dry foods in flake or micro-pellet form. To maintain health and vibrant coloration, the diet
should be supplemented with live and frozen foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, cyclops, and small-sized bloodworms.

In the wild, Black Neon Tetras feed on small invertebrates, insect larvae, and plant matter. Occasional vegetable
supplements (spirulina, blanched vegetables) are therefore also beneficial.

Feed the fish 2-3 times daily in small portions that are consumed within 2-3 minutes. Overfeeding can lead to
deteriorating water quality and health problems.

## Care and Maintenance

The Black Neon Tetra is a hardy fish with a lifespan of 3-5 years when properly cared for. The basic care guidelines are
straightforward:

- Weekly water changes of 20-25% to maintain stable parameters.
- Regular monitoring of temperature, pH, and water hardness using test kits.
- Timely filter cleaning and removal of organic debris from the bottom.
- Maintaining a consistent lighting schedule (8-10 hours per day).

Black Neon Tetras are fairly resistant to diseases, but under poor conditions they may be susceptible to ich (white spot
disease) and fungal infections. Quarantining new fish and keeping the aquarium clean are the best preventive measures.

## Compatibility

The Black Neon Tetra is an exceptionally peaceful schooling fish that is perfectly suited for community aquariums. They
should be kept in groups of at least 6-8 individuals — in a school, the fish feel more confident and display more
vibrant coloration.

Excellent tank mates include other peaceful characins: [flame tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-flammeus),
[black phantom tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-megalopterus), and [cardinal tetra](/en/paracheirodon-axelrodi). Black Neon
Tetras also get along well with [corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda), small rasboras, and peaceful dwarf cichlids.

It is not recommended to keep Black Neon Tetras with large or aggressive species that may see these small fish as prey.
Tank mates prone to fin nipping should also be avoided.

## Breeding

Breeding _Hyphessobrycon herbertaxelrodi_ at home requires some experience but is quite achievable. Prepare a separate
breeding tank of 10-20 liters with soft water (GH 2-4), slightly acidic pH (5.5-6.5), and a temperature of 26-28°C.

Place a spawning grid or fine-leaved plants (such as Java moss) on the bottom to protect the eggs from being eaten by
the parents. Lighting should be dim.

Before spawning, separate the breeding pair for 7-10 days and feed them generously with live foods. Spawning typically
occurs in the morning hours. The female lays 100-150 small eggs, after which the adults must be removed from the
breeding tank.

The eggs develop within 24-36 hours, and the fry begin free-swimming after 3-4 days. First food should be infusoria and
brine shrimp nauplii. The fry grow slowly and require clean water and regular feeding in small portions.
