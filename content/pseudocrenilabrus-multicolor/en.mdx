## Overview

_Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ is one of the first cichlids ever kept in home aquariums, with records of its captive
care dating back to the early 1900s. This species belongs to the family Cichlidae and inhabits freshwater bodies across
Eastern and Northeastern Africa — from the Nile basin to the lakes of Uganda and Kenya. In the wild, it occupies
shallow, densely vegetated areas of rivers, lakes, and swamps.

Adults reach 5–7 cm in length and live 5–7 years with proper care. Males are significantly more colorful than females:
their bodies shimmer with iridescent shades of blue, green, and yellow, while their anal fins display distinctive
orange-red egg spots (ocelli). Females are more modestly colored in silvery-beige tones, which helps them remain
inconspicuous during the brooding period.

The defining feature of this species is maternal mouthbrooding — the female incubates fertilized eggs inside her mouth.
This remarkable behavior earned _Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ its common names: "Egyptian Mouthbrooder" and
"Multicolored Mouthbrooder." Its hardiness, compact size, and captivating behavior make this fish an excellent choice
for aquarists of any experience level.

## Tank Requirements

A pair of _Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ requires an aquarium of at least 60 liters. If you plan to keep a small group
of one male and several females, opt for a tank of 100 liters or more — this will help reduce territorial conflicts.

Optimal water parameters:

- Temperature: 22–28 °C
- General hardness (GH): 8–15
- pH: 6.5–7.5

One of the greatest advantages of this species is its remarkable adaptability to varying water conditions. These fish
tolerate fluctuations in water parameters well, making them a forgiving choice for beginners.

For the aquascape, use a sandy or fine gravel substrate, plenty of live plants (such as Vallisneria, Anubias, and Java
moss), and shelters made from rocks and driftwood. Caves and grottos are especially important — they serve as
territorial markers and refuges for brooding females. Moderate or subdued lighting is preferred, mimicking the fish's
natural habitat.

## Feeding and Diet

_Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ is an omnivorous species with an excellent appetite. In the wild, these fish feed on
small invertebrates, insect larvae, and algal growth. In the aquarium, the staple diet should consist of high-quality
flakes or small cichlid pellets.

To maintain vibrant coloration and robust health, regularly supplement dry food with live and frozen options:

- Brine shrimp (both nauplii and adults)
- Daphnia
- Bloodworms (in moderation)
- Cyclops

Vegetable supplements are also beneficial: blanched spinach, cucumber slices, or spirulina-based foods. Feed 2–3 times
daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. Note that the female does not eat during the egg
incubation period, so it is important to feed her well before spawning.

## Care and Maintenance

Caring for _Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ is straightforward, even for a beginner aquarist. The key principle is
maintaining stable water parameters and a clean aquarium.

Core recommendations:

- Water changes: 20–25 % weekly
- Filtration: moderate flow; an internal filter or a small canister filter will suffice
- Parameter monitoring: regularly test pH, hardness, and nitrate levels
- Aeration: ensure adequate oxygen saturation

These fish are diurnal and most active during daylight hours. Males periodically engage in territorial displays, flaring
their fins and showing off their vivid colors — this is normal behavior and requires no intervention. Make sure the tank
provides enough hiding spots for females, especially during the breeding season.

## Compatibility

_Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ is a moderately territorial species. Outside of the breeding period, these fish behave
quite peacefully and are well-suited for community tanks with appropriately sized and tempered tankmates.

Good tankmates include:

- Small characins (tetras)
- [Rasboras](/en/rasbora-trilineata) and small barbs
- [Corydoras](/en/corydoras-aeneus) and other peaceful catfish
- [Danios](/en/danio-rerio)

Avoid housing them with:

- Large, aggressive cichlids
- Slow-moving fish with long, flowing fins
- Very small species that may become targets

During spawning, the male actively defends his territory, so ensure the aquarium is spacious enough and contains zones
separated by decorations. Keeping one male with two to three females is the optimal ratio, reducing pressure on
individual fish.

## Breeding

Breeding _Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ is one of the most rewarding experiences for an aquarist, and it requires
minimal effort. This species is a true maternal mouthbrooder: the female collects the fertilized eggs in her mouth and
incubates them for 10–14 days.

Spawning proceeds as follows: the male selects a patch of substrate, cleans it, and begins actively displaying his
coloration to attract a female. After the eggs are laid and fertilized, the female gathers them into her buccal cavity.
A single clutch typically contains 30 to 80 eggs, depending on the female's size and age.

During incubation, the female does not eat and tends to stay hidden in sheltered areas. At the end of the brooding
period, she releases fully formed fry that are already capable of swimming and feeding independently. For the first few
days, the fry may retreat into the mother's mouth when they sense danger.

First foods for the fry:

- Brine shrimp nauplii
- Microworms
- Finely crushed dry food

For maximum fry survival, it is recommended to move the brooding female to a separate rearing tank of 20–30 liters.
_Pseudocrenilabrus multicolor_ reaches sexual maturity at 4–6 months of age, and under good conditions, spawning occurs
regularly.
