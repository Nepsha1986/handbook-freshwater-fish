## Overview

_Moenkhausia pittieri_ is a freshwater fish from the family Characidae, endemic to the Lake Valencia basin in northern
Venezuela. It earned the name "Diamond Tetra" thanks to its unique iridescent scales that create a stunning diamond-like
shimmer under side lighting. This effect is particularly striking in mature specimens, whose scales reflect a full
spectrum of colors.

Diamond Tetras reach a size of 5-6 cm and live for 3 to 5 years. Sexual dimorphism is clearly visible: males develop
elongated dorsal and anal fins as they mature, which take on an impressive veil-like form. Females have more modest fins
and a rounder body.

These are peaceful schooling fish that thrive best in groups of six or more. In a school, they display natural behavior
and their most vibrant coloration, especially when males compete for the attention of females.

## Tank Requirements

A group of Diamond Tetras requires an aquarium with a minimum volume of 75 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 24-28°C, pH 6.0-7.5, and hardness (GH) 5-15 dGH. These fish prefer clean, well-filtered water with moderate
flow.

To best showcase the beauty of their iridescent scales, a dark substrate and side lighting are recommended. The aquarium
should be densely planted with live vegetation while leaving enough open space for swimming. Floating plants will help
create subdued light, closer to their natural habitat conditions.

Driftwood and rocks provide additional shelter and help the fish feel more confident. A tight-fitting lid is essential,
as tetras are prone to jumping.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Moenkhausia pittieri_ is an omnivorous species that feeds on small invertebrates, insects falling onto the
water surface, and plant matter. In the aquarium, they readily accept a variety of foods.

The staple diet can consist of high-quality flakes and micro-pellets. To enhance coloration and maintain health, the
diet should be regularly supplemented with live or frozen foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, and cyclops.
Vegetable supplements such as spirulina are also beneficial.

Feed 2-3 times daily in small portions that the fish can consume within 2-3 minutes. A varied diet is the key to vibrant
coloration and a strong immune system.

## Care and Maintenance

Diamond Tetras are relatively undemanding fish with a below-average care difficulty level. Weekly water changes of
25-30% will help maintain stable parameters and a clean environment. Regular monitoring of ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate
levels with test kits is important.

Filtration should be effective but not create an excessively strong current. An external canister filter or an internal
filter with adjustable flow is recommended.

With proper care, these fish rarely fall ill. However, deteriorating water quality may make them susceptible to ich and
bacterial infections. Quarantining new fish and maintaining stable conditions are the best preventive measures.

## Compatibility

_Moenkhausia pittieri_ is a peaceful species that makes an excellent addition to a community aquarium with other
non-aggressive fish of comparable size. Outstanding tank mates include other South American tetras:
[neon tetras](/en/paracheirodon-innesi), [cardinal tetras](/en/paracheirodon-axelrodi),
[black neon tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-herbertaxelrodi), and [Congo tetras](/en/phenacogrammus-interruptus).

Among bottom-dwelling fish, good companions include [bronze corydoras](/en/corydoras-aeneus),
[panda corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda), and [bristlenose plecos](/en/ancistrus-dolichopterus). Calm rasboras such as
[harlequin rasboras](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha) also coexist beautifully with Diamond Tetras.

Avoid keeping them with large or aggressive species, as well as fin-nipping fish — the males' long fins may become
targets. Keeping them in a school of at least six reduces stress and minimizes intraspecific competition.

## Breeding

Breeding _Moenkhausia pittieri_ in the aquarium is achievable, though it requires some preparation. Set up a separate
breeding tank of 20-30 liters with soft, slightly acidic water (pH 6.0-6.5) and a temperature of 26-28°C. Place
fine-leaved plants such as Java moss or a synthetic spawning mop on the bottom.

Selecting a breeding pair is straightforward: males are distinguished by their elongated dorsal and anal fins and a more
slender body. Before spawning, condition the breeders with generous live food feedings for 1-2 weeks.

Spawning typically occurs in the morning. The female scatters eggs among the plants, after which the adults must be
removed to prevent egg predation. Incubation lasts 24-36 hours. During the first days, fry feed on infusoria, then
transition to newly hatched brine shrimp. The fry tank should be kept dark, as eggs and larvae are sensitive to light.
