## Overview

The Odessa Barb (_Pethia padamya_) is a small and strikingly beautiful freshwater fish from the Cyprinidae family,
native to the rivers and streams of central Myanmar. Although the species was only formally described in 2008, it had
been known in the aquarium hobby long before under the name "Odessa barb" — it is believed the fish entered the trade
through aquarists in Odessa, Ukraine, during the 1970s.

Adults reach 5–7 cm in length and live 5–7 years with proper care. The males' most distinctive feature is a broad, vivid
red stripe running along the entire body from head to tail, set against silvery-olive scales with dark speckling.
Females are more modestly colored in golden-silver tones but remain attractive in their own right. The body is compact
and slightly elongated, typical of the barb shape.

_Pethia padamya_ is a diurnal, active, and schooling fish. In groups of 6 or more, the barbs display natural behavior:
males engage in impressive "tournaments," competing with one another in color intensity. Keeping them in a school also
reduces stress and makes the fish more confident.

### How to Distinguish Males from Females?

Sexual dimorphism in _Pethia padamya_ is clearly pronounced:

- **Coloration**: Males possess a vivid red-orange stripe along the body; females are pale yellowish without a distinct
  red stripe.
- **Body shape**: Females are larger and fuller, especially in the belly area. Males are slimmer and more compact.
- **Color intensity**: Males' coloration becomes especially vivid during breeding and when competing with other males.

## Tank Requirements

A school of 6–8 Odessa Barbs requires an aquarium of at least **80 liters**. The fish are active and need ample open
swimming space, so elongated tanks are preferred.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature**: 22–26°C
- **pH**: 6.0–7.5
- **Hardness (dGH)**: 5–10

The aquarium layout should combine dense vegetation along the back and side walls with open space in the center. A dark
substrate of fine gravel or sand will highlight the males' vibrant coloration. Add several pieces of driftwood and rocks
for shelter. Floating plants help create soft, diffused lighting that approximates natural conditions. Filtration should
be moderate, without strong current. A lid is essential, as barbs can jump out of the water when startled.

## Feeding and Diet

_Pethia padamya_ is an omnivorous species that presents no feeding challenges. The diet should consist of:

- High-quality flakes or small granules for tropical fish (daily staple)
- Frozen foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, cyclops
- Live foods: small bloodworms, daphnia, tubifex — excellent supplementary feeding, especially before breeding
- Vegetable supplements: blanched spinach, zucchini, spirulina tablets

Feed twice daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. A varied diet with regular inclusion of
live and frozen foods is the key to maintaining the males' intense red coloration. Carotenoids found in brine shrimp and
cyclops are particularly beneficial for color enhancement.

## Care and Maintenance

The Odessa Barb is an undemanding species with a care level of 2 out of 5, suitable even for beginning aquarists. Key
recommendations:

- **Water changes**: 20–25% weekly
- **Filtration**: an internal or external filter with moderate flow
- **Aeration**: recommended to maintain optimal oxygen levels
- **Lighting**: moderate; under bright light, the males' red stripe appears especially striking

Regularly monitor ammonia and nitrite levels — both should remain at zero. Keep nitrate concentrations below 20 mg/l.
Vacuum the substrate during each water change to remove food remnants and organic waste. Observe your fish closely:
healthy Odessa Barbs are active, have good appetites, and display vivid coloration. Lethargy, color loss, or refusal to
eat are early signs of trouble.

## Compatibility

_Pethia padamya_ is a peaceful and sociable species, well-suited for community aquariums. Unlike some other barbs, the
Odessa Barb very rarely nips the fins of tank mates, making it one of the best candidates for a mixed community.

Excellent tank mates:

- [Rosy Barb](/en/pethia-conchonius) and [Black Ruby Barb](/en/pethia-nigrofasciata) — related species with similar
  temperament
- [Zebra Danio](/en/danio-rerio) — active and fast, a great match in personality
- [Neon Tetras](/en/paracheirodon-innesi) and [Cardinal Tetras](/en/paracheirodon-axelrodi) — peaceful schooling tetras
- [Harlequin Rasboras](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha) — calm and non-confrontational
- [Panda Corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda) — peaceful bottom dwellers that complement the mid-level swimmers
- [Black Skirt Tetra](/en/gymnocorymbus-ternetzi) — an active tetra of suitable size

Avoid keeping with large or aggressive cichlids. Slow-moving, long-finned species such as [Bettas](/en/betta-splendens)
are also not recommended as tank mates, although the risk of nipping from Odessa Barbs is minimal.

## Breeding

Breeding _Pethia padamya_ is a moderately challenging task, achievable for aquarists with basic experience. This is an
egg-scattering species with no parental care.

To set up for spawning:

- A separate breeding tank of 20–30 liters
- Soft water at 24–26°C with a pH around 6.5
- Fine-leaved plants (Java moss, Cabomba) or a synthetic spawning mop
- A protective mesh over the bottom to prevent egg predation

For 1–2 weeks before the planned spawn, feed the breeding group generously with live foods to stimulate the maturation
of reproductive products. In the evening, introduce a pair or a group (2 males to 1 female) into the breeding tank.
Spawning typically occurs the following morning. The female deposits up to 150–200 semi-transparent eggs, which fall
among the plants or under the mesh.

Immediately after spawning, remove the adults — they will actively consume their own eggs. Larvae hatch within 24–48
hours, and after 3–4 days the fry become free-swimming. Initial food should be infusoria and liquid fry food, followed
by baby brine shrimp. By 2–3 months, the juveniles develop the species' characteristic coloration, with the males' red
stripe appearing at around 4–5 months.
