## Overview

_Neolamprologus leleupi_, commonly known as the Lemon Cichlid, is one of the most striking cichlids from Lake Tanganyika
in East Africa. This species captivates aquarists with its uniform bright yellow-orange coloration, making it one of the
most vividly colored Tanganyikan cichlids.

In the wild, _Neolamprologus leleupi_ inhabits the rocky coastal zones of Lake Tanganyika, hiding in crevices and caves
among the rocks. Its body is elongated and more slender compared to most other cichlids, allowing it to maneuver easily
through narrow rock crevices. Adults reach a size of 9–12 cm. With proper care, their lifespan is 8–10 years.

By temperament, _Neolamprologus leleupi_ is a semi-aggressive fish with pronounced territorial behavior, especially
during the breeding season. The care level is considered intermediate, making this species better suited for aquarists
with some experience keeping cichlids.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Neolamprologus leleupi_ requires an aquarium of at least 100 liters. The setup should include numerous rocks
forming caves, grottos, and crevices — this replicates the natural habitat and helps reduce aggression between fish.

Water parameters should match Lake Tanganyika conditions — hard, alkaline water: temperature 23–27 °C, pH 7.5–9.0,
general hardness (GH) 7–20. Parameter stability is crucial, as this species is sensitive to sudden fluctuations.
Powerful filtration and weekly water changes of 15–20% are essential.

A sandy or fine gravel substrate works best. Lighting can be moderate. Live plants are not necessary for a tank with
this species, but hardy varieties such as Anubias can be used if desired.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Neolamprologus leleupi_ feeds on small invertebrates, insect larvae, and zooplankton found in crevices
between rocks. In the aquarium, the diet should be varied and protein-based.

High-quality cichlid pellets and flakes can form the dietary staple. The diet should be regularly supplemented with
frozen or live foods such as brine shrimp, daphnia, bloodworms, and cyclops. A varied diet helps maintain good health
and the vibrant yellow coloration of these fish.

Feed 2–3 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within a few minutes. Overfeeding should be avoided, as it
negatively affects water quality.

## Care and Maintenance

Regular water changes (15–20% weekly) and quality filtration are the foundation of successful _Neolamprologus leleupi_
care. Ammonia and nitrite levels must be kept at zero, while nitrate levels should remain below 20 mg/L.

It is important to provide ample hiding places in the form of rock caves and crevices. Each fish needs its own
territory, so decorations should be arranged to divide the aquarium into zones. This significantly reduces stress and
intraspecific aggression.

Water parameters should be tested regularly, with prompt action taken if any deviations are detected. _Neolamprologus
leleupi_ does not tolerate unstable conditions well, so maintaining consistent environmental parameters is the key to
these fish's health and longevity.

## Compatibility

_Neolamprologus leleupi_ is best kept in a biotope aquarium replicating Lake Tanganyika conditions, alongside other
species from the same lake. Good tank mates include Julidochromis, Altolamprologus, and shell-dwelling cichlids
(Lamprologus species) that occupy different ecological niches.

When choosing tank mates, it is important to consider that _Neolamprologus leleupi_ actively defends its territory,
especially during the breeding season. This species should not be kept with small, peaceful fish that may become targets
of aggression. Similarly, avoid housing them with large, aggressive species that could dominate the Lemon Cichlids.

To minimize conflicts, the aquarium should be spacious enough with plenty of hiding spots so that each species can
establish its own territory. Keeping a group of several _Neolamprologus leleupi_ together is possible provided the tank
volume is sufficient and shelters are abundant.

## Breeding

Breeding _Neolamprologus leleupi_ is quite achievable in the home aquarium. This is a cave spawner: a pair selects a
secluded spot among the rocks — a cave or crevice — and deposits eggs on the inner surface of the shelter.

The female lays between 50 and 150 eggs and takes the primary role in caring for the clutch, although the male also
participates in defending the territory. During spawning and fry-rearing, the fish become considerably more aggressive
and will resolutely protect their territory from any intruders.

Egg incubation takes approximately 3–4 days at a temperature of 25–26 °C. The fry become free-swimming about one week
after hatching. Suitable first foods include baby brine shrimp and microworms. Parental care continues for several
weeks, after which the young gradually begin to establish their own territories.

For successful breeding, it is important to provide the pair with a dedicated shelter and maintain stable water
parameters. As the fry grow, it is recommended to move them to a separate rearing tank to avoid conflicts with the
parents.
