## Overview

_Paracheirodon axelrodi_ is a small characin of the family Characidae, widely regarded as one of the most beautiful and
commercially important ornamental freshwater fish in the world. In the wild, the Cardinal Tetra inhabits the Rio Negro
and Orinoco River basins in South America, where it thrives in typical blackwater biotopes — slow-moving tributaries
stained dark by tannins from fallen leaves, with extremely low mineral content.

The fish reaches 4–5 cm in length and lives 3–5 years with proper care. Its body is slender and slightly laterally
compressed. A brilliant neon-blue stripe runs along the upper half, while a vivid red stripe extends from the head all
the way to the tail fin. This is the key difference from its closest relative, the
[Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi): in the Neon Tetra, the red stripe covers only the rear half of the body, whereas
in the Cardinal Tetra it spans the entire length, creating a considerably more striking appearance.

The third member of the genus, the [Green Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-simulans), is even smaller and more modestly
colored, with predominantly green-blue tones. All three species are well suited to aquarium keeping, but the Cardinal
Tetra is considered the most impressive due to the intensity of its coloration.

_Paracheirodon axelrodi_ is one of the most commercially significant ornamental fish: millions of specimens are exported
annually from Brazil and Colombia, providing livelihoods for local fishermen while simultaneously serving as an
incentive for the conservation of natural habitats.

## Tank Requirements

A school of _Paracheirodon axelrodi_ requires an aquarium of at least 40 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 24–28 °C, pH 5.5–7.0, general hardness GH 2–10. The Cardinal Tetra is a quintessential soft-water species:
it displays its brightest coloration and most robust health in soft, slightly acidic water. In hard, high-pH water, the
fish become pale and more susceptible to disease.

Ideally, the aquarium setup should replicate a natural blackwater biotope: dark substrate, driftwood, fallen Indian
almond or oak leaves that release tannins and acidify the water. Floating plants will create diffused lighting under
which the neon-blue stripe of the fish literally glows. Dense vegetation along the perimeter provides shelter, while the
central area should remain open for the school to swim freely.

Filtration should be effective but with a gentle flow: Cardinal Tetras prefer the weak currents characteristic of their
natural habitats. A peat filter or the addition of peat extract will help create conditions closely approximating a
blackwater biotope.

## Feeding and Diet

_Paracheirodon axelrodi_ is an omnivorous fish with a small mouth. In the wild, its diet consists of small
invertebrates, insect larvae, zooplankton, and plant detritus. In the aquarium, the staple diet should be high-quality
flakes or micro-pellets formulated for small tropical fish.

Regularly supplement the diet with live and frozen foods: daphnia, brine shrimp, cyclops, and bloodworms. Protein-rich
foods are particularly important for maintaining the vibrant red-and-blue coloration. Occasional plant-based supplements
such as spirulina flakes or finely grated vegetables also benefit overall health.

Feed 1–2 times daily in small portions that the school consumes within 2–3 minutes. Cardinal Tetras feed predominantly
in the middle water column. Avoid overfeeding — leftover food quickly degrades water quality, which is critical for this
pollution-sensitive species.

## Care and Maintenance

The care level for _Paracheirodon axelrodi_ is rated 2 out of 5, making it suitable for aquarists with some basic
experience. The key requirement is stable soft-water parameters without sudden fluctuations.

Weekly water changes of 20–25% are essential. Replacement water should closely match the temperature and hardness of the
aquarium water to avoid stress. Monitor ammonia and nitrite levels — they must remain at zero, while nitrates should
stay below 20 mg/l.

Keep Cardinal Tetras in schools of at least 8–10 individuals: in a large group, the fish feel more secure, display
coordinated schooling behavior, and reveal their coloration to its fullest. In small groups, they become timid, often
hide, and lose their vibrancy.

Cardinal Tetras are susceptible to Neon Tetra Disease (pleistophorosis), a specific condition caused by the parasite
_Pleistophora hyphessobryconis_. Affected fish lose coloration, become emaciated, and isolate themselves from the
school. There is no cure, so the most important prevention measures are quarantining new fish and maintaining pristine
water conditions.

## Compatibility

_Paracheirodon axelrodi_ is an exceptionally peaceful fish with an aggression rating of 1 out of 5, perfectly suited to
community aquariums with non-aggressive inhabitants.

The best tank mates are other small, peaceful species that prefer soft water: [Neon Tetras](/en/paracheirodon-innesi),
[Green Neon Tetras](/en/paracheirodon-simulans), [Black Neon Tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-herbertaxelrodi),
[Glowlight Tetras](/en/hemigrammus-erythrozonus), and [Ember Tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-amandae). Excellent companions
also include [Chili Rasboras](/en/rasbora-brigittae), [Pygmy Corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus), and
[Sterbai Corydoras](/en/corydoras-sterbai).

Among bottom-dwellers, [Otocinclus](/en/otocinclus-vittatus) are an ideal match — peaceful algae eaters that also prefer
soft water. Dwarf cichlids such as [Ram Cichlids](/en/mikrogeophagus-ramirezi) and
[Cockatoo Dwarf Cichlids](/en/apistogramma-cacatuoides) coexist well with Cardinal Tetras provided sufficient tank
volume.

Strictly avoid housing them with large or predatory fish: Cardinal Tetras are easy prey due to their small size. Active
fin-nipping species are also unsuitable tank mates.

## Breeding

Breeding _Paracheirodon axelrodi_ in captivity is rated 3 out of 5 in difficulty — achievable but requiring preparation
and attention to detail. In the wild, spawning coincides with the rainy season and occurs in extremely soft, acidic
water.

Prepare a breeding tank of 10–20 liters with very soft water: GH 1–4, pH 5.0–6.0, temperature 26–28 °C. Sterilize the
tank and equipment to prevent fungal growth on the eggs. Line the bottom with fine-leaved plants such as Java moss or a
spawning grid. Lighting must be minimal: _Paracheirodon axelrodi_ eggs are extremely light-sensitive.

Condition the breeding pair with abundant live foods for 1–2 weeks beforehand. Select a male with the most intense
coloration and a female with a noticeably rounded belly. Introduce the pair to the breeding tank in the evening —
spawning typically occurs the following morning. The female scatters 100–500 small, translucent eggs among the plants.

After spawning, immediately remove the adults and darken the tank. Eggs incubate for 24–36 hours. The larvae feed on
their yolk sacs for the first few days and begin free-swimming on days 4–5. Initial food should be infusoria and liquid
fry food, transitioning to baby brine shrimp after 7–10 days. Fry grow slowly and develop their characteristic
coloration by 2–3 months of age.
