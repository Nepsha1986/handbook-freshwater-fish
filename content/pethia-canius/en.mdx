## Overview

The Canara Barb (_Pethia canius_) is a small freshwater fish from the Cyprinidae family, found in streams and small
rivers of western India, primarily in the state of Karnataka. In the wild, this species inhabits shallow areas with
moderate current, sandy or rocky substrate, and abundant riparian vegetation.

Adults reach only 4–5 cm in length, making the Canara Barb one of the most compact members of the _Pethia_ genus. Life
expectancy in an aquarium is 3–5 years with proper care. The body is elongated, slightly laterally compressed, with a
silvery-olive base tone and distinctive dark markings. The fins are transparent, sometimes with a slight yellowish
tinge.

This is a diurnal, schooling species with a peaceful temperament. The fish actively move through the middle and upper
water layers, constantly exploring the aquarium space. _Pethia canius_ should be kept in groups of 6–8 individuals — in
a school they feel more confident, and males display brighter coloration as they compete for female attention.

## Tank Requirements

A school of 6–8 Canara Barbs requires an aquarium of at least **60 liters**. Thanks to the fish's small size, this
volume is sufficient, but as with all schooling species, more space is always better.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature**: 20–26°C
- **pH**: 6.5–7.5
- **Hardness (dGH)**: 5–15

For the aquascape, I recommend combining dense planting along the sides and back wall with open swimming space in the
center. Fine-leaved plants such as cabomba or hornwort provide natural shelter and help the fish feel secure. A dark
substrate of fine gravel or sand brings out the fish's coloration nicely. A few pieces of driftwood and smooth stones
complete the natural look.

Filtration should maintain water clarity without creating a strong current — Canara Barbs prefer calm water or moderate
flow. A lid is advisable, as the fish may jump when startled.

## Feeding and Diet

_Pethia canius_ is an omnivorous species that causes no feeding difficulties. In nature, their diet consists of small
invertebrates, insect larvae, and algal biofilm. In the aquarium, the staple diet should include:

- High-quality flakes or micro-granules for small tropical fish (daily staple)
- Frozen foods: brine shrimp, daphnia, cyclops, small bloodworms
- Live foods: baby brine shrimp, micro worms, daphnia — especially beneficial for pre-spawning conditioning
- Vegetable supplements: spirulina, blanched spinach, or zucchini

Feed twice daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. Given the Canara Barb's small mouth, choose
appropriately sized food particles. A varied and balanced diet supports fish health and promotes more vivid coloration.

## Care and Maintenance

The Canara Barb is an undemanding species with a low care difficulty level, suitable for beginner aquarists. Key care
recommendations:

- **Water changes**: 20–25% weekly
- **Filtration**: an internal or small external filter with moderate flow
- **Aeration**: recommended to maintain comfortable oxygen levels
- **Lighting**: moderate, with shaded areas from floating plants

Regularly monitor water parameters using test kits. Ammonia and nitrites should be at zero, and nitrate levels should
not exceed 20 mg/l. Vacuum the substrate during each water change to remove food remnants and organic waste. Under
stable conditions, _Pethia canius_ is practically disease-free. However, sudden parameter fluctuations or poor water
quality can lead to ich and bacterial infections.

## Compatibility

The Canara Barb is one of the most peaceful barbs, making it an excellent candidate for a community tank with gentle
fish of comparable size. Unlike more boisterous relatives, _Pethia canius_ very rarely shows interest in nipping
neighbors' fins.

Good tank mates:

- [Black Ruby Barb](/en/pethia-nigrofasciata) — a related species with similar care requirements
- [Cherry Barb](/en/puntius-titteya) — another peaceful and compact barb
- [Harlequin Rasbora](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha) — a calm schooling fish
- [Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi) and [Cardinal Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-axelrodi) — colorful, peaceful
  companions
- [Panda Corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda) and [Bronze Corydoras](/en/corydoras-aeneus) — peaceful bottom dwellers
- [Dwarf Gourami](/en/trichogaster-lalius) — a calm inhabitant of the upper layers

Avoid keeping them with large, aggressive species or active predators. Due to their small size, Canara Barbs can become
prey for larger fish. Schooling (6 or more individuals) helps the fish feel confident and exhibit natural behavior.

## Breeding

Breeding _Pethia canius_ at home is possible but requires some preparation. This is an egg-scattering species that
spreads eggs among plants and shows no parental care.

To set up for spawning:

- A separate breeding tank of 15–20 liters
- Soft water: pH 6.5–7.0, GH 5–8, temperature 24–26°C
- Fine-leaved plants (Java moss, cabomba) or a synthetic spawning mop
- A protective mesh on the bottom to prevent egg predation

For 1–2 weeks before spawning, feed the broodstock intensively with live foods — this stimulates gonad maturation. A
female ready to spawn becomes noticeably plumper, while the male intensifies in color and activity. Spawning typically
occurs in the morning. The female deposits several dozen small eggs that scatter among the plants.

Remove the adults immediately after spawning, as they are prone to eating their own eggs. Larvae hatch within 24–48
hours, and fry become free-swimming after 2–3 days. Initial food should be infusoria and liquid fry food; after 5–7
days, they can be transitioned to baby brine shrimp. Fry grow relatively slowly and develop adult coloration by 3–4
months.
