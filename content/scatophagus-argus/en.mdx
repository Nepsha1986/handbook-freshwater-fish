## Overview

_Scatophagus argus_, commonly known as the Spotted Scat or Argus Fish, is one of the most recognizable brackish water
species in the aquarium hobby. Belonging to the family Scatophagidae, this fish is found throughout the coastal waters
of the Indo-Pacific region — from East Africa to Australia and Polynesia. In the wild, Spotted Scats inhabit river
estuaries, mangrove swamps, and coastal lagoons where salinity levels constantly fluctuate.

The body of _Scatophagus argus_ is tall, laterally compressed, with a characteristic rounded shape. The base coloration
ranges from silvery to greenish-bronze, and the entire body is covered with large dark spots that give the fish its
common name. Juveniles are more brightly colored, often displaying a contrasting reddish tint on the upper body. Adults
reach 30–38 cm and can live up to 15 years with proper care.

It is worth noting that the dorsal fin of the Spotted Scat has venomous spines that can deliver a painful sting if
handled carelessly. While not life-threatening, caution should be exercised when working with this fish.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Scatophagus argus_ requires a spacious aquarium of at least 300 liters. These fish are active swimmers that
grow to an impressive size, so cramped conditions lead to stress and disease. They are best kept in groups of 3–4 or
more — solitary specimens tend to become skittish and reclusive.

Optimal water parameters: temperature 24–28°C, pH 7.5–8.5, hardness (GH) 10–20. The key aspect of keeping this species
is that they require brackish water. For juveniles, low salinity (around 1.005) is sufficient, but it should be
gradually increased to 1.010–1.015 as the fish matures. Some adults can even adapt to fully marine conditions.

Use rocks, driftwood, and large shells for decoration. Spotted Scats will typically eat live plants, so plastic
decorations or salt-tolerant species like Java moss are preferable. Substrate should be coarse sand or fine gravel.
Powerful filtration and good aeration are essential.

## Feeding and Diet

_Scatophagus argus_ is an omnivorous species with a strong preference for plant matter. In the wild, Spotted Scats feed
on algae, detritus, small invertebrates, and even waste — in fact, the genus name literally translates to "filth eater."

In the aquarium, offer a varied diet. The staple should be high-quality pellets or flakes for large fish. Be sure to
supplement with plant-based foods: blanched lettuce leaves, spinach, spirulina, and nori. For protein, offer brine
shrimp, bloodworms, krill, mussels, and shrimp — both frozen and live.

Feed 1–2 times daily, offering only as much food as the fish can consume in 3–5 minutes. Spotted Scats are prone to
overeating, so portion control is important. A weekly fasting day can be beneficial.

## Care and Maintenance

Despite their general hardiness, _Scatophagus argus_ requires stable water conditions. Weekly water changes of 25–30%
are essential — Spotted Scats produce significant waste due to their size and appetite. Use a powerful external filter
rated for 2–3 times the actual tank volume.

Monitor salinity levels regularly using a refractometer or hydrometer. Sudden salinity fluctuations are one of the main
causes of stress and illness in these fish. When performing water changes, always prepare replacement water with the
same salinity as the tank water.

Watch the condition of the fins — when water quality deteriorates, Spotted Scats are among the first to develop
bacterial infections. At the first signs of fin cloudiness or body spots, check water parameters and increase the
frequency of water changes if necessary.

## Compatibility

_Scatophagus argus_ is a relatively peaceful species, especially when kept in a group. However, due to their large size,
they are not suitable for community tanks with small fish. Ideal tank mates are other large brackish water inhabitants:
[Mono Argentus](/en/monodactylus-argenteus), archerfish, large gobies, and certain cichlid species that tolerate
brackish water.

Avoid keeping Spotted Scats with small, slow-moving fish — they may nip at their neighbors' fins. Also avoid aggressive
territorial species that would intimidate the relatively mild-mannered Scats.

Keeping a group of 4–6 individuals helps distribute intraspecific aggression and makes the fish more confident. When
kept alone or in pairs, Spotted Scats often become nervous and hide.

## Breeding

Breeding _Scatophagus argus_ in aquarium conditions is extremely challenging, with virtually no documented successful
cases. In the wild, these fish migrate from brackish waters to the open sea to spawn. Eggs and larvae develop in marine
water, and the juveniles gradually return to estuaries and mangrove habitats.

Sexual dimorphism is minimal, which further complicates pair selection. Females are presumably slightly larger than
males, but visual differentiation is nearly impossible.

A breeding attempt would require a very large aquarium with the capability to gradually transition the fish to fully
marine water and back, along with conditions that simulate seasonal migrations. Currently, virtually all Spotted Scats
available in the trade are wild-caught specimens or raised on specialized farms in Southeast Asia.
