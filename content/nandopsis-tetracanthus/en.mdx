## Overview

_Nandopsis tetracanthus_, known as the Cuban Cichlid, is the only cichlid species endemic to the island of Cuba. This
unique status makes it one of the most fascinating and prized members of the Cichlidae family in the aquarium hobby. In
the wild, it inhabits rivers, lakes, and canals across Cuba, preferring areas with rocky substrates and abundant hiding
spots.

The Cuban Cichlid is a large, powerful fish that reaches 20–30 cm in length. Its body is robust, slightly laterally
compressed, featuring a distinctive pattern of dark spots and speckles on a lighter background that gives it a truly
unique appearance. Coloration can vary depending on mood, age, and keeping conditions. With proper care, _Nandopsis
tetracanthus_ lives 10–15 years, making it a long-term aquarium resident.

The Cuban Cichlid has a distinctly territorial and aggressive temperament (especially during spawning), making it best
suited for experienced aquarists who can provide appropriate keeping conditions.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Nandopsis tetracanthus_ requires an aquarium of at least 200 liters. Given the large size and territorial
nature of this fish, bigger is always better. For a pair, a volume of 300 liters or more is recommended.

Optimal water parameters are: temperature 24–28 °C, general hardness (GH) 10–20 dGH, and pH 6.5–8.0. The Cuban Cichlid
is quite hardy and adapts to a wide range of parameters, but stability of conditions is critically important.

Sand or fine gravel works best as substrate. Large rocks, driftwood, and caves are essential to create hiding spots and
territorial boundaries. The Cuban Cichlid typically uproots live plants, so hardy species such as Anubias or artificial
plants are preferable. Powerful filtration and good aeration are essential.

## Feeding and Diet

_Nandopsis tetracanthus_ is an omnivorous species with predatory tendencies. In the wild, the Cuban Cichlid feeds on
small fish, crustaceans, insects, and plant matter. In the aquarium, the diet should be varied and well-balanced.

High-quality pellets designed for large cichlids can form the dietary staple. The diet must be supplemented with live or
frozen foods: bloodworms, brine shrimp, shrimp, and pieces of fish fillet. Vegetable matter is also important — blanched
spinach leaves, peas, and spirulina are all suitable.

Feed adult fish 1–2 times per day, offering only as much food as they can consume within 3–5 minutes. A varied diet
promotes vibrant coloration and good health.

## Care and Maintenance

The Cuban Cichlid requires an intermediate level of experience from the aquarist. Weekly water changes of 25–30% are
necessary to maintain water quality. Regularly monitor ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate levels — _Nandopsis tetracanthus_
is sensitive to high concentrations of nitrogenous compounds.

A powerful external filter is essential, as this large fish produces a significant amount of waste. Keep an eye on
decorations: Cuban Cichlids actively rearrange their aquarium, moving rocks and substrate. Make sure any rock structures
are stable and cannot collapse.

It is important to maintain a consistent lighting schedule and avoid sudden changes in water parameters. With proper
care, the Cuban Cichlid rarely falls ill, though under stress or poor conditions it may be susceptible to common cichlid
diseases such as ich and hole-in-the-head disease.

## Compatibility

_Nandopsis tetracanthus_ is a highly aggressive species, and selecting tankmates requires careful consideration. During
spawning, aggression increases dramatically, and the Cuban Cichlid can pose a threat even to large fish.

Suitable tankmates include other large and robust Central American cichlids, such as the
[Firemouth Cichlid](/en/thorichthys-meeki), [Salvini Cichlid](/en/trichromis-salvini), or
[Jaguar Cichlid](/en/parachromis-managuensis). Keeping with large catfish such as the
[Striped Raphael Catfish](/en/platydoras-costatus) is also possible. It is strongly discouraged to keep them with small
or peaceful species — they will be treated as prey.

The ideal setup is keeping a bonded pair in a species-only aquarium. This allows observation of natural behavior and
minimizes the risk of conflicts with other fish.

## Breeding

Breeding _Nandopsis tetracanthus_ in the aquarium is entirely achievable with an established pair. Cuban Cichlids form
strong monogamous pair bonds that can last for many years. The connection between partners is very strong, and the pair
jointly defends their territory and offspring.

To stimulate spawning, raise the water temperature by 1–2 °C and increase the frequency of water changes. The female
lays eggs on a flat rock or other smooth surface that has been meticulously cleaned by both parents beforehand. A clutch
can contain 200 to 500 eggs.

Both parents actively guard the eggs and fry, displaying extreme aggression toward all other aquarium inhabitants during
this period. It is strongly recommended to remove all tankmates at this time, or better yet, breed the pair in a
separate aquarium of at least 200 liters. Fry begin free-swimming 5–7 days after hatching and can be fed newly hatched
brine shrimp and finely ground food.
