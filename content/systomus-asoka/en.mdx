## Overview

_Systomus asoka_ is a striking member of the Cyprinidae family, endemic to Sri Lanka. In the wild, this fish inhabits
fast-flowing mountain rivers and streams with rocky substrates and clear water. The Asoka Barb grows to 10–15 cm and
displays an attractive coloration with golden and reddish hues that intensifies significantly when kept in a group.

This is a schooling species, and in a group of 8–10 individuals, the fish exhibit their most natural behavior: active
swimming in the middle water column, hierarchical interactions, and vivid coloration. Unlike some other barbs, such as
the [Tiger Barb](/en/puntius-tetrazona), _Systomus asoka_ is far less prone to fin-nipping behavior. With proper care,
they can live for 5–8 years in an aquarium.

## Tank Requirements

A school of Asoka Barbs requires an aquarium of at least 200 liters. The key consideration is water flow — in the wild,
these fish live in streams with active currents, so using a powerhead or directing the output of an external filter will
greatly improve their comfort.

Optimal water parameters: temperature 22–26°C, pH 6.0–7.5, general hardness (GH) 5–15. The water should be
well-oxygenated — this is easily achieved with an air stone or by creating surface agitation.

Fine gravel or coarse sand works best as a substrate, mimicking the riverbed. Arrange smooth rocks and driftwood to
create visual barriers and territory divisions. Choose hardy plants that can tolerate current — Anubias, Bucephalandra,
and ferns attached to rocks work best.

## Feeding and Diet

_Systomus asoka_ is an omnivore with a preference for protein-rich foods. A base diet of high-quality flakes or pellets
formulated for barbs is suitable, but regular supplementation with live and frozen foods is essential for maintaining
health and vibrant coloration: bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, and cyclops.

Vegetable supplementation is also important — offer blanched spinach leaves, cucumber slices, or spirulina-based foods
once a week. Feed 2–3 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. This approach prevents
overfeeding and helps maintain water quality.

## Care and Maintenance

Weekly water changes of 25–30% are essential for successful keeping. _Systomus asoka_ is sensitive to nitrate
accumulation, so it is important to keep nitrate levels below 20 mg/l. An external canister filter with a turnover rate
of at least 5–6 times the tank volume per hour will provide the necessary biological and mechanical filtration.

Test water parameters regularly using liquid test kits. Pay attention to fish behavior — loss of color, lethargy, or
clamped fins may indicate stress or the onset of disease. Under good conditions, Asoka Barbs remain active throughout
the day and eagerly approach the front glass at feeding time.

## Compatibility

The Asoka Barb is a moderately peaceful species well-suited for a community aquarium with fish of similar size and
temperament. Keeping them in a group of at least 6 significantly reduces intraspecific aggression and stress.

Good tank mates include [Denison Barbs](/en/sahyadria-denisonii), [Cherry Barbs](/en/puntius-titteya),
[Clown Loaches](/en/chromobotia-macracanthus), [Zebra Loaches](/en/botia-striata), as well as larger danios and
rasboras. For bottom dwellers, [Kuhli Loaches](/en/pangio-kuhlii) make excellent companions.

Avoid keeping them with slow-moving, long-finned fish or very small species — the barbs' active swimming may cause
stress. Large aggressive cichlids are also unsuitable tank mates.

## Breeding

Breeding _Systomus asoka_ at home is quite achievable with proper preparation. This is an egg-scattering species that
shows no parental care.

Prepare a separate breeding tank of 50–80 liters with soft water (GH 4–8, pH 6.0–6.5) and a temperature 1–2°C higher
than usual. Place fine-leaved plants or a mesh grid on the bottom so that eggs fall through and remain inaccessible to
the parents. Keep lighting subdued.

Condition the breeding pair with live foods for two weeks before spawning. Females can be identified by their rounder
bellies. Spawning typically occurs at dawn — the pair scatters eggs among the plants. Remove the adults immediately
after spawning.

Larvae hatch within 24–36 hours and become free-swimming by day 3–4. First foods should be infusoria and brine shrimp
nauplii. As the fry grow, transition them to microworms and finely crushed dry foods.
