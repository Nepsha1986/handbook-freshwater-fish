## Overview

_Parachromis managuensis_ is one of the largest and most impressive cichlids from Central America, belonging to the
family Cichlidae. In the wild, this species inhabits Lakes Managua and Nicaragua, as well as rivers and reservoirs in
Honduras, Costa Rica, and southern Mexico. The species is named after Lake Managua — the capital lake of Nicaragua. The
same waters are home to the closely related [Nicaragua Cichlid](/en/cichlasoma-nicaraguense), with which the Jaguar
Cichlid shares its natural habitat.

This is a true giant among aquarium cichlids: in captivity, _Parachromis managuensis_ reaches 25–35 cm, while wild
specimens can grow even larger. The body is powerful and elongated, with a large head and a wide mouth — a classic
predator profile. The coloration deserves special attention: juveniles display a plain silvery-grey body with dark
vertical bands, but as they mature, the pattern transforms dramatically. Adults develop the signature jaguar pattern — a
scattering of large, irregularly shaped black spots on a golden-yellow or silvery background. It is this resemblance to
the jaguar's coat that earned the species its popular name. Males are typically larger than females and possess more
intense coloration.

With proper care, the Jaguar Cichlid lives 10–15 years. This is a fish for experienced aquarists — it combines large
size, pronounced territoriality, and predatory instincts, demanding a responsible approach to husbandry.

## Tank Requirements

Given the impressive size of _Parachromis managuensis_, keeping this species requires a tank of at least 450 liters —
one of the highest minimum volumes among popular aquarium cichlids. For a pair or community with other large fish, tanks
of 600 liters or more are preferable. The aquarium should be long and wide to provide ample swimming space.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature:** 24–30 °C
- **pH:** 7.0–8.7
- **Hardness (GH):** 10–20 dGH

Notably, the Jaguar Cichlid tolerates alkaline water well — its pH range of up to 8.7 is one of the broadest among
popular cichlids. However, parameter stability matters more than hitting an ideal range.

Coarse sand or fine gravel works best as substrate. Decor should consist of large rocks, driftwood, and caves that
provide hiding spots and visual territory boundaries. Rocks must be stable and securely positioned, as adult Jaguar
Cichlids are strong enough to displace and overturn decorations. Live plants are incompatible with this species — the
fish uproots and destroys them. The tank lid must be securely fastened: the large and powerful _Parachromis managuensis_
is capable of jumping out of the water.

Robust filtration is essential. An external canister filter rated for the tank volume is necessary to maintain water
quality given the substantial waste produced by this large fish.

## Feeding and Diet

_Parachromis managuensis_ is a pronounced predator. In the wild, its diet consists primarily of small fish, crustaceans,
and large aquatic insects. In the aquarium, this appetite is easily satisfied with a balanced diet of quality foods.

The dietary foundation should be specialized pellets or sticks for large predatory cichlids. Regular supplementation is
recommended:

- **Frozen foods:** large bloodworms, shrimp, mussels, squid pieces
- **Fish fillets:** lean marine fish (pollock, cod) — in small pieces
- **Live foods:** large worms, crickets, feeder insects

Feeding live fish should be avoided. This not only carries the risk of introducing diseases and parasites but also
reinforces aggressive behavior. Frozen foods fully meet the protein requirements.

Adults should be fed once daily, while juveniles benefit from twice-daily feedings. One fasting day per week supports
healthy digestion. Overfeeding should be strictly avoided — large cichlids are prone to obesity, which shortens
lifespan.

## Care and Maintenance

The Jaguar Cichlid demands significant experience from the aquarist (care level 4 out of 5). The main challenge lies in
managing a large, aggressive, and high-waste-producing fish.

Weekly water changes of 25–30% are mandatory. Under heavy biological load, changes should be performed more frequently
or increased in volume. Regularly test water for ammonia, nitrites, and nitrates — _Parachromis managuensis_, despite
its hardiness, suffers from elevated nitrogenous waste concentrations.

Gravel vacuuming should be part of weekly maintenance. Monitor filter media condition and rinse them in tank water, not
under the tap. Check the lid attachment and decoration stability — Jaguar Cichlids actively rearrange their tank space.

The species is most susceptible to hole-in-the-head disease (hexamitiasis) from poor water quality or monotonous diet,
and ich from temperature drops. The best prevention is stable water parameters, regular water changes, and a varied
diet.

## Compatibility

_Parachromis managuensis_ is a highly aggressive species with pronounced territoriality. Any fish small enough to be
swallowed will be treated as food. Selecting tank mates for this predator is a serious task requiring a large tank
volume and careful planning.

Suitable tank mates are limited to large, robust species capable of defending themselves:

- [Oscar](/en/astronotus-ocellatus) — a comparably sized large cichlid and one of the classic companions for large
  predators
- [Cuban Cichlid](/en/nandopsis-tetracanthus) — a species of similar temperament from the Caribbean region
- [Salvini Cichlid](/en/trichromis-salvini) — a hardy Central American cichlid, given sufficient space
- [Texas Cichlid](/en/herichthys-carpintis) — a tough and territorial fish that pairs well in spacious tanks
- [Red Terror Cichlid](/en/cichlasoma-festae) — a large and aggressive species with similar requirements

Keeping with small or medium-sized fish, as well as peaceful species, is strictly not recommended — they will be
promptly eaten or terrorized. Large catfish (such as the [Striped Raphael](/en/platydoras-costatus)) may work as bottom-
dwelling companions, but only with ample hiding spots available.

The ideal setup is a bonded pair in a species-only tank. This is the safest and most rewarding arrangement, allowing you
to observe the natural behavior of these impressive fish.

## Breeding

Breeding _Parachromis managuensis_ in the aquarium is achievable but requires experience and preparation (difficulty 4
out of 5). The main challenge is the extreme aggression of the pair during spawning and fry rearing.

Sexual dimorphism is apparent: males are noticeably larger, with a more massive head and brighter coloration. Females
develop a visibly rounded belly when ready to spawn. Pair formation is best achieved by raising a group of juveniles
together — forcibly pairing two adults often results in serious injury.

To stimulate spawning, raise the temperature to 28–30 °C and perform a large water change. The pair selects a flat rock
or other smooth surface and meticulously cleans it. The female can lay up to 5,000 eggs — making this one of the most
prolific cichlid species. Clutch size depends on the female's size and age.

Both parents zealously guard the clutch and fry, displaying extreme aggression toward all tank inhabitants during this
period. Spawning in a separate tank of at least 300 liters is strongly recommended. Incubation lasts 3–5 days depending
on temperature. After hatching, larvae remain on the substrate for another 5–7 days, feeding on their yolk sacs.
Free-swimming fry can be fed brine shrimp nauplii and finely crushed food. Fry grow rapidly but unevenly — larger
individuals may prey on smaller siblings, so size-based sorting is recommended.
