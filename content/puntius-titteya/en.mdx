## Overview

The Cherry Barb (_Puntius titteya_) is one of the most popular and recognizable members of the Cyprinidae family in the
global aquarium hobby. This small but remarkably attractive species was first described in 1929 and has since firmly
established itself as a favorite among aquarists of all levels — from beginners to seasoned enthusiasts.

In the wild, _Puntius titteya_ inhabits the southwestern regions of Sri Lanka, dwelling in shaded streams and small
rivers of the Kelani and Nilwala basins that flow through tropical rainforests. These waterways are characterized by
soft, slightly acidic water tinted with tannins from fallen leaves, moderate current, and dense riparian vegetation.
Unfortunately, due to deforestation and urbanization, the species' natural habitats are shrinking, and _Puntius titteya_
is listed as vulnerable on the IUCN Red List. However, virtually all fish available in the trade are captive-bred.

Adults reach a size of 5-6 cm. The species' most distinctive feature is pronounced sexual dimorphism in coloration.
Males display a rich cherry-red color that becomes especially vivid during spawning and under comfortable keeping
conditions. Females are more subdued — their bodies have a sandy-yellowish hue with a characteristic dark longitudinal
stripe running from the snout to the base of the tail. This same stripe is present in males as well, but often becomes
obscured by their intense red coloration.

The Cherry Barb is a schooling fish with a peaceful temperament. In an aquarium, a group of 8-10 individuals feels most
comfortable, with males displaying vibrant coloration as they try to impress females and compete with one another
without any real aggression.

## Tank Requirements

A tank volume of at least 80 liters is suitable for keeping a school of Cherry Barbs. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 22-26°C, pH 6.0-7.5, and general hardness GH 5-15. The species is quite undemanding regarding water
chemistry, making it an excellent choice for beginner aquarists.

The aquarium should be densely planted with live vegetation — cryptocorynes, Java moss, vallisneria, and other species
that create shaded areas. Floating plants will help dim the light and recreate the atmosphere of Sri Lanka's shady
forest streams. A dark substrate works best, as it makes the cherry-red coloration of males look especially striking.
Driftwood and dried Indian almond or oak leaves will not only decorate the tank but also enrich the water with
beneficial tannins.

Filtration should be moderate, without creating strong current. Cherry Barbs prefer calm water with gentle flow.
Lighting should be subdued or diffused, which most accurately replicates their natural habitat conditions.

## Feeding and Diet

_Puntius titteya_ is an omnivorous species with undemanding tastes. In the wild, its diet consists of small
invertebrates, insect larvae, algae, and plant detritus. In the aquarium, high-quality flakes or micro-pellets for small
tropical fish can serve as the staple diet.

To maintain vibrant coloration and overall health, the diet should be supplemented with live and frozen foods: daphnia,
cyclops, brine shrimp nauplii, and small bloodworms. A vegetable component is also important — periodically offer
spirulina-based foods or blanched pieces of zucchini and spinach.

Feed 2 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2-3 minutes. Overfeeding leads to obesity and
deteriorating water quality, which is particularly undesirable for this species.

## Care and Maintenance

The Cherry Barb is one of the most undemanding aquarium fish, with a lifespan of 3-5 years under proper care. The key to
successful keeping is water parameter stability and regular tank maintenance.

Weekly water changes of 20-25% of the total volume will help maintain cleanliness and consistent water chemistry. It is
important to avoid sudden temperature fluctuations during water changes — new water should be dechlorinated and brought
to the aquarium temperature beforehand. Regularly test ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate levels using test kits.

Cherry Barbs have a robust immune system, but under stressful conditions — overcrowding, poor water quality, lack of
hiding spots — they may be susceptible to ich (white spot disease) and bacterial infections. Prevention lies in
maintaining stable keeping conditions and quarantining new fish before introducing them to the tank.

Note that pale coloration in males most often indicates stress or uncomfortable conditions rather than disease. It is
worth checking water parameters and ensuring adequate hiding spots in the aquarium.

## Compatibility

The Cherry Barb possesses an exceptionally peaceful temperament and is an ideal inhabitant for community aquariums.
Unlike some of its more active relatives, such as the [Tiger Barb](/en/puntius-tetrazona), the Cherry Barb is not prone
to fin-nipping and does not disturb slow-moving tankmates.

_Puntius titteya_ should be kept in a school of at least 8 individuals with a female majority (a ratio of 1 male to 2-3
females). In a larger group, males display their most vibrant coloration as they compete for female attention.

Ideal tankmates include other peaceful fish of comparable size: [Neon Tetras](/en/paracheirodon-innesi),
[Harlequin Rasboras](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha), [Flame Tetras](/en/hyphessobrycon-flammeus),
[Pygmy Corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus), and [Honey Gouramis](/en/trichogaster-chuna). Cherry Barbs also coexist well
with peaceful shrimp, although small juvenile shrimp may be perceived as food.

Keeping them with large or aggressive species — cichlids, large barbs — is not recommended, as these can intimidate
these modest fish.

## Breeding

Breeding Cherry Barbs at home is not particularly difficult and is well within the abilities of even novice aquarists.
For spawning, prepare a separate tank of 20-30 liters with soft water (GH 4-8), pH 6.0-6.5, and a temperature of
25-26°C.

Place fine-leaved plants on the bottom of the breeding tank — Java moss or cabomba — or use a spawning grid to protect
the eggs. Lighting should be subdued. Before spawning, separate the breeders for 1-2 weeks and feed them generously with
live foods.

Introduce the prepared pair into the breeding tank in the evening. Spawning typically occurs the following morning: the
male actively courts the female, displaying his most vivid coloration. The female lays 200-300 eggs, scattering them
among the plants. After spawning, the adults must be removed immediately, as they are prone to eating their own eggs.

Incubation lasts 24-48 hours. The larvae feed on their yolk sacs for the first few days before becoming free-swimming.
Initial food consists of infusoria and brine shrimp nauplii. The fry grow relatively quickly and develop their
characteristic coloration by 2-3 months of age.
