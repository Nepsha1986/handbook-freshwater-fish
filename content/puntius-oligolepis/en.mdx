## Overview

_Puntius oligolepis_, commonly known as the Checkerboard Barb or Island Barb, is a small freshwater fish belonging to
the family Cyprinidae, endemic to the island of Sumatra, Indonesia. In the wild, this species inhabits calm streams and
river tributaries with dense riparian vegetation.

The Checkerboard Barb owes its name to a distinctive pattern: each scale has a dark edge, creating a clearly visible
checkerboard (grid-like) pattern across the body. Adults reach a size of 4-5 cm, making them one of the most compact
barb species. Males are noticeably more colorful than females — their fins develop an orange-red hue with striking black
margins, and during spawning, the coloration becomes especially intense. Females are more subdued, displaying yellowish
tones.

Although _Puntius oligolepis_ is less popular than the [Tiger Barb](/en/puntius-tetrazona) or
[Cherry Barb](/en/puntius-titteya), it is one of the best fish for beginner aquarists — hardy, undemanding, and
completely peaceful. With proper care, they can live 3-5 years in an aquarium.

## Tank Requirements

Thanks to their modest size, _Puntius oligolepis_ is an excellent choice for small and nano aquariums. The minimum
volume for a school of 6-8 individuals is 40 liters (approximately 10 gallons). The tank should be densely planted with
live vegetation while leaving open swimming space in the middle and upper zones.

Optimal water parameters:

- Temperature: 22-26 °C (72-79 °F)
- pH: 6.0-7.5
- General Hardness (GH): 5-15

Fine sand or dark-toned gravel works best as a substrate — the checkerboard pattern stands out much more impressively
against a dark background. The addition of driftwood and a few rock shelters will create conditions closer to their
natural habitat. Water flow should be moderate — too strong a current will exhaust these small fish. A lid or cover
glass is essential, as barbs are prone to jumping.

## Feeding and Diet

_Puntius oligolepis_ is an omnivorous species that presents no feeding challenges whatsoever. The dietary staple can
consist of high-quality flakes or micro-pellets designed for small tropical fish. To maintain vibrant coloration and
overall health, regularly supplement their menu with live or frozen foods: daphnia, brine shrimp, bloodworms, and
cyclops.

Vegetable supplementation is also important — blanched spinach leaves, zucchini pieces, or spirulina-based foods will
provide essential micronutrients. Feed 2-3 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2-3 minutes.
Overfeeding is one of the most common mistakes among beginner aquarists, leading to deteriorating water quality.

## Care and Maintenance

The Checkerboard Barb is one of the least demanding aquarium fish, making it a superb choice for newcomers to the hobby.
Weekly water changes of 20-25% and maintaining stable parameters form the foundation of successful care.

The aquarium should be equipped with a reliable filter providing both mechanical and biological filtration. Regularly
test ammonia and nitrite levels — these must remain at zero. Nitrates should ideally be kept below 20 mg/l. Lighting
should be moderate and diffused — bright light can cause stress, while subdued lighting will highlight the natural
beauty of their coloration.

Pay attention to the condition of your fish: loss of color intensity, clamped fins, or refusal to eat may indicate
stress or the onset of disease. When conditions are kept stable, Checkerboard Barbs are remarkably resistant to illness.

## Compatibility

_Puntius oligolepis_ is one of the most peaceful barb species. Unlike its more famous relative, the
[Tiger Barb](/en/puntius-tetrazona), it never nips the fins of tank mates and shows no aggression.

Checkerboard Barbs should be kept in groups of at least 6 individuals — in a school, they feel more confident, display
natural behavior, and show their best coloration. Within the group, an interesting hierarchy forms, and males actively
compete with one another, displaying their vivid fins.

Ideal tank mates include:

- Small barbs: [Cherry Barb](/en/puntius-titteya), [Snakeskin Barb](/en/desmopuntius-rhomboocellatus)
- Rasboras: [Harlequin Rasbora](/en/trigonostigma-heteromorpha), [Hengel's Rasbora](/en/trigonostigma-hengeli)
- Small danios: [Zebra Danio](/en/danio-rerio), [Celestial Pearl Danio](/en/danio-margaritatus)
- Catfish: [Panda Corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda), [Pygmy Corydoras](/en/corydoras-pygmaeus)
- Small tetras: [Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi), [White Cloud Mountain Minnow](/en/tanichthys-albonubes)

Avoid housing them with large or aggressive species, as well as fish with long, flowing fins.

## Breeding

Breeding _Puntius oligolepis_ at home is quite achievable, though it requires some preparation. This species is an egg
scatterer and does not exhibit any parental care.

For spawning, prepare a separate tank of 15-20 liters with fine-leaved plants (Java moss, Cabomba) or a synthetic
spawning substrate. Water level should be about 15 cm, temperature 24-26 °C, and pH around 6.5. It is advisable to place
a mesh on the bottom through which eggs can fall but parents cannot reach.

Separate the male and female 1-2 weeks before spawning and feed them generously with live foods. A female ready to spawn
will become noticeably plumper, while the male will develop especially intense coloration. Place the fish in the
breeding tank in the evening — spawning typically occurs at dawn. After egg laying, the parents must be removed
immediately, as they will consume the eggs.

Eggs incubate for 24-48 hours. Fry become free-swimming 3-4 days after hatching. Initial food should be infusoria or
liquid fry food, followed by baby brine shrimp. With good feeding, the fry grow relatively quickly and begin developing
their characteristic checkerboard pattern by 3-4 months of age.
