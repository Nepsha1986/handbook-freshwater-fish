## Overview

_Sahyadria denisonii_ is a stunning member of the Cyprinidae family, native to the fast-flowing mountain streams and
rivers of the Western Ghats in southwestern India. This fish immediately catches the eye with a bright red stripe
running from the tip of the snout through the eye to the middle of the body, contrasted by a bold black line along the
flank. The torpedo-shaped body with a silvery sheen and yellow-black caudal fin makes the Denison barb one of the most
beautiful freshwater species available.

In the wild, _S. denisonii_ inhabits clean, well-oxygenated waters with rocky substrates and moderate current. The
species is listed as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List, making captive breeding particularly important. Adults reach 9–12
cm in length and live 5–8 years with proper care. These are diurnal, active schooling fish — keeping them in groups of
at least 6 is essential for their well-being.

## Tank Requirements

A group of 6–8 Denison barbs requires an aquarium of at least 150 liters. An elongated tank is preferable, as these fish
need long swimming lanes for their active lifestyle. Optimal water parameters: temperature 22–28°C, pH 6.5–7.8, hardness
5–15 dGH.

The aquarium should be equipped with a powerful external filter that creates moderate current — this replicates their
natural habitat. Fine gravel or sand substrate works best, supplemented with several large rocks and driftwood for
shelter. Live plants such as Anubias and Cryptocoryne, planted along the sides and back wall, will leave the central
area clear for swimming. A tight-fitting lid is essential — Denison barbs are prone to jumping, especially during
acclimation or when stressed.

## Feeding and Diet

_Sahyadria denisonii_ is an omnivorous species, which makes feeding straightforward. High-quality granules or flakes for
tropical fish can serve as the dietary staple. To maintain health and vibrant coloration, regularly offer live or frozen
foods: bloodworms, brine shrimp, and daphnia.

A vegetable component is also important — blanched spinach, zucchini, or spirulina flakes will round out the diet. Feed
2–3 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. Dietary variety is the key to vivid
coloration and active behavior in these barbs.

## Care and Maintenance

Despite being relatively undemanding, Denison barbs require stable water parameters. Weekly water changes of 20–25% are
mandatory. It is important to monitor ammonia and nitrite levels — these should remain at zero, while nitrates should
not exceed 20 mg/l.

Regular filter maintenance and substrate vacuuming will help maintain water quality. Watch the temperature closely —
sudden fluctuations are harmful to this species. Using a thermometer and test kits for weekly parameter checks is
recommended. With proper care, these fish remain active, display schooling behavior, and reward the keeper with
brilliant coloration.

## Compatibility

The Denison barb is a peaceful schooling fish well suited for a community aquarium with similarly sized tankmates. Good
companions include [zebra danio](/en/danio-rerio), [Sterba's corydoras](/en/corydoras-sterbai),
[Siamese algae eater](/en/crossocheilus-siamensis), as well as larger rasboras and medium-sized peaceful cichlids. Among
bottom dwellers, the [clown loach](/en/chromobotia-macracanthus) makes a suitable tankmate.

Avoid housing them with small fish that may be intimidated by the school's active behavior, as well as aggressive
species that could cause stress. Keeping fewer than 6 individuals leads to increased shyness and faded coloration.

## Breeding

Breeding _Sahyadria denisonii_ in captivity is a challenging task, reflecting its difficulty rating of 4 out of 5. In
the wild, spawning occurs during the monsoon season in fast-flowing streams. To stimulate breeding in an aquarium, a
separate spawning tank of at least 100 liters with soft, slightly acidic water (pH 6.5–7.0) and a temperature of around
26°C is required.

Breeders should be separated for 1–2 weeks beforehand and conditioned with generous feedings of live foods. Place
fine-leaved plants or synthetic spawning mops in the tank for egg deposition. Parents must be removed immediately after
spawning, as they tend to eat the eggs. Larvae hatch within 24–48 hours, and after 2–3 days the fry become free-swimming
and begin feeding. First foods should be infusoria and newly hatched brine shrimp. Fry grow slowly and require clean
water with frequent, small feedings.
