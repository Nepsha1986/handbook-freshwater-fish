## Overview

The Freshwater Blenny (_Salaria fluviatilis_) is the only fully freshwater representative of the family Blenniidae in
Europe. In the wild, it inhabits clean rivers and streams of the Mediterranean basin — from the Iberian Peninsula to
Turkey and North Africa, favoring rocky stretches with moderate to fast current.

The body is elongated, slightly laterally compressed, scaleless, and covered in a mucous coating. Males develop a
characteristic head crest that becomes more pronounced during the spawning season. Coloration ranges from olive-green to
brown with dark marbled patches and bands. Adults reach 15–20 cm in length and live 5–8 years with proper care.

The Freshwater Blenny is a benthic species, spending most of its time among rocks and shelters. It moves in
characteristic short hops along the bottom, propping itself on its pectoral fins. This is a diurnal fish with pronounced
territorial behavior, making it a particularly fascinating species to observe.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping a Freshwater Blenny requires an aquarium of at least 100 liters. Water parameters: temperature 10–20°C, pH
7.5–8.5, hardness GH 9–12. This fish is accustomed to cool water and tolerates prolonged heating above 22°C poorly, so
cooling may be necessary during summer months.

The aquarium setup should mimic a rocky riverbed. The core decoration consists of large and medium-sized rocks forming
numerous small caves and crevices — this is critically important for this species. The substrate should be sand or fine
gravel. Driftwood also works well as additional shelter.

Good aeration and moderate current are essential, achieved with a powerhead or directed filter outflow. Lighting should
be moderate. Live plants are not required, but hardy species such as Anubias and mosses help create a more natural
environment and extra hiding spots.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Salaria fluviatilis_ feeds on small invertebrates, insect larvae, algae growth, and detritus. In the
aquarium, the diet should be varied and include live or frozen foods: bloodworms, tubifex, brine shrimp, and daphnia.
These foods form the dietary staple.

High-quality sinking pellets for bottom-dwellers and spirulina-based foods work well as supplements. The Freshwater
Blenny rarely accepts flakes, as it feeds predominantly at the bottom. Offering blanched zucchini or cucumber pieces
occasionally is also beneficial.

Feed 1–2 times daily in small portions. It is important to ensure food reaches the bottom and remains accessible to the
fish, especially when keeping them with more active tankmates that may intercept food in the water column.

## Care and Maintenance

The Freshwater Blenny requires stable conditions and clean, well-oxygenated water. Weekly water changes of 25–30% are
essential. Filtration must be efficient — an external canister filter with enough power to create moderate current is
recommended.

Test water parameters regularly, paying particular attention to ammonia and nitrite levels — this fish is sensitive to
pollution. The substrate should be vacuumed during each water change, with special attention to spaces between rocks
where food debris accumulates.

Monitor the condition of the fish's mucous coating, which serves as protection against infections. If you notice
dullness or whitish patches on the body, check water quality immediately. During summer, control the temperature and use
an aquarium chiller or fan if necessary.

## Compatibility

_Salaria fluviatilis_ has a moderately territorial temperament, especially toward conspecifics and other bottom-dwelling
species. When keeping multiple individuals, the tank must be spacious enough with plenty of shelters — a minimum of two
to three hiding spots per fish.

Good tankmates are peaceful fish that prefer the middle and upper water layers. Small cyprinids and characins that do
not require tropical temperatures are suitable choices. Avoid keeping them with aggressive cichlids and large predators.

Housing with other territorial bottom-dwellers such as gobies or other blennies is not recommended, as this will lead to
constant conflict. Small shrimp are also poor companions, as they may become prey.

## Breeding

Breeding _Salaria fluviatilis_ in the aquarium is a challenging task, rated 4 out of 5 in difficulty. In the wild,
spawning occurs in spring as water temperature rises to 18–20°C.

To stimulate spawning, it is recommended to mimic seasonal changes: keep the fish at a reduced temperature of 12–14°C
for several weeks, then gradually raise it to 18–20°C while increasing the photoperiod. The tank should contain flat
rocks or ceramic tubes, which the male selects as a spawning substrate.

The male prepares and guards the nest, attracting the female to it. After egg deposition, the male assumes all parental
care — aerating the eggs and defending them from other fish. Incubation lasts approximately 2–3 weeks depending on
temperature. Once hatched, fry should be fed newly hatched brine shrimp and microworms, gradually transitioning to
larger foods as they grow.
