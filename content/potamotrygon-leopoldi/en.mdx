## Overview

_Potamotrygon leopoldi_ is one of the most spectacular freshwater stingrays in the world, rightfully earning the
nickname "Black Diamond Stingray." This species belongs to the family Potamotrygonidae and is native to the Xingu River
basin in Brazil. Its jet-black disc adorned with contrasting white or cream spots makes each individual unique — no two
rays share the same pattern.

The disc diameter of an adult ray reaches 30–50 cm, but the total body length including the tail is considerably greater
— up to 70–80 cm. With proper care, their lifespan can reach 10–15 years. Despite their relatively peaceful temperament,
_P. leopoldi_ is a demanding and expensive fish that requires serious experience, a large aquarium, and significant
financial investment to maintain.

**Important:** the ray has a venomous spine on its tail. A sting is extremely painful and can pose a serious health
risk. All maintenance work in the aquarium must be performed with extreme caution — never corner the ray.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Potamotrygon leopoldi_ requires an aquarium of at least 500 liters, with tanks of 800 liters or more being
preferable. The aquarium should have a large bottom footprint — floor space matters more than height for stingrays. The
optimal shape is a wide, shallow tank.

Water parameters:

- Temperature: 24–28°C
- pH: 6.5–7.5
- Hardness (GH): 3–10

The substrate must be fine, soft sand exclusively. Stingrays spend much of their time on the bottom and frequently bury
themselves in the substrate; sharp rocks or gravel will damage their delicate underside. Decorations should be kept to a
minimum and securely anchored, as rays easily move lightweight objects.

Filtration is the key element of successful husbandry. _P. leopoldi_ is extremely sensitive to water quality and
produces a substantial bioload. A powerful external canister filter (or multiple filters) with a turnover rate of at
least 8–10 tank volumes per hour is essential. Ammonia and nitrites must always remain at zero, while nitrates should be
kept below 20 mg/l.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Potamotrygon leopoldi_ is a predator feeding on small fish, crustaceans, mollusks, and aquatic insects. In
the aquarium, the diet should be varied and protein-rich:

- Staple foods: shrimp, mussels, squid, tilapia or pollock fillet
- Live and frozen foods: bloodworms, earthworms, small fish
- Specialized ray pellets (once the ray has been trained to accept them)

Juvenile rays should be fed daily in small portions, while adults eat 2–3 times per week. Overfeeding must be avoided:
leftover food rapidly degrades water quality, to which stingrays are highly sensitive. It is recommended to remove
uneaten food within 15–20 minutes of feeding.

## Care and Maintenance

Keeping _Potamotrygon leopoldi_ demands discipline and attention to detail. Water changes of 25–30% should be performed
weekly, using aged water that matches the tank temperature. Sudden fluctuations in parameters cause severe stress and
can lead to feeding refusal.

Regularly test ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate levels. Service filters in stages to avoid disrupting the biological
filtration. Substrate vacuuming is necessary but should be done carefully without disturbing the ray.

When working in the aquarium, always be mindful of the venomous tail spine. Use long gloves and avoid sudden movements.
If the ray presses flat against the bottom and raises its tail, this is a warning — back off and allow it to calm down.

Subdued lighting is preferred. Bright light causes discomfort for stingrays. The aquarium must be securely covered with
a lid, as rays are capable of jumping out of the water.

## Compatibility

_Potamotrygon leopoldi_ is generally peaceful and can be kept with large, calm fish that occupy the middle and upper
water layers. Good tank mates include large species such as [oscars](/en/astronotus-ocellatus),
[polypteruses](/en/polypterus-endlicherii), arowanas, or large pleco catfish.

Completely unsuitable companions include:

- Small fish — they will be eaten
- Aggressive cichlids that may damage the ray's delicate disc
- Bottom-dwelling fish that compete for territory on the substrate

The ideal setup is a species-only tank with one ray or a pair. When keeping multiple rays, ensure sufficient bottom area
so that each individual has its own territory. The recommended sex ratio is one male to one or two females.

## Breeding

Breeding _Potamotrygon leopoldi_ in captivity is challenging but achievable. Stingrays are livebearers: the female
carries the developing young for 3–4 months and gives birth to 1–8 fully formed pups with a disc diameter of 6–8 cm.

To stimulate breeding, stable ideal water parameters, generous and varied feeding, and a spacious aquarium are all
required. The male fertilizes the female using modified pelvic fins (claspers). A pregnant female becomes noticeably
larger and may become more reclusive.

Newborn rays should be moved to a separate rearing tank with soft sand and pristine water quality. Feeding of the young
begins immediately after birth with small bloodworms, brine shrimp, and chopped shrimp. Juvenile rays grow relatively
quickly with proper nutrition and diligent water maintenance.
