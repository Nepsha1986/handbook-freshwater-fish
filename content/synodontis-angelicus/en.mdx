## Overview

_Synodontis angelicus_ is one of the most visually stunning members of the family Mochokidae, earning its "angelic" name
through its remarkable appearance. In the wild, this species inhabits the Congo River basin in Central Africa, where it
favors rocky substrates, driftwood, and areas with moderate current.

The hallmark of this catfish is its striking pattern of bright white or yellowish spots scattered across a dark brown or
black body. In juveniles, the spots are large and well-defined; as the fish matures, the spots tend to become smaller
and more numerous. The body is robust and flattened on the underside, with three pairs of barbels around the mouth — a
characteristic feature of synodontis catfish. Adults reach 25–30 cm in length and can live 10–15 years with proper care.

_S. angelicus_ is primarily nocturnal. During the day it prefers to hide in shelters, becoming active at dusk to forage
for food. This behavior should be taken into account when planning the aquarium layout and feeding schedule.

## Tank Requirements

_Synodontis angelicus_ requires a spacious aquarium with a minimum volume of 200 liters. Optimal water parameters are:
temperature 24–28 °C, pH 7.0–8.5, and general hardness (GH) 10–20. The water should be clean and well-oxygenated, as
this species is sensitive to elevated nitrate levels.

The aquarium setup should include numerous hiding spots: rock caves, ceramic tubes, and driftwood with hollows. Having
dedicated shelters is essential for the fish's comfort and stress reduction. The substrate should be fine and smooth
(sand or fine gravel), since synodontis catfish actively dig through the substrate and can damage their barbels on rough
material.

Lighting should be moderate or subdued. If bright lights are used, be sure to create shaded areas with floating plants
or driftwood. Filtration must be powerful, with effective mechanical and biological media — a large catfish produces a
significant bioload.

## Feeding and Diet

_Synodontis angelicus_ is an omnivore with a preference for protein-rich foods. In the wild, it feeds on small
invertebrates, insect larvae, detritus, and algae growth.

In the aquarium, the diet should be based on high-quality sinking pellets and catfish wafers. Regularly offer live or
frozen foods such as bloodworms, tubifex, brine shrimp, and daphnia. Vegetable supplementation is also important —
spirulina-based tablets, blanched zucchini, or spinach work well.

Feeding is best done in the evening, shortly before or after lights out, matching the fish's nocturnal lifestyle. Young
fish should be fed daily, while adults do well on 4–5 feedings per week, avoiding overfeeding. Note that this catfish
can be quite greedy, so make sure other bottom-dwelling tank mates also receive their share.

## Care and Maintenance

Keeping _Synodontis angelicus_ is not particularly challenging for aquarists with basic experience. The key is
maintaining stable water parameters and aquarium cleanliness.

Weekly water changes of 20–30 % are essential. Regularly vacuum the substrate, especially around hiding spots where
uneaten food accumulates. Monitor ammonia, nitrite, and nitrate levels — nitrates should not exceed 20–30 mg/l.

Clean the filter every 2–3 weeks, rinsing media in aquarium water to preserve beneficial bacteria. Keep an eye on the
condition of the barbels — damage may indicate unsuitable substrate or poor water quality.

Overall, _S. angelicus_ is a robust fish with good resistance to common diseases. However, like all scaleless fish, it
is sensitive to elevated copper levels and certain medications — an important consideration when treating other tank
inhabitants.

## Compatibility

_Synodontis angelicus_ has a peaceful temperament and is well-suited for community tanks with medium to large fish.
Excellent tank mates include moderately tempered African cichlids, such as
[Neolamprologus brichardi](/en/neolamprologus-brichardi) or
[Altolamprologus compressiceps](/en/altolamprologus-compressiceps).

It coexists well with other synodontis species — [Synodontis multipunctatus](/en/synodontis-multipunctatus),
[Synodontis petricola](/en/synodontis-petricola), and [Synodontis eupterus](/en/synodontis-eupterus). When keeping
multiple specimens, provide enough hiding spots — at least one per individual.

Avoid housing with small fish (neon tetras, small rasboras) that a large adult catfish might treat as prey. Also steer
clear of overly aggressive species that could damage the barbels or prevent the synodontis from feeding peacefully.

## Breeding

Breeding _Synodontis angelicus_ in captivity is moderately difficult and seldom achieved. Sexual dimorphism is subtle:
females are usually slightly larger and fuller than males, particularly in the abdominal area.

To stimulate spawning, gradually lower the temperature by 2–3 °C and then return it to normal, simulating the rainy
season. Intensive feeding with live foods for several weeks prior to spawning also increases the chances of success. The
breeding tank should contain caves and crevices — females deposit their eggs in these sheltered spots.

Egg incubation takes 3–5 days depending on temperature. The parents do not care for their offspring and may eat their
own eggs, so it is best to remove the adults after spawning. Starter food for fry includes brine shrimp nauplii and
infusoria. As the fry grow, they can be transitioned to crushed sinking foods. Juveniles grow slowly and require stable
water parameters and regular feeding.
