## Overview

_Platydoras costatus_ is one of the most recognizable and fascinating members of the Doradidae family, native to the
Amazon and Orinoco river basins in South America. In the wild, this catfish inhabits slow-moving rivers, flooded
forests, and tributaries with soft sandy or muddy bottoms.

The defining feature of _Platydoras costatus_ is its rows of bony scutes along the lateral line and powerful serrated
pectoral spines. Because of these spines, you should never use a net to catch this fish -- the spines easily become
entangled in mesh and can injure both the fish and the aquarist. Always use a plastic container for transfers.

Another remarkable trait is the ability to produce audible "croaking" or "grunting" sounds. The catfish generates these
using its pectoral fins and swim bladder, earning it the common name "Talking Catfish." These sounds are most often
heard during feeding or when the fish is removed from water.

Adults reach 15-20 cm in length and can live 10-15 years with proper care, making them true long-lived companions in the
home aquarium. Their coloration consists of striking alternating dark brown and white or cream stripes running from head
to tail.

## Tank Requirements

A comfortable setup for _Platydoras costatus_ requires an aquarium of at least 150 liters. Recommended water parameters
are:

- **Temperature:** 22-28 °C
- **pH:** 6.0-7.5
- **Hardness (GH):** 4-18

Fine sand or smooth rounded gravel works best as substrate -- this catfish actively rummages through the bottom in
search of food, and coarse substrate can damage its barbels. Provide plenty of hiding spots: driftwood, ceramic tubes,
rock arrangements, and dense plant growth. _Platydoras costatus_ is a nocturnal species that spends the day tucked away
in shelter, so multiple shaded retreats are essential.

Lighting should be subdued, or floating plants can be used to create shaded areas. Filtration should be efficient but
without excessively strong current.

## Feeding and Diet

_Platydoras costatus_ is an omnivore with a hearty appetite. The staple diet should consist of sinking pellets and
wafers designed for bottom-dwelling fish. Supplement regularly with live or frozen foods such as bloodworms, tubifex,
brine shrimp, and glassworms.

For the vegetable component, offer spirulina-based tablets or blanched pieces of cucumber, zucchini, or spinach. In the
wild, this catfish also feeds on small crustaceans and aquatic insects.

Feed in the evening after the main lights are turned off -- this is when the fish emerges from hiding and actively
searches for food. Make sure food reaches the bottom and is not intercepted by faster-moving tank mates. Remove any
uneaten food promptly to maintain water quality.

## Care and Maintenance

The care level for _Platydoras costatus_ is considered easy, making it suitable even for beginner aquarists. The key is
to maintain stable water parameters and perform regular water changes (20-25% weekly).

The filter should provide reliable mechanical and biological filtration. Periodically test for ammonia, nitrite, and
nitrate levels -- while this fish is hardy, chronic pollution will shorten its lifespan.

Keep in mind the species' behavioral traits: the catfish hides during the day, and there is no reason to worry if you do
not see it until after dark. When moving the fish, never use a net -- the serrated pectoral spines become hopelessly
tangled in mesh. Always transfer using a plastic container or cup.

With a lifespan of 10-15 years, _Platydoras costatus_ becomes a genuine companion. Many aquarists report that over time
their fish learns to recognize its owner and emerges from hiding when approached.

## Compatibility

_Platydoras costatus_ is one of the most peaceful catfish species and coexists beautifully with most non-aggressive
fish. Excellent tank mates include medium to large [tetras](/en/phenacogrammus-interruptus), peaceful cichlids,
[Corydoras](/en/corydoras-sterbai), [Ancistrus](/en/ancistrus-dolichopterus), and other bottom dwellers. A close
relative, the [Whitespot Doradid](/en/agamyxis-pectinifrons) from the same Doradidae family, also makes a fine
companion.

Avoid keeping them with very small fish such as neons or microrasboras -- at night, the catfish may accidentally swallow
a sleeping neighbor of suitable size. Overly aggressive or territorial species should also be avoided, although the bony
armor of _Platydoras costatus_ provides solid protection.

They can be kept in groups of 2-3 individuals -- in this case, provide a separate hiding spot for each fish. A group
tends to feel more confident and may display more interesting behavior.

## Breeding

Breeding _Platydoras costatus_ in captivity is extremely challenging, and successful spawnings in home aquariums are
virtually undocumented. In the wild, breeding occurs during the rainy season when water parameters shift dramatically.

Distinguishing males from females is difficult: females tend to be slightly larger and fuller in the abdominal area,
especially prior to spawning. Sexual maturity is reached at approximately 2-3 years of age.

To trigger spawning in an aquarium, some breeders attempt to simulate the rainy season by lowering the temperature by
2-3 °C followed by a gradual increase, increasing water changes, and softening the water. However, even these measures
rarely lead to success. Most specimens available in the trade are wild-caught or farm-bred in South America using
hormonal stimulation.
