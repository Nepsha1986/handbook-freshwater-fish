## Overview

_Labidochromis caeruleus_ is a freshwater cichlid from the mbuna group, found along the rocky coastlines of Lake Malawi
in East Africa. Despite the Latin species name translating to "blue," the most popular aquarium morph is the brilliant
yellow form with distinctive black stripes on the dorsal and anal fins. In the wild, this species inhabits rocky
biotopes at depths of 10 to 40 meters.

The Yellow Lab Cichlid reaches a size of 8-10 cm and is widely regarded as one of the most peaceful mbuna species.
Thanks to its striking coloration, calm temperament, and hardy nature, this fish has become a true classic in Malawi
cichlid keeping and an excellent choice for those taking their first steps with African cichlids.

## Tank Requirements

_Labidochromis caeruleus_ requires an aquarium of at least 120 liters. Optimal water parameters are: temperature
24-28°C, hardness 10-20 GH, and pH 7.8-8.6. Like all Malawi cichlids, this species needs hard, alkaline water — soft,
acidic conditions are harmful to its health.

The aquarium should be decorated in a rocky biotope style: stacks of rocks forming caves and crevices serve as the
foundation of the layout. Coral sand or fine coral gravel works best as substrate, helping maintain the required pH and
hardness levels. Live plants are rarely used since most species cannot tolerate hard alkaline water, although Anubias
and certain Vallisneria species may survive. Filtration should be powerful and aeration vigorous.

## Feeding and Diet

In the wild, _Labidochromis caeruleus_ feeds primarily on small invertebrates extracted from rock crevices, as well as
algae growth. In the aquarium, the fish readily accepts high-quality dry foods — flakes and pellets designed for Malawi
cichlids. It is important that the diet is based on foods with a high vegetable content.

Frozen foods such as cyclops, daphnia, and brine shrimp can be offered periodically. However, bloodworms and tubifex
should be avoided — high-protein foods can cause Malawi bloat, to which mbuna are particularly susceptible. Feed small
portions 2-3 times daily for best results.

## Care and Maintenance

Caring for Yellow Lab Cichlids is straightforward and well within reach of even beginner aquarists. The key is
maintaining stable water parameters. Weekly water changes of 20-30% are essential, and the fresh water should match the
tank water in temperature and hardness.

It is important to keep the filter clean and service it regularly. With proper care, _Labidochromis caeruleus_ lives
6-10 years. The fish is fairly resistant to disease, but poor water quality or improper feeding can lead to ich or
Malawi bloat. Regular observation of the fish's behavior and appetite will help detect problems early.

## Compatibility

The Yellow Lab Cichlid is one of the most peaceful mbuna, but it is still a cichlid capable of territorial behavior,
especially during spawning. The best tankmates are other peaceful Malawi cichlids: [Aulonocara](/en/aulonocara-nyassae),
[Rusty Cichlid](/en/iodotropheus-sprengerae), [Cobalt Zebra](/en/maylandia-callainos). They can also be kept with
[Cynotilapia afra](/en/cynotilapia-afra) and [Trewavas's Labeotropheus](/en/labeotropheus-trewavasae).

Aggressive species such as [Melanochromis auratus](/en/melanochromis-auratus) or
[Kenyi Cichlid](/en/maylandia-lombardoi) should be avoided. As bottom dwellers,
[Synodontis petricola](/en/synodontis-petricola) or [Synodontis multipunctatus](/en/synodontis-multipunctatus) make
excellent companions. It is recommended to keep a group of one male with several females to reduce intraspecific
aggression.

## Breeding

Breeding _Labidochromis caeruleus_ in the aquarium is not particularly difficult. This species is a maternal
mouthbrooder: after spawning, the female collects the fertilized eggs in her mouth and incubates them for 18-25 days.
During this period, the female does not eat, so it is important that she is in good condition before spawning.

To stimulate breeding, raise the temperature to 27-28°C and provide a varied diet. The female typically lays 10-25 eggs.
Once the fry are released, they can be fed baby brine shrimp or finely crushed dry food. The fry grow relatively quickly
and develop adult coloration by 6-8 months of age.
