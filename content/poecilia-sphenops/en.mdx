## Overview

The Molly (_Poecilia sphenops_) is one of the most popular aquarium fish in the family Poeciliidae, originating from
freshwater and brackish habitats across Central and South America. In the wild, these fish inhabit rivers, streams,
coastal lagoons, and even river estuaries. It is precisely this ability to adapt to varying salinity levels that makes
mollies truly unique among aquarium fish.

Adults reach a size of 8–10 cm. Thanks to decades of selective breeding, numerous color varieties exist: the black molly
(solid black body), the dalmatian molly (white body with black spots), as well as golden, silver, and orange forms.
Mollies have a sturdy, slightly elongated body with rounded fins. Males are typically smaller than females and possess a
gonopodium — a modified anal fin used for fertilization.

Mollies are diurnal fish, actively swimming in the middle and upper layers of the aquarium. They are very peaceful and
get along well with most non-aggressive tankmates. With proper care, their lifespan is 3–5 years.

## Tank Requirements

A minimum tank volume of 75 liters is required for keeping a small group of mollies. These fish are quite active and
need room to swim, so an overly cramped tank will lead to stress and declining health.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature:** 24–28 °C
- **pH:** 7.0–8.5
- **General Hardness (GH):** 10–30

Mollies are among the few aquarium fish that actually prefer hard, alkaline water. Soft, acidic water is entirely
unsuitable for them and leads to a weakened immune system. A practical tip: adding a small amount of aquarium salt (1–2
g per liter) has a beneficial effect on mollies' well-being and helps prevent fungal infections.

The tank should be planted with live plants that tolerate hard water — Java moss, Vallisneria, and Anubias work well.
Plants provide shelter and serve as an additional food source. Good filtration and aeration are essential, as mollies
are sensitive to water quality. A light-colored substrate is recommended — it makes the fish look more striking.

## Feeding and Diet

Mollies are omnivorous, but their diet should be predominantly plant-based. In the wild, they actively graze on algae,
detritus, and small invertebrates.

A recommended aquarium diet includes:

- **Staple food:** high-quality flakes or pellets for livebearers with a high spirulina content
- **Vegetable supplements:** blanched spinach or lettuce leaves, cucumber slices, spirulina wafers
- **Animal-based foods:** frozen or live brine shrimp, daphnia, and bloodworms — 2–3 times per week
- **Algae:** mollies eagerly graze on algal growth on glass and decorations, helping keep the tank clean

Feed mollies 2–3 times daily in small portions that the fish consume within 2–3 minutes. Overfeeding is one of the most
common mistakes among beginner aquarists, leading to obesity and deteriorating water quality.

## Care and Maintenance

Mollies are considered one of the most undemanding aquarium fish (care level 2 out of 5), but this does not mean they
require no attention.

Essential care guidelines:

- Weekly water changes of 25–30% of the tank volume
- Regular water parameter testing (monitoring nitrate levels and pH is especially important)
- Filter cleaning every 2–3 weeks
- Gravel vacuuming during each water change

Mollies are susceptible to ich (white spot disease) and fungal infections, particularly when kept in water that is too
soft or too cold. At the first signs of illness (white spots on the body, frayed fins, lethargy), it is advisable to
raise the temperature to 28 °C and add aquarium salt. Stable water parameters and a balanced diet are the best
prevention against any disease.

## Compatibility

Mollies are exceptionally peaceful fish (aggression level 1 out of 5) that are well suited for community tanks. It is
best to keep them in groups of 4–6 individuals with a female majority (a ratio of 1 male to 2–3 females) to reduce
stress from male courtship.

Ideal tankmates include:

- [Guppies](/en/poecilia-reticulata) and [Endler's guppies](/en/poecilia-wingei) — close relatives with similar
  requirements
- [Swordtails](/en/xiphophorus-hellerii) and [platies](/en/xiphophorus-maculatus) — other livebearers with compatible
  water parameters
- [Bronze corydoras](/en/corydoras-aeneus) — peaceful bottom-dwellers that complement the mid-level swimming zone of
  mollies
- [Bristlenose plecos](/en/ancistrus-dolichopterus) — calm sucker-mouth catfish

Avoid keeping mollies with aggressive cichlids, large predatory fish, or species that prefer soft, acidic water (such as
neon tetras or discus), as water parameter requirements will be incompatible.

## Breeding

Breeding mollies is straightforward even for beginners (breeding difficulty 2 out of 5). _Poecilia sphenops_ are
livebearers: the female carries fertilized eggs inside her body and gives birth to fully formed fry.

Gestation lasts approximately 4–6 weeks. A pregnant female can be identified by her swollen belly and a dark spot near
the anal fin (the so-called "gravid spot"). A single female can produce 20 to 80 fry per batch, depending on her age and
size.

For successful breeding:

- Provide dense plantings of fine-leaved plants (Java moss, hornwort) where fry can hide
- Alternatively, use a breeding box for the pregnant female, transferring her a few days before the expected birth
- Return the female to the main tank immediately after birth to prevent her from eating the fry

Molly fry are relatively large and can accept finely crushed flakes, baby brine shrimp (Artemia nauplii), and microworms
from day one. With good nutrition and stable water parameters, fry grow quickly and develop adult coloration by 3–4
months of age.
