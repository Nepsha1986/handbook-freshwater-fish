## Overview

_Pangasius sutchi_, commonly known as the Iridescent Shark or Shark Catfish, is a large freshwater fish belonging to the
family Pangasiidae. In the wild, this species inhabits the Mekong and Chao Phraya river basins across Thailand, Vietnam,
Laos, and Cambodia. The Iridescent Shark is one of the most commercially important food fish in Southeast Asia — its
fillets are sold worldwide under the names "pangasius" or "basa."

The common name "Shark Catfish" comes from its streamlined body shape and tall dorsal fin, which resemble the silhouette
of a shark. Juveniles display a striking iridescent stripe along their body, which unfortunately fades as they mature.
Adult fish reach impressive sizes of 60 to 90 cm in length, with a lifespan of 10-15 years.

It is important to note that Iridescent Sharks are often sold in pet stores as small juveniles of just 5-8 cm, with no
warning to buyers about their eventual near-meter size. Before purchasing this species, one must carefully evaluate
their ability to provide proper long-term care.

## Tank Requirements

_Pangasius sutchi_ is one of the most demanding species in terms of tank volume. The minimum aquarium size is 500
liters, though a group of these fish will require an even larger tank. The aquarium should be predominantly long to
provide ample swimming space — this is critically important for this species.

Optimal water parameters are: temperature 24-28°C, pH 6.5-7.5, and general hardness (GH) 5-15. A powerful filtration
system with good water flow is essential, simulating river-like conditions. Weekly water changes of 25-30% are
mandatory.

Sand or fine rounded gravel works best as substrate. Decorations should be placed along the periphery of the tank,
leaving the central area open for swimming. Sharp rocks and driftwood with protruding edges should be avoided, as
panicked Iridescent Sharks can injure themselves during sudden dashes. A secure lid is essential — frightened fish are
capable of jumping out of the tank.

## Feeding and Diet

_Pangasius sutchi_ is an omnivorous species with an excellent appetite. In the wild, they feed on small fish,
crustaceans, insects, and plant matter. In the aquarium, the staple diet should consist of high-quality pellets
formulated for large fish.

In addition to the staple food, offer frozen and live foods: shrimp, bloodworms, pieces of fish, and squid. A vegetable
component is also important — blanched lettuce, spinach leaves, and pieces of zucchini and cucumber. A varied diet
ensures good health and vibrant coloration.

Adult fish should be fed 1-2 times daily, in portions they can consume within 3-5 minutes. Juveniles should be fed more
frequently — 2-3 times per day. Keep in mind that Iridescent Sharks are prone to overeating, so it is important to
monitor food quantities.

## Care and Maintenance

The key characteristic of Iridescent Sharks is their extremely skittish and nervous temperament. Sudden movements near
the tank, abrupt light changes, or loud noises can send them into a panic, causing them to dart around the aquarium and
crash into walls and decorations. This can result in serious injuries, so the tank should be placed in a calm location,
away from high-traffic areas and noise sources.

Regular monitoring of water parameters is crucial. Watch ammonia and nitrite levels closely — large fish produce
significant amounts of waste. Weekly substrate vacuuming and water changes will help maintain a healthy environment.
When conditions deteriorate, Iridescent Sharks are susceptible to bacterial infections and fin rot.

It must be emphasized that _Pangasius sutchi_ is a schooling fish and should be kept in a group of at least 3-5
individuals. A solitary Iridescent Shark experiences severe stress, becomes even more skittish, and may refuse food.
Considering the adult size and the need for group keeping, this species is truly suitable only for very large aquariums
or ornamental ponds.

## Compatibility

_Pangasius sutchi_ is a relatively peaceful species, but it can swallow any fish that fits in its mouth. Tank mates
should be of comparable size and non-aggressive. Good companions include other large catfish such as the
[Sailfin Pleco](/en/pterygoplichthys-gibbiceps), large cichlids like the [Oscar](/en/astronotus-ocellatus), and the
[Bala Shark](/en/balantiocheilos-melanopterus).

Do not keep Iridescent Sharks with small fish — tetras, guppies, neons — as they will be treated as food. Aggressive
territorial species are also unsuitable, as they will constantly harass the timid Iridescent Sharks, increasing their
stress levels.

## Breeding

Breeding _Pangasius sutchi_ in a home aquarium is virtually impossible. In the wild, these fish are a migratory species
— they travel hundreds of kilometers upstream to spawn, a behavior that cannot be replicated in captivity.

On commercial farms in Southeast Asia, Iridescent Shark breeding is achieved through hormonal stimulation in specially
equipped ponds of enormous size. The fish are egg scatterers, and a single female can release tens of thousands of eggs
in one spawning event.

Aquarists should accept the fact that _Pangasius sutchi_ specimens they acquire are fish to be enjoyed throughout their
long lives, but producing offspring in captivity without specialized equipment is not feasible.
