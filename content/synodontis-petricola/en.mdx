## Overview

_Synodontis petricola_ is a small catfish from the family Mochokidae, endemic to Lake Tanganyika in East Africa. In the
wild, it inhabits rocky sections of the shoreline, hiding in crevices and caves between stones. Thanks to its compact
size (10–12 cm) and calm temperament, this species has become one of the most popular Synodontis catfish in the aquarium
hobby.

The body of _S. petricola_ is elongated, colored in a light beige or cream tone with contrasting dark brown or black
spots. The fins are adorned with broad black-and-white bands, giving the fish an elegant appearance. Like other
Synodontis species, it has three pairs of barbels that help it navigate and find food in darkness.

_Synodontis petricola_ is a predominantly nocturnal fish. During the day, it hides among rocks and shelters, becoming
active at dusk to explore the aquarium in search of food. Its lifespan in captivity is 8–15 years, making it a long-term
resident of the home aquarium.

## Tank Requirements

A group of 3–5 _Synodontis petricola_ requires an aquarium with a minimum volume of 150 liters. These catfish are social
fish that feel uncomfortable when kept alone, so group keeping is essential.

Water parameters should match the conditions of Lake Tanganyika:

- **Temperature:** 23–28 °C
- **pH:** 7.5–8.5
- **Hardness (GH):** 8–20

The aquarium setup should mimic a rocky biotope. Use stacked rocks to create numerous small caves and crevices — each
catfish should have its own hiding spot. Sandy substrate is preferred, as these fish enjoy sifting through it in search
of food. Plants are not necessary, but hardy species that tolerate alkaline water (Anubias, Vallisneria) can complement
the setup.

Filtration should be efficient, providing good biological purification and moderate water flow. Aeration is essential,
as Lake Tanganyika water is rich in oxygen.

## Feeding and Diet

_Synodontis petricola_ is an omnivorous species, which greatly simplifies diet selection. In nature, these catfish feed
on small invertebrates, insect larvae, algae, and detritus.

In the aquarium, the diet should be based on high-quality sinking granules or tablets for bottom-dwelling fish.
Supplement the menu with live or frozen foods: bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, and tubifex. Vegetable supplements
such as spirulina tablets or blanched spinach leaves help maintain digestive health.

Feeding is best done in the evening or after the main lights are turned off — this aligns with the catfish's nocturnal
lifestyle. Food should be offered in moderate amounts, ensuring none remains uneaten by morning. One to two feedings per
day is sufficient.

## Care and Maintenance

Keeping _Synodontis petricola_ is not particularly difficult, making it a good choice for aquarists with basic
experience. Key care aspects include:

- **Water changes:** 20–25% of the volume weekly. Use water with parameters close to the aquarium water to avoid sudden
  fluctuations in pH and hardness.
- **Substrate cleaning:** Siphon the sandy substrate every 1–2 weeks, paying attention to areas around rocks where food
  debris accumulates.
- **Parameter monitoring:** Regularly check pH, hardness, and nitrate levels. Tanganyikan species are sensitive to
  elevated nitrates — aim to keep them below 20 mg/l.
- **Lighting:** Moderate or subdued. Bright light causes stress in nocturnal fish, so provide shaded zones.

These catfish are quite hardy and resistant to disease when properly maintained. The key to good health is stable water
parameters and the absence of sudden changes.

## Compatibility

_Synodontis petricola_ is an ideal companion for a Lake Tanganyika cichlid aquarium. It coexists beautifully with
species from the genera _Julidochromis_, _Neolamprologus_, _Altolamprologus_, and _Lamprologus_. Keeping them alongside
small to medium-sized Tanganyikan cichlids creates a striking and harmonious biotope.

Suitable tank mates:

- Small to medium Lake Tanganyika cichlids
- Other _Synodontis_ species of similar size
- Shell-dwelling Lamprologus species

Unsuitable tank mates:

- Large aggressive cichlids that could harm the catfish
- Small shrimp and snails (may be eaten)
- Fish requiring soft, acidic water, as the water parameters are incompatible

When kept in groups, _S. petricola_ display interesting social behavior, establishing a hierarchy within the school.
They rarely conflict with each other, limiting interactions to mild squabbles over hiding spots.

## Breeding

Breeding _Synodontis petricola_ in the aquarium is achievable and not overly difficult when certain conditions are met.
An interesting feature of this species is its tendency toward brood parasitism: in the wild, they deposit their eggs
among spawning cichlids, which then incubate the foreign offspring in their mouths alongside their own.

To stimulate spawning in the aquarium, provide:

- Stable water parameters with a temperature of 25–27 °C
- Generous feeding with live and frozen foods for 1–2 weeks before the expected spawning
- Flat rocks and small caves for egg deposition

Eggs are deposited in shelters and develop over 3–5 days. Newly hatched fry can be fed with brine shrimp nauplii and
finely crushed dry food. To improve fry survival rates, it is recommended to transfer them to a separate rearing tank,
as adult fish may consume eggs and fry.

Sexual dimorphism in _S. petricola_ is subtle: females are generally slightly larger and fuller-bodied than males,
especially when ready to spawn.
