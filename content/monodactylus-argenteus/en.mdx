## Overview

_Monodactylus argenteus_ is a remarkable representative of the family Monodactylidae, native to the coastal waters of
the Indo-Pacific region. In the wild, this species is found along the coasts of Africa, Southeast Asia, and Australia.
Juveniles are commonly encountered in freshwater rivers, but as they mature, they migrate to estuaries and coastal
brackish waters, with adults often transitioning to fully marine conditions.

The Silver Moony possesses a characteristic disc-shaped, laterally compressed body with a diamond-like profile. Its
silvery coloration with vertical dark bars in juveniles makes it one of the most recognizable brackish water species.
Adults reach impressive sizes of 20-25 cm, and their lifespan under proper care spans 8-12 years. This is a schooling
species with diurnal activity that must be kept in groups of at least 5 individuals to maintain natural behavior.

## Tank Requirements

Keeping _Monodactylus argenteus_ requires a spacious aquarium with a minimum volume of 300 liters. Given the large size
and active nature of these fish, along with the need for group keeping, a generous tank volume is critically important.

The key requirement is brackish water. Recommended parameters are: temperature 24-28°C, pH 7.5-8.5, and general hardness
(GH) 10-25. Marine salt at a concentration of 5-15 g/L is used to create brackish conditions, and salinity should be
gradually increased as the fish mature.

The aquarium should be arranged with ample open swimming space. Rocks, driftwood, and salt-tolerant plants such as Java
moss or Anubias work well as decoration. A secure lid is essential — Silver Moonies are prone to jumping. Powerful
filtration is necessary to maintain water quality given the significant bioload of a school of large fish.

## Feeding and Diet

The Silver Moony is an omnivorous fish with an excellent appetite. In the wild, it feeds on small invertebrates, algae,
and organic detritus.

In the aquarium, the dietary foundation should consist of high-quality pellet or flake foods designed for large fish.
The diet must be supplemented with live and frozen foods: brine shrimp, bloodworms, daphnia, and glassworms. A vegetable
component is equally important — spirulina flakes, blanched lettuce leaves, spinach, or zucchini slices provide
essential micronutrients.

Feeding twice daily in small portions is recommended. A varied diet supports vibrant coloration and strengthens the
immune system. Overfeeding should be avoided, as leftover food rapidly degrades water quality in a brackish aquarium.

## Care and Maintenance

_Monodactylus argenteus_ is a fairly hardy species, but stability of keeping conditions is of paramount importance.
Weekly water changes of 20-30% are mandatory to maintain environmental quality. During water changes, it is important to
monitor salinity levels, adding marine salt to fresh water to achieve the correct concentration.

A reliable filtration system with biological, mechanical, and chemical stages is necessary to handle the substantial
bioload. Regular testing of water parameters (pH, salinity, ammonia, nitrites) helps identify deviations promptly.

When properly maintained, Silver Moonies are resistant to most common diseases. However, sudden fluctuations in salinity
or temperature can trigger ich and bacterial infections. Quarantining new fish before introduction is essential.

## Compatibility

The Silver Moony is a peaceful schooling fish that coexists well with other similarly sized species capable of
tolerating brackish conditions. Excellent tankmates include [mollies](/en/poecilia-sphenops) and
[orange chromides](/en/etroplus-maculatus), which also thrive in brackish water. Archerfish, scats, and other
monodactylus species are also suitable companions.

Keeping them with small fish is not recommended — the active and large Silver Moonies may intimidate smaller tankmates.
Aggressive and territorial species that could damage the delicate fins of Silver Moonies should be avoided. The optimal
setup is a species-only tank with a school of 5-6 individuals or a brackish water biotope with appropriately sized
companions.

## Breeding

Breeding _Monodactylus argenteus_ in aquarium conditions is an extremely challenging task, with successful cases
reported only rarely. Sexual dimorphism in this species is poorly defined, making sex determination difficult.

In the wild, Silver Moonies spawn in large shoals in open coastal waters, scattering eggs into the water column. To
stimulate spawning in captivity, a very spacious aquarium, a substantial group of mature individuals, and careful
conditioning with a varied high-protein diet are required. Gradual increases in salinity and temperature may trigger
spawning behavior.

The eggs are pelagic and small, hatching within 24-48 hours. Rearing the fry presents the primary challenge due to their
tiny size and need for the smallest live foods (infusoria, rotifers). Breeding this species is only feasible for
experienced aquarists with appropriate equipment.
