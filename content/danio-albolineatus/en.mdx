## Overview

Danio albolineatus (_Danio albolineatus_), better known among aquarists as the Pearl Danio, is one of the most beautiful
representatives of the Danio genus. This small fish, reaching 5–6 cm in length, captivates with its characteristic
pearlescent iridescence: depending on the angle of light, its body shimmers with bluish, pinkish, and golden hues. A
fine luminous stripe runs along the lateral line, giving the fish an elegant appearance.

In the wild, _Danio albolineatus_ inhabits freshwater bodies of Southeast Asia — streams and rivers of Myanmar,
Thailand, and Sumatra. These fish prefer shallow areas with moderate current and abundant vegetation. In their natural
environment, they form small schools and actively move through the upper and middle water layers.

The Pearl Danio is a diurnal fish with a lively and energetic temperament. Watching a school of these fish is a true
delight — they are constantly on the move, shimmering in the light. With proper care, their lifespan in an aquarium is
3–5 years.

## Tank Requirements

A comfortable home for a school of Pearl Danios requires an aquarium of **at least 60 liters**. These are active
swimmers that need plenty of open space, so choose an elongated tank — length matters more than height.

Optimal water parameters:

- **Temperature:** 22–27 °C
- **pH:** 6.5–7.5
- **General Hardness (GH):** 5–12

The aquarium should be equipped with a lid, as danios are excellent jumpers capable of leaping out of an open tank.
Plants are best placed along the sides and back wall, leaving the central area free for swimming. Vallisneria, cabomba,
and other long-stemmed plants work well.

Filtration should be effective but without excessive current. A moderate water flow mimics their natural habitat
conditions and benefits the fish. Lighting should be moderate — under it, the pearlescent sheen of their bodies is
displayed most beautifully.

## Feeding and Diet

_Danio albolineatus_ is an omnivorous species, and feeding these fish typically presents no challenges. The staple diet
can consist of high-quality dry foods: flakes and small granules designed for tropical fish. The food should be fine
enough for the fish to swallow easily.

To maintain health and vibrant coloration, I recommend regularly supplementing the diet with live or frozen foods:
daphnia, cyclops, bloodworms, and brine shrimp. Live food is especially beneficial when conditioning fish for breeding.

It is best to feed small portions 2–3 times per day. Pearl Danios feed primarily at the surface and in mid-water, so
choose foods that sink slowly. Uneaten food remnants should be promptly removed.

## Care and Maintenance

The Pearl Danio is one of the most undemanding aquarium species, making it an excellent choice for beginner aquarists.
Nevertheless, basic care principles must be followed.

Perform weekly water changes of 20–25 % of the tank volume — this is a key factor in the health of any aquarium fish.
Regularly test water parameters using test kits: it is easier to prevent deviations from optimal values than to deal
with their consequences. Monitor the filter and rinse filter media in aquarium water as needed.

Vacuum the substrate during each water change to remove organic waste. Keep nitrate levels under control — they should
not exceed 20–30 mg/L. Also monitor temperature: sudden fluctuations can weaken the fish's immune system.

With proper care, Pearl Danios display vibrant coloration, active behavior, and live up to 5 years.

## Compatibility

_Danio albolineatus_ is a peaceful schooling fish perfectly suited for community aquariums. They must be kept in a
**group of at least 6 individuals** — in a school, the fish feel confident, display natural behavior, and show their
brightest coloration.

Excellent tank mates include other peaceful species of comparable size:

- Other danios, such as [Zebra Danio](/en/danio-rerio) or [Glowlight Danio](/en/danio-choprae)
- [Rasboras](/en/rasbora-trilineata) and other small cyprinids
- Small tetras, such as [Neon Tetra](/en/paracheirodon-innesi) or [Ember Tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-amandae)
- [Corydoras](/en/corydoras-panda) and other peaceful bottom-dwelling fish

Avoid keeping them with large or aggressive species, as well as fish with long, flowing fins — active danios may nip at
them. Pearl Danios should also not be housed with slow-moving or timid species, as the school's activity may cause them
stress.

## Breeding

Breeding _Danio albolineatus_ is not particularly difficult, though it does require some preparation. These fish are egg
scatterers — they scatter eggs among plants and show no parental care.

For spawning, prepare a separate tank of 15–20 liters with a water level of about 15 cm. Place fine-leaved plants (Java
moss is an excellent choice) or a spawning mop on the bottom. A mesh on the bottom can also help protect the eggs from
being eaten by the parents. Raise the water temperature in the breeding tank to 25–27 °C, and maintain the pH at
6.5–7.0.

For 1–2 weeks before spawning, feed the breeders generously with live food. Females can be identified by their more
rounded bellies. Introduce a pair or a group (2 males per 1 female) into the breeding tank in the evening — spawning
typically occurs at dawn.

After spawning, be sure to remove the adults, or they will eat the eggs. Larvae hatch within 24–36 hours, and after
another 2–3 days, the fry become free-swimming and begin to feed. Starter food should be infusoria and microworms; after
a week, you can switch to brine shrimp nauplii. The fry grow quickly and develop their characteristic pearlescent sheen
by about two months of age.
