## Overview

The Kribensis (_Pelvicachromis kribensis_) is a small yet strikingly beautiful dwarf cichlid from the Cichlidae family,
found in slow-moving rivers and streams of West Africa, primarily in Nigeria and Cameroon. This species is closely
related to the more widely known [Pelvicachromis pulcher](/en/pelvicachromis-pulcher) and is frequently confused with it
in the trade, although it differs in coloration and body shape.

Adults reach a size of 7–10 cm, with males noticeably larger than females. The body is elongated and slightly laterally
compressed, featuring a distinctive broad dark stripe running from the snout to the tail. During spawning, females
develop an intense crimson-cherry coloration on the belly, making them even more eye-catching than males. With proper
care, they can live 5–8 years.

_Pelvicachromis kribensis_ is one of the best choices for those looking to explore the world of cichlids. This fish has
a vibrant personality, fascinating behavior, and modest care requirements, making it suitable for both beginners and
experienced aquarists.

### How to Tell Males from Females

Sexual dimorphism in Kribensis is quite pronounced:

- **Males** are larger (up to 10 cm), with a more elongated body and pointed dorsal and anal fins featuring bright
  edging.
- **Females** are smaller (up to 7 cm), with a rounder belly and shorter fins. During breeding, their belly takes on a
  rich crimson-red color.

## Tank Requirements

A pair of Kribensis can be comfortably housed in an aquarium of at least 70 liters. If keeping them in a community setup
with other fish, a tank of 100 liters or more is recommended.

**Water parameters:**

- Temperature: 24–28°C
- pH: 6.0–7.5
- General Hardness (GH): 5–15

The substrate should be sand or fine gravel, as Kribensis enjoy digging through the substrate and may uproot plants with
delicate root systems. Hiding spots are essential: clay pots, coconut shells, rock caves, and driftwood. Shelters are
critically important for this species, as they serve as both spawning sites and territorial refuges.

Plants are welcome but should be hardy species such as anubias, Java fern, and cryptocorynes, ideally attached to rocks
or driftwood. Moderate lighting is preferred; in brightly lit tanks, Kribensis tend to feel less secure. Filtration is a
must, with moderate water flow. Weekly water changes of 20–25% will help maintain stable parameters.

## Feeding and Diet

_Pelvicachromis kribensis_ is an omnivorous fish with a healthy appetite. In the wild, it feeds on small invertebrates,
insect larvae, algae, and detritus. In the aquarium, provide a varied diet:

- **Staple food**: high-quality cichlid flakes or pellets.
- **Live and frozen foods**: bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, and tubifex — offer 2–3 times a week to maintain vibrant
  coloration.
- **Vegetable supplements**: blanched spinach leaves, cucumber or zucchini slices, and spirulina-based foods.

Feed 1–2 times daily in small portions that the fish can consume within 2–3 minutes. Overfeeding should be avoided, as
Kribensis are prone to overeating. A weekly fasting day is beneficial.

A practical tip from experience: increasing the proportion of live and frozen foods before spawning stimulates breeding
and improves egg quality.

## Care and Maintenance

Kribensis is one of the hardiest cichlid species, making it an excellent choice for aquarists of any experience level.
Key guidelines for successful keeping:

- **Parameter stability**: Although the fish tolerates a wide range of conditions, sudden fluctuations in temperature
  and pH should be avoided. Keep parameters within the recommended range.
- **Regular water changes**: 20–25% of the tank volume weekly. Use a gravel vacuum to remove food debris from the
  substrate.
- **Behavioral observation**: Kribensis have strong personalities. An established pair will actively defend its
  territory, especially during breeding. This is normal behavior and no cause for concern.

These fish handle minor deviations from ideal parameters well, possess strong immune systems, and rarely fall ill when
properly maintained. The main problems arise when they are kept in tanks that are too small or lack adequate hiding
spots — in such cases, the fish become stressed and overly aggressive.

## Compatibility

_Pelvicachromis kribensis_ is a relatively peaceful cichlid that works well in a community aquarium. However, during
breeding, the pair will actively defend their territory, chasing away any fish that approach the shelter containing
their eggs.

**Good tankmates:**

- Small to medium-sized tetras — [flame tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-flammeus),
  [black neon tetra](/en/hyphessobrycon-herbertaxelrodi), [Congo tetra](/en/phenacogrammus-interruptus)
- [Corydoras](/en/corydoras-aeneus) and other peaceful bottom-dwellers
- [Bristlenose plecos](/en/ancistrus-sp)
- Rasboras

**Unsuitable tankmates:**

- Large and aggressive cichlids
- Very small fish (may become targets during breeding)
- Other bottom-dwelling species that compete for the same shelters

If keeping more than one pair of Kribensis, the tank should be significantly larger (150 liters or more), with enough
hiding spots for each pair. Otherwise, territorial conflicts are inevitable.

## Breeding

Breeding _Pelvicachromis kribensis_ is one of the most rewarding experiences in the hobby and is entirely achievable
even for beginners. They are cave spawners: the female deposits eggs on the walls or ceiling of a shelter, and both
parents actively care for the brood.

**Triggering spawning:**

- Raise the water temperature to 26–28°C.
- Increase the proportion of live foods in the diet.
- Ensure there are enough suitable shelters in the tank (clay pots, coconut shells).

The female lays 50–200 eggs and takes primary responsibility for guarding the clutch, rarely leaving the shelter.
Meanwhile, the male patrols the territory and drives away any fish approaching the nest. The eggs hatch in 3–5 days, and
the fry become free-swimming after another 5–7 days.

Parental care in Kribensis is remarkably well-developed: both parents escort the school of fry around the tank, guiding
them to feeding spots and shielding them from danger. It is a truly captivating sight to witness.

**Raising the fry:**

- First days: baby brine shrimp (nauplii), microworms.
- From week 2: finely crushed flakes, cyclops.
- From one month: standard foods in small particle sizes.

An interesting fact: water pH affects the sex ratio of the offspring. More acidic water (pH 6.0–6.5) produces a higher
proportion of females, while more alkaline water (pH 7.0–7.5) yields more males. This is useful to know if you are
planning targeted breeding.
