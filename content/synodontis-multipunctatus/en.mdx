## Overview

_Synodontis multipunctatus_ is one of the most remarkable members of the family Mochokidae, native to the rocky
coastlines of Lake Tanganyika in East Africa. In the aquarium hobby, this catfish earned the nickname "cuckoo" due to
its extraordinary reproductive strategy: during the spawning of mouthbrooding cichlids, it slips its own eggs into the
mix, tricking the cichlids into incubating foreign offspring in their mouths.

The body of _S. multipunctatus_ is moderately elongated, silvery-grey with numerous dark spots across the entire
surface, which is how the species got its scientific name. Adults reach 15-25 cm in length, and with proper care, they
can live for 8-15 years. Like most synodontis catfish, this species is predominantly nocturnal: during the day it
prefers to hide among rocks and shelters, becoming active at dusk to explore its territory in search of food.

In the aquarium, the Cuckoo Catfish truly reveals its personality when kept in groups, displaying fascinating social
interactions. This is a lively and active species that makes an excellent addition to a Lake Tanganyika biotope
aquarium.

## Tank Requirements

A comfortable group of _Synodontis multipunctatus_ requires an aquarium of at least 200 liters. Optimal water parameters
are: temperature 23-28°C, pH 7.0-8.5, general hardness (GH) 8-20. These values reflect the natural conditions of Lake
Tanganyika, and maintaining their stability is critically important for the fish's health.

The aquarium layout should replicate a rocky biotope. Use large stones to create grottos, crevices, and caves — these
are the shelters where the catfish spend their daylight hours. Ideally, each individual should have its own hiding spot
to reduce intraspecific competition. Fine to medium sand works best as a substrate, allowing the catfish to safely sift
through it while foraging.

Filtration must be powerful — Lake Tanganyika inhabitants are accustomed to clean, well-oxygenated water. An external
canister filter rated at 4-6 times the tank volume per hour is the optimal choice. These catfish should be kept in
groups of at least 3-5 individuals — solitary specimens tend to become shy and display increased aggression.

## Feeding and Diet

_Synodontis multipunctatus_ is an omnivorous species with a broad range of dietary preferences. In the wild, it feeds on
small invertebrates, mollusks, algae, and detritus. In the aquarium, the core diet should consist of high-quality
sinking pellets and tablets designed for bottom-dwelling fish.

Be sure to supplement the diet with live and frozen foods: bloodworms, brine shrimp, daphnia, and tubifex. A vegetable
component is equally important — spirulina tablets, blanched zucchini, or cucumber will help ensure the necessary
nutritional balance.

Given the species' nocturnal activity, the main feeding should be done in the evening, shortly before lights-out or
immediately after. This ensures the catfish receive their share before more active daytime fish consume all the food.
Feed 1-2 times daily in moderate portions, avoiding overfeeding — leftover food on the bottom quickly degrades water
quality.

## Care and Maintenance

Keeping _Synodontis multipunctatus_ is not particularly challenging for an aquarist with basic experience. Weekly water
changes of 20-30% are essential for maintaining parameter stability and removing accumulated nitrates. When performing
water changes, ensure the fresh water matches the aquarium's temperature and hardness parameters.

Vacuum the substrate regularly, especially in feeding zones — as bottom-dwelling fish, synodontis produce a considerable
amount of waste. Monitor ammonia and nitrite levels with test kits at least every two weeks, particularly during the
first months after setting up the aquarium.

Pay attention to the condition of the shelters: rocks must be securely positioned, as active catfish may dig under them.
Moderate lighting is preferable, or provide shaded areas — under excessively bright light, the fish will remain hidden
and you will rarely see them. A "sunrise-sunset" mode in your lighting system will help you observe the fish during
their peak activity.

## Compatibility

The Cuckoo Catfish fits perfectly into a Lake Tanganyika community tank. The best tankmates are medium-sized cichlids:
[Neolamprologus brichardi](/en/neolamprologus-brichardi), [Neolamprologus leleupi](/en/neolamprologus-leleupi),
[Altolamprologus compressiceps](/en/altolamprologus-compressiceps),
[Altolamprologus calvus](/en/altolamprologus-calvus). Larger species such as
[Cyphotilapia frontosa](/en/cyphotilapia-frontosa) and [Tropheus duboisi](/en/tropheus-duboisi) also coexist well with
these catfish.

Among related species, [Synodontis petricola](/en/synodontis-petricola) can be added — they occupy a similar ecological
niche and often coexist peacefully. However, avoid keeping them with small fish under 5 cm in length, as they may be
seen as prey. It is also not recommended to combine them with species requiring soft, acidic water, such as South
American tetras or Apistogramma.

During feeding, _S. multipunctatus_ can be assertive and push aside slower tankmates, so ensure all aquarium inhabitants
receive adequate food.

## Breeding

Breeding _Synodontis multipunctatus_ in captivity is a fascinating process that requires the presence of mouthbrooding
cichlid hosts. This is what makes the species truly unique: without cichlids that incubate eggs in their mouths, natural
reproduction is impossible.

To stimulate spawning, maintain stable water parameters and provide generous feeding for a group of several males and
females. When the mouthbrooding cichlids begin to spawn, the catfish instantly become active: they rush into the
spawning area and deposit their own eggs at the precise moment when the female cichlid is collecting eggs into her
mouth. Unable to distinguish the foreign eggs from her own, the cichlid female incubates the catfish eggs alongside her
own clutch.

_S. multipunctatus_ fry develop faster than cichlid fry, hatching within 3-5 days. While inside the cichlid's mouth, the
young catfish often consume the host's eggs, providing themselves with additional nutrition. After being released from
the oral cavity, the fry should be separated and fed with artemia nauplii and finely ground dry food.

Breeding difficulty is rated as moderate: the main challenge is selecting suitable host cichlids and creating conditions
that stimulate their spawning. Cichlids of the genus Haplochromis and other mouthbrooding species from Lake Tanganyika
are best suited for this purpose.
