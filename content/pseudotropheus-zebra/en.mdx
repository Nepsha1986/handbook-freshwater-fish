## Overview

_Pseudotropheus zebra_, commonly known as the Zebra Mbuna, is one of the most recognizable cichlids from the mbuna group
inhabiting the rocky shores of Lake Malawi in Africa. This species belongs to the family Cichlidae and reaches a size of
10–12 cm. In the wild, Zebra Mbuna populate rocky coastal zones where they feed on algae growing on stones.

One of the most striking features of this species is its remarkable diversity of color morphs. The classic form displays
a blue body with dark vertical bars, but there are also red-orange morphs (known as "Red Zebra"), albino forms, and
other variations. This polymorphism makes the species especially popular among Lake Malawi cichlid enthusiasts.

By temperament, _Pseudotropheus zebra_ is a territorial and aggressive fish, which is typical for the mbuna group. With
a lifespan of 8–12 years, this is a long-term and vibrant aquarium resident, though its care requires experience and
understanding of Malawi cichlid behavior.

## Tank Requirements

A minimum aquarium volume of 150 liters is required for keeping _Pseudotropheus zebra_. However, given their aggressive
nature, tanks of 200–300 liters are preferable — the extra space allows for more hiding spots and helps reduce
territorial conflicts.

Water parameters should match the conditions of Lake Malawi:

- **Temperature:** 24–28°C
- **pH:** 7.8–8.6
- **Hardness (GH):** 10–20

The key element of the setup is an abundance of rock formations with numerous caves, crevices, and passages. Rocks can
be stacked all the way to the surface, creating a complex landscape. Sand or fine gravel works best as substrate. Live
plants generally do not survive in this type of aquarium — the fish dig them up and nibble on them. The exception is
tough-leaved species like Anubias attached to rocks.

Powerful filtration and good aeration are essential. Malawi cichlids are sensitive to nitrate levels, making effective
biological filtration critically important.

## Feeding and Diet

_Pseudotropheus zebra_ is a primarily herbivorous species. In the wild, these fish scrape algae growth from rocks, and
their digestive system is adapted to this type of diet.

The core diet in the aquarium should consist of:

- Spirulina-based and algae-based foods
- Specialized pellets and flakes for mbuna cichlids
- Blanched vegetables: zucchini, spinach, lettuce leaves

**Important:** avoid foods high in animal protein (bloodworms, tubifex). Overfeeding with such foods is one of the
primary causes of "Malawi bloat," a dangerous condition that can be fatal. Occasional and minimal use of brine shrimp or
daphnia as a supplement is acceptable.

Feed small portions 2–3 times per day. This is more physiologically appropriate than one large feeding and reduces
strain on the digestive system.

## Care and Maintenance

Weekly water changes of 20–30% are an essential requirement for healthy _Pseudotropheus zebra_ care. Malawi cichlids do
not tolerate high nitrate concentrations, so regular monitoring of water parameters is necessary.

Keep an eye on the condition of rock structures: stones must be stacked securely so that active fish do not topple them.
Periodically check the filter and clean it to prevent a drop in performance.

One important aspect of mbuna husbandry is the technique of moderate overstocking. Keeping a larger number of fish in
the tank disperses the dominant male's aggression among multiple targets, preventing him from focusing on a single
victim. However, this method only works when accompanied by appropriately increased filtration capacity and more
frequent water changes.

Watch for signs of stress: darkened coloration, refusal to eat, or hiding in corners. These symptoms may indicate
illness or excessive pressure from dominant individuals.

## Compatibility

_Pseudotropheus zebra_ is one of the most aggressive mbuna cichlids, and tankmate selection must be carefully
considered. The best companions are other mbuna of comparable size and temperament:

- [Labidochromis caeruleus](/en/labidochromis-caeruleus) — a relatively peaceful species that pairs well with zebras
  thanks to its contrasting yellow coloration
- [Melanochromis auratus](/en/melanochromis-auratus) — another aggressive mbuna capable of holding its own
- [Cynotilapia afra](/en/cynotilapia-afra) — an active mbuna from the same biotope
- [Chindongo demasoni](/en/chindongo-demasoni) — small but feisty mbuna

It is strongly advised against keeping them with peaceful cichlids such as [Aulonocara](/en/aulonocara-nyassae), as well
as with small tropical fish. Avoid housing them with species that have similar coloration — this provokes heightened
aggression.

The optimal setup is one male with a group of 3–4 females. Having multiple males in the same tank leads to fierce
fighting unless the space is sufficiently large.

## Breeding

_Pseudotropheus zebra_ is a maternal mouthbrooder: the female incubates fertilized eggs in her mouth for 21–28 days.
This is a reliable method of protecting offspring, characteristic of most mbuna cichlids.

The spawning process unfolds as follows: the male selects a flat area on a rock and actively courts the female,
displaying vivid coloration and characteristic movements. After the eggs are laid and fertilized, the female collects
them in her mouth. The male's anal fin features so-called "egg spots" (releasers) that help the female orient correctly
while gathering eggs.

For successful breeding, it is recommended to:

- Maintain the temperature at 26–28°C
- Provide the female with plenty of hiding places
- Avoid disturbing the brooding female — stress can cause premature release of fry

Fully formed fry emerge ready for independent life and can feed immediately. Starter foods include crushed spirulina
flakes and brine shrimp nauplii. With good care, fry grow quickly and develop their characteristic morph coloration by
6–8 months of age.
